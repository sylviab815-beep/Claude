# Slash Editor — Reference Examples

## Example: Full Slash Assessment

### Setup: Chapter from a Sage Kennedy holiday billionaire romance, currently 4,200 words

---

## SLASH ASSESSMENT: Chapter 6 — The Charity Gala

**Verdict:** BLOATED

**Word Count:** 4,200 → **Target:** 2,800

### IMMEDIATE CUTS

1. **Para 3-5 (getting ready montage):** Three paragraphs describing her outfit selection process. Reader doesn't care. One line: she chose the red dress. Move on.

2. **Para 12-14 (backstory about her college roommate):** This information is irrelevant to the scene and stops momentum dead. The roommate isn't in this book. Cut entirely.

3. **Page 4, dialogue exchange about the catering:** Eight lines of small talk that reveals nothing about character and advances nothing. Cut to the moment that matters — when he appears.

4. **Para 28-31 (her internal debate about approaching him):** You've written four paragraphs of her waffling. This is a Sage Kennedy heroine — she's confident, not paralyzed. Cut to one line of hesitation, then action.

5. **Final two paragraphs (reflection on the evening):** The chapter should end on his invitation, not her processing it afterward. Cut the reflection. End on momentum.

### CONSOLIDATION NEEDED

1. **The ballroom description (page 2):** Currently 187 words of setting. This is romance, not architecture digest. Consolidate to 40 words max — hit the holiday sparkle and move to character.

2. **Their first conversation (page 5-6):** Good banter but it circles. They make the same point three times. Keep the sharpest version, cut the repetition.

3. **The interruption scene (page 7):** His business rival appears. Currently takes a full page before the tension lands. Trim setup, accelerate to conflict.

### SCENES ON TRIAL

| Scene | Current Purpose | Verdict |
|-------|-----------------|---------|
| Getting ready | Establish her nervousness | CUT — show nervousness in the gala, not before |
| Arrival/ballroom | Setting and atmosphere | TRIM — 40 words, not 200 |
| First eye contact | Tension building | KEEP |
| Conversation at bar | Banter, attraction | KEEP but consolidate |
| Rival interruption | Stakes, external conflict | KEEP but trim setup |
| His invitation | Chapter hook | KEEP — but end HERE |
| Her reflection | Processing | CUT — reader doesn't need this |

### VOICE CHECK

**Target Voice:** Sage Kennedy
**Current Voice:** 70% on-brand, drifting in middle section
**Drift Detected:** Yes

**Specifics:**
- Para 12-14 reads introspective and melancholy — wrong energy for Sage Kennedy
- The waffling in para 28-31 undermines the confident heroine voice
- The banter is landing but her internal thoughts between dialogue beats are too heavy
- Holiday sparkle is present in setting but missing from her POV — she should be enjoying this, not dreading it

### FINAL NOTES

Cut 1,400 words. This chapter has good bones buried under fat. The banter works. The chemistry works. Everything around it is bloat. Slash it and you'll have a chapter that moves.

## Example: Quick Slash (Single Scene)

### Setup: A morning-after scene in a Ruthie Clark dark fantasy

---

**SCENE:** Morning after the blood ritual, her POV waking in his bed

**VERDICT:** TRIM

**WORD COUNT:** 890 → 600

**Issues:**
- First three paragraphs are her slowly waking up. We don't need the full process. Start with eyes open.
- Paragraph 5 repeats the emotional beat from paragraph 4 — she's processing the same guilt twice.
- The description of his sleeping form goes on too long. This is Ruthie Clark — lush but not indulgent. Pick your three best details, cut the rest.
- Final paragraph tells us what she's feeling instead of showing it through action.

**Action:**
- Cut paragraphs 1-2 entirely. Start with "His arm was heavy across her ribs."
- Delete paragraph 5. 
- Trim his physical description from 127 words to 50. Keep: the scar, the way his hand still gripped her wrist, one detail about his face in sleep.
- Rewrite final paragraph as action: what does she DO with this feeling? Don't tell me she feels trapped — show her testing whether she can slip free without waking him.

**Voice Check:** On-brand. The atmosphere is right, the intensity is there. Just needs tightening — lush shouldn't mean bloated.

## Example: Voice Verification Report

### Setup: Full chapter check for a Moxie Savage mafia romance

---

## VOICE VERIFICATION: Chapter 9

**Target Voice:** Moxie Savage (mafia dark romance, heat 5/5)

**Overall Assessment:** VOICE DRIFT DETECTED — Currently reading 60% Moxie, 40% generic contemporary

### BRAND MARKERS CHECK

| Marker | Expected | Found | Status |
|--------|----------|-------|--------|
| Raw intensity | High | Medium | ⚠️ DRIFT |
| Possessive tension | Present throughout | Only in dialogue | ⚠️ DRIFT |
| Moral complexity | Yes | Yes | ✓ |
| High heat | Explicit | Present but restrained | ⚠️ DRIFT |
| Dark edge | Constant undercurrent | Intermittent | ⚠️ DRIFT |
| Sweetness | Minimal/earned | Creeping in | ⚠️ DRIFT |

### SPECIFIC DRIFT POINTS

1. **Page 2, her internal monologue:** "He wasn't what she expected. Maybe there was more to him than the reputation suggested."
   - **Problem:** This is too soft, too romance-standard. Moxie Savage heroines don't soften this fast. They fight the attraction harder.
   - **Fix:** She can notice complexity, but her reaction should be more guarded, more self-protective.

2. **Page 4, the confrontation scene:**
   - **Problem:** He backs down too easily. Moxie Savage heroes don't back down. They might redirect, they might get strategic, but they don't concede.
   - **Fix:** He needs to maintain dominance even while appearing to give her what she wants. It's a power play, not a compromise.

3. **Page 6-7, the heat scene:**
   - **Problem:** This reads like a 3/5 heat level. For Moxie Savage, explicit means EXPLICIT. You've faded to black at the moment it should escalate.
   - **Fix:** Stay in the scene. Full sensory immersion. This reader bought dark mafia romance — deliver what they came for.

4. **Page 8, morning after:**
   - **Problem:** "She traced the tattoo on his chest, wondering about each mark." This is tender. Moxie Savage can have tender moments, but they need to be earned through fire, and this is chapter 9 — too early for this level of softness.
   - **Fix:** If she's touching him, it should feel dangerous. She's not safe. She shouldn't feel safe.

### VOICE RECALIBRATION NEEDED

The chapter needs a pass focused on:
1. **Hardening her internal voice** — less wonder, more wariness
2. **Maintaining his edge** — he's dangerous, even when he's being "nice"
3. **Escalating the heat** — don't pull back at the crucial moment
4. **Reducing softness** — every tender moment needs a dark counterweight

### VERDICT

Rewrite required before this chapter is brand-consistent. The plot beats are fine. The voice has drifted toward generic contemporary romance. Moxie Savage readers want intensity, danger, and explicit heat. This chapter is currently too safe.

## Example: Scene Justification Audit

### Setup: Evaluating whether a subplot scene earns its place

---

## SCENE JUSTIFICATION: The Coffee Shop Encounter (Chapter 4)

**Scene Summary:** Heroine runs into her sister at a coffee shop. They discuss the heroine's new job and sister gives unsolicited advice about her love life.

### JUSTIFICATION TEST

| Question | Answer | Earning Its Place? |
|----------|--------|-------------------|
| Does it advance the plot? | No — no new information that affects story direction | ✗ |
| Does it deepen character? | Marginally — we learn sister is overbearing, but this was established in Ch 1 | ✗ |
| Does it escalate tension/stakes? | No — if anything, it releases tension | ✗ |
| Does it deliver on genre promise? | No — no romance, no hero, no heat, no humor that lands | ✗ |

**VERDICT:** CUT

**But I need to show their relationship—**

You showed it in Chapter 1. This scene repeats that information. If the sister relationship matters to the plot later, seed that specific element. A full coffee shop scene where nothing changes is not earning its 800 words.

**What if I really want to keep it?**

Then it needs to DO something. Options:
1. Hero walks in during the conversation — now it has romantic tension
2. Sister reveals information that changes heroine's understanding of the situation
3. Heroine makes a decision during this scene that affects her next action

If you can't make it do one of those things, it goes.
