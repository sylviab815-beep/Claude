# Proofread Checklist

The final polish pass. This is about correctness, consistency, and a clean reading experience. Use during the IDENTIFY phase of Pass 3.

## How to Use This Checklist

Read the manuscript slowly — slower than normal reading speed. Read for errors, not for story. Some editors read backwards (last paragraph first) for spelling; this isn't required but reading at reduced speed is essential.

## CRITICAL: Thoroughness Requirements

**Process every chapter individually.** Do not batch the whole manuscript and look for patterns. Read each chapter on its own and run every section of this checklist against it.

**Run each checklist section as a SEPARATE sweep per chapter.** Do not try to catch everything in one read-through. For each chapter:
- First sweep: Grammar & Syntax ONLY
- Second sweep: Punctuation ONLY
- Third sweep: Spelling & Homophones ONLY
- Fourth sweep: Consistency checks ONLY
This multi-pass approach catches far more than a single "general proofread."

**Minimum expected findings:** A professional proofreader finds 1-3 issues per page (~250 words). For a 60,000-word manuscript (~240 pages), expect 50-150+ corrections. If you find fewer than 5 issues per 10,000 words, you are skimming — go back and run each checklist section as a dedicated sweep.

**No manuscript is error-free.** If your initial scan produces fewer than 30 total issues for a full-length novel, you MUST re-scan chapter by chapter with each category as a separate pass. Do NOT tell the author their manuscript is "clean" or "essentially error-free" — that means you didn't look hard enough.

**Log per-chapter counts.** Every chapter must have an issue count in your output. If any chapter shows zero issues, re-read it — a zero-issue chapter in fiction proofreading is extraordinarily rare.

**Do NOT do formatting cleanup unless specifically asked.** Removing extra whitespace, fixing carriage returns, standardizing paragraph spacing — these are NOT proofreading. If the user asks for a proofread, do grammar, punctuation, spelling, homophones, word choice, and consistency. Period.

## 1. Grammar & Syntax

### Subject-Verb Agreement
- Check compound subjects ("The detective and her partner was → were")
- Check subjects separated from verbs by clauses ("The pile of books on the table were → was")
- Check collective nouns used consistently (team is/are — pick one and stick with it)

### Tense Consistency
- Identify the primary tense (past or present) and flag any unintentional shifts
- Flashbacks should have clear tense transitions in and out
- Internal thoughts in present tense within past-tense narration are fine — but should be consistent in style

### Pronoun Reference
- Every pronoun should have a clear, unambiguous antecedent
- In scenes with two same-gender characters, check that "she/he/they" is never ambiguous
- Check for orphaned "it" and "this" without clear referents

### Modifiers
- Flag dangling modifiers ("Walking down the street, the building came into view" — the building isn't walking)
- Flag misplaced modifiers ("She only ate the cake" vs. "She ate only the cake")
- Flag squinting modifiers (could modify either the preceding or following element)

### Sentence Structure
- Flag comma splices (two independent clauses joined by a comma with no conjunction)
- Flag run-on sentences (two independent clauses with no punctuation or conjunction)
- Flag sentence fragments — acceptable in fiction for style, but flag if they seem accidental

## 2. Punctuation

### Dialogue Punctuation
- Comma before closing quote when followed by a dialogue tag: "Let's go," she said.
- Period before closing quote when followed by action: "Let's go." She grabbed her coat.
- Question marks and exclamation points replace the comma: "Are you sure?" she asked.
- New speaker = new paragraph, always
- Interrupted dialogue uses em-dash inside the quotes: "I was just going to—"
- Trailing off uses ellipsis inside the quotes: "I thought maybe..."

### Em-Dashes
- Consistent style throughout (no spaces: word—word, OR spaces: word — word, pick one)
- Used for interruptions, asides, and emphasis — not as a substitute for all other punctuation
- No more than 2 em-dashes per paragraph (overuse dilutes impact)

### Ellipses
- Consistent style (three dots: ... OR spaced: . . . — pick one)
- Used for trailing off in dialogue or thought, hesitation, omission
- Not overused (more than 3 per chapter suggests a crutch)

### Commas
- Check for Oxford comma consistency (either always use it or never — don't mix)
- Check comma usage in compound sentences (comma before conjunction joining independent clauses)
- No comma between subject and verb, regardless of clause length

### Semicolons
- Used to join closely related independent clauses
- Not overused in fiction (more than 2 per chapter is usually too many)
- Never used to join a dependent and independent clause

### Apostrophes
- Possessives: singular = 's (James's), plural ending in s = s' (the dogs' bowl)
- Check it's (it is) vs. its (possessive) — the most common error in English
- Check contractions match character voice (wouldn't vs. would not)

## 3. Spelling & Word Choice

### Homophones
Scan for these commonly confused pairs:
- their / there / they're
- its / it's
- your / you're
- affect / effect
- than / then
- who's / whose
- lay / lie / laid / lain
- further / farther
- peek / peak / pique
- discrete / discreet
- principal / principle
- complement / compliment
- stationary / stationery
- pour / pore (over)
- reign / rein / rain

### Commonly Misused Words
- "literally" used figuratively (acceptable in casual dialogue, flag in narration)
- "nauseous" (causing nausea) vs. "nauseated" (feeling nausea)
- "begging the question" (a logical fallacy, not "raising the question")
- "could care less" (should be "couldn't care less")
- "hone in" (should be "home in")

### Spelling
- Check character names are spelled consistently throughout
- Check place names and proper nouns
- Check any invented words (fantasy/sci-fi terms, magical terminology) are consistent

## 4. Consistency Tracking

Build a consistency checklist as you read. Track:

### Character Details
| Character | Eye Color | Hair | Age | Height | Other Physical | Quirks/Habits |
|-----------|-----------|------|-----|--------|----------------|---------------|
| [Name] | | | | | | |

Flag any contradictions (blue eyes in Ch. 3, green in Ch. 12).

### Location Details
| Location | Description | Direction/Distance from Other Locations | Key Features |
|----------|-------------|----------------------------------------|--------------|
| [Place] | | | |

Flag contradictions (the bakery is east of the square in Ch. 2, west in Ch. 8).

### Timeline
Track the chronology of events. Flag:
- Days of the week that don't add up
- Seasons/weather inconsistent with the timeline
- Characters referencing events that haven't happened yet
- Travel times that don't match stated distances
- Characters in two places at once

### Magical/World Rules
For fantasy, sci-fi, paranormal — track the rules of the world:
| Rule | First Established | Violations |
|------|------------------|------------|
| [Rule] | Ch. X | |

### Formatting Conventions
- Chapter heading style (Chapter One / Chapter 1 / ONE / 1)
- Scene break markers (*** or ### or — or blank line)
- Internal thought formatting (italics or not)
- Text message / letter formatting
- Flashback formatting

Ensure consistency throughout.

## 5. Final Read-Through

After all corrections are identified, read the full manuscript one more time focusing on:

### Flow
- Do sentences read smoothly when spoken aloud (mentally)?
- Are there any passages where you stumble even though they're technically correct?
- Is the reading rhythm pleasant?

### Reader Experience
- Are there any remaining moments that pull you out of the story?
- Does the first paragraph still hook?
- Does the last paragraph still satisfy?
- Is the title still appropriate after reading the full, edited manuscript?

## Output Format

Produce a correction list organized by type:

```
## Grammar & Syntax (count: N)
- [Ch. X, para Y]: "original text" → "corrected text" — [rule]

## Punctuation (count: N)
- [Ch. X, para Y]: "original text" → "corrected text" — [rule]

## Spelling & Word Choice (count: N)
- [Ch. X, para Y]: "original" → "corrected" — [reason]

## Consistency Issues (count: N)
- [Contradiction description]: Ch. X says [A], Ch. Y says [B] → Standardize to [chosen]

## Formatting (count: N)
- [Issue description] → [Correction]

## Consistency Reference Tables
[Character, Location, Timeline, and Rules tables as built during the read]
```
