# Voice Matching Guide

How to analyze and preserve an author's unique voice during editing. This is the most important skill in the editing pipeline — an invisible edit that makes the author's book better without making it sound like someone else wrote it.

## Why Voice Preservation Matters

Every author has a fingerprint: their sentence rhythms, their metaphor preferences, their balance of dialogue to narration, their humor register, their vocabulary level. An edit that "fixes" the prose by flattening the voice has made the book worse, not better. The goal is always: make this book the best version of *itself*.

## Step 1: Analyze the Voice Before Editing

Before making any changes, read at least 3 chapters and document the following:

### Sentence Architecture
- **Average sentence length:** Short and punchy? Long and flowing? Mixed?
- **Sentence variety:** Does the author vary length, or do they tend toward uniformity?
- **Complexity level:** Simple sentences, compound, complex, compound-complex?
- **Fragment usage:** Does the author use fragments for emphasis? How often?

### Vocabulary Profile
- **Register:** Formal, casual, literary, conversational, academic?
- **Vocabulary range:** Simple and accessible, or rich and unusual?
- **Jargon/slang:** Does the author use field-specific language, regional dialect, era-specific terms?
- **Profanity level:** None, mild, moderate, heavy? Which words specifically?

### Narrative Stance
- **Psychic distance:** How close to the character's thoughts? (Deep POV vs. observational)
- **Introspection ratio:** How much internal thought vs. external action?
- **Description density:** Sparse and clean, or lush and detailed?
- **Sensory preferences:** Which senses does the author lean on? (Visual heavy? Tactile? Olfactory?)

### Dialogue Characteristics
- **Tag style:** Minimal (said/asked only) or varied? Action beats or dialogue tags?
- **Subtext level:** Characters say what they mean, or operate through implication?
- **Humor style:** Witty banter, dry observation, slapstick, dark humor, wordplay?
- **Formality in dialogue:** Do characters speak in contractions? Full sentences? Fragments?

### Pacing Signature
- **Chapter length consistency:** Regular or varied?
- **Scene transition style:** Hard cuts, bridges, time-skips with markers?
- **Action-to-reflection ratio:** Does the author linger on emotional aftermath or cut ahead?

### Metaphor & Imagery
- **Metaphor density:** Frequent and literary, or rare and impact-focused?
- **Source domain:** Where do metaphors come from? (Nature, mechanics, cooking, warfare, music?)
- **Simile vs. metaphor preference:** "Like a" or "was a"?
- **Recurring images:** Any image clusters or motifs?

## Step 2: Create a Voice Profile

After analysis, write a brief Voice Profile (3-5 sentences) that captures the author's style. This profile travels with every FIX subagent to ensure consistency.

**Example Voice Profile:**
"This author writes in a close, conversational first-person present tense with short sentences that punch and longer ones that breathe. Vocabulary is accessible but precise — she'll use the exact right word rather than a fancy one. Humor is dry and self-deprecating, delivered through internal monologue. Metaphors are rare but always drawn from domestic life (cooking, cleaning, home repair). Dialogue is clipped and subtextual — characters rarely say what they actually mean."

## Step 3: Apply the Voice Profile During Editing

### The Matching Test
For every rewritten passage, ask:
1. Could this have been written by the same person who wrote the rest of the manuscript?
2. Does the sentence rhythm match the surrounding paragraphs?
3. Is the vocabulary consistent with the author's range?
4. Would the POV character plausibly think/observe this?

### Common Voice Violations to Avoid

**Don't elevate the vocabulary:**
- If the author writes "big," don't change it to "enormous" or "monumental"
- If the author writes "walked," don't change it to "ambled" or "traversed" unless the author uses that level elsewhere

**Don't smooth out deliberate roughness:**
- Some authors use fragments, run-ons, or comma splices deliberately for voice
- If a pattern appears consistently, it's probably intentional

**Don't add literary devices the author doesn't use:**
- If the author doesn't write extended metaphors, don't add one
- If the author uses simple similes, don't replace them with complex metaphorical structures

**Don't impose your rhythm:**
- If the author writes long, flowing sentences, don't chop them all into short punchy ones
- If the author is terse, don't expand single sentences into descriptive paragraphs

**Don't standardize away personality:**
- Regional spellings, dialect, and intentional non-standard usage are voice features
- Character-specific speech patterns (dropped g's, run-together words) should be preserved

## Step 4: Verify Voice Preservation After Editing

After each FIX phase, the VERIFY phase should specifically check:

1. **Read the opening paragraph of the original and the edit side by side.** Do they feel like the same author?
2. **Read a random mid-manuscript paragraph from both.** Same test.
3. **Read the original's strongest passage and the edit's version.** Has the strength been preserved?
4. **Check that the Voice Profile still accurately describes the edited version.**

If the edited version reads noticeably differently from the original in ways not accounted for by the fixes, the edit has gone too far. Pull back.

## The Golden Rule

When in doubt, leave it. A missed fix is better than a voice violation. The author's personality on the page is more valuable than editorial perfection.
