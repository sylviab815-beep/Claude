# Developmental Assessment Checklist

A comprehensive framework for evaluating fiction manuscripts at the structural level. Use this checklist during the Developmental Assessment.

**CRITICAL: This is an ASSESSMENT ONLY. Do NOT rewrite any prose. Do NOT add content. Do NOT modify the manuscript in any way. Produce a report with issues and suggested approaches. The author decides what to change and how.**

## How to Use This Checklist

Read the full manuscript before evaluating. A developmental edit requires seeing the complete arc, not just individual chapters. As you read, note page/chapter references for every issue. After reading, work through each section below systematically.

Rate each area: **Strong** (no changes needed), **Adequate** (minor tweaks), **Needs Work** (significant revision), or **Critical** (fundamental problem affecting reader experience).

## CRITICAL: Thoroughness Requirements

**Every section below must be addressed in your assessment.** Do not skip sections because the manuscript seems strong. Even a strong manuscript has areas rated "Adequate" that could be improved. A developmental assessment that says "everything is great" is useless to the author.

**Minimum expected findings:** Every manuscript has at least 2-3 "Needs Work" or "Critical" areas. If your assessment finds zero significant issues, you are not applying the checklist rigorously. Re-read each section and look for what could be BETTER, not just what's broken. A developmental editor's job is to find room for improvement, not to validate.

**Be specific with chapter references.** "Pacing sags in the middle" is not useful. "Chapters 8-11 repeat the same emotional beat (protagonist doubts herself) without advancing the external plot — the reader is treading water for 12,000 words" is useful. Every issue must cite specific chapters.

**Rate every section.** Do not leave any section unrated. The author needs to see your assessment of every area, even if it's "Strong — [specific reason]."

## 1. Opening & Hook

- Does the first page establish voice, tone, and genre?
- Is the protagonist introduced within the first chapter with a clear sense of who they are?
- Is the inciting incident present or clearly foreshadowed within the first 10% of the manuscript?
- Does the opening create a question the reader needs answered?
- Is there a reason to keep reading by the end of Chapter 1?

**Common problems:** Starting too early (before the story begins), starting too late (in media res without grounding), opening with weather/waking up/looking in a mirror, info-dumping backstory before the reader cares.

## 2. Story Structure & Plot Architecture

Evaluate the manuscript against standard story structure (adapt terminology to genre):

| Beat | Expected Position | Check |
|------|------------------|-------|
| Opening / Status Quo | 0-10% | Is the protagonist's normal world established? |
| Inciting Incident | 10-15% | Is there a clear disruption that launches the story? |
| First Plot Point / Lock-In | 20-25% | Does the protagonist commit to the central conflict? |
| Midpoint Shift | 45-55% | Does something change the protagonist's understanding or approach? |
| Dark Moment / All Is Lost | 70-80% | Is there a genuine setback with real stakes? |
| Climax | 85-92% | Does the central conflict come to a head? |
| Resolution | 92-100% | Are threads resolved satisfyingly? |

**Check for:**
- Sagging middle (Chapters 5-10 in a 16-chapter novella, Chapters 10-20 in a 30-chapter novel)
- False climaxes (tension peaks too early, then the real climax feels anticlimactic)
- Rushed endings (the last 10% tries to resolve everything in 2 pages)
- Missing midpoint shift (the story feels like two halves of the same thing rather than an evolution)

## 3. Pacing

- Are there any chapters or sections where the story stalls?
- Are action/tension scenes followed by breathing room (and vice versa)?
- Does the pace accelerate as the story approaches the climax?
- Are time-skips handled smoothly or do they jolt the reader?
- Is scene length varied appropriately (not all scenes the same length)?

**Pacing red flags:**
- Multiple consecutive chapters of the same emotional register
- Long stretches of internal monologue without external action
- Travel/transition scenes that don't advance character or plot
- Climactic sequences that describe instead of dramatize

## 4. Character Development

### Protagonist
- Is the protagonist's goal clear (both external and internal)?
- Does the protagonist make meaningful choices that drive the plot?
- Is the protagonist active or reactive? (Active = making decisions. Reactive = things happening to them.)
- Does the protagonist change between the first and last chapter?
- Is the change earned through the events of the story?
- Are the protagonist's flaws specific and story-relevant (not just "clumsy" or "too caring")?

### Antagonist / Opposition
- Is the antagonist motivated by something understandable (even if wrong)?
- Does the antagonist have a plan that would work if the protagonist didn't intervene?
- Is the antagonist present enough in the story (directly or through consequences)?

### Supporting Cast
- Does each significant secondary character serve a story purpose?
- Do secondary characters have their own goals and agency?
- Can you distinguish characters by voice alone (cover the dialogue tags)?
- Are there any redundant characters who could be combined?

## 5. Conflict & Stakes

- Is the central conflict sustained throughout (not resolved too easily at any point)?
- Are stakes personal to the protagonist (not just abstract "the world will end")?
- Do stakes escalate as the story progresses?
- Are there consequences when the protagonist fails or makes mistakes?
- Is there both external conflict (plot) and internal conflict (character arc)?

## 6. Point of View

- Is the POV consistent throughout? (No accidental head-hopping, no omniscient intrusions in close-third or first-person)
- Does the narrative voice match the POV character's personality, education, and worldview?
- Are observations filtered through the POV character's awareness and limitations?
- In first-person: does the narrator have a distinct voice, or does it read like a generic reporter?
- In third-person limited: is the psychic distance consistent within scenes?

## 7. Worldbuilding & Setting

- Is the setting established early enough that the reader isn't floating in white space?
- Is exposition delivered through action and interaction (not lectures)?
- Are the "rules" of the world consistent throughout?
- Does the setting feel lived-in (sensory details, specificity) or generic?
- Is there too much worldbuilding (reader drowning in details they don't need yet)?

## 8. Dialogue Function

At the structural level, dialogue should:
- Reveal character (values, personality, background)
- Advance plot (new information, decisions, confrontations)
- Create or escalate conflict
- Establish relationships and power dynamics

**Structural dialogue problems:**
- Scenes that are pure dialogue with no grounding in setting or action
- Characters explaining plot to each other that they both already know ("As you know, Bob...")
- Dialogue that could be cut entirely without affecting the story
- Conversations that circle the same point without progressing

## 9. Emotional Arc

- Does the story deliver the emotional experience the genre promises?
- Are major emotional beats set up before they pay off?
- Is there emotional variety (not just one note throughout)?
- Are quiet, intimate moments given as much craft as dramatic ones?
- Does the ending leave the reader with the intended emotional residue?

## 10. Genre Expectations

Evaluate against the specific genre's reader contract:

**Romance:** Is the central relationship the main plot engine? Is the HEA/HFN earned? Are both love interests fully developed?

**Mystery/Thriller:** Does the mystery play fair with clues? Is the reveal surprising but inevitable in retrospect? Are red herrings planted convincingly?

**Fantasy/Sci-Fi:** Is the magic system/technology consistent? Does the world serve the story (not the other way around)? Is the learning curve manageable?

**Horror:** Does dread build progressively? Are there genuine scares beyond shock value? Is the threat meaningful?

**Literary:** Is the thematic exploration earned through story events (not lectures)? Is the prose style serving the narrative or overshadowing it?

## Output Format

After completing the evaluation, produce:

1. **Executive Summary** (2-3 paragraphs): Overall assessment — what's working, what's the biggest structural issue, and what's the overall recommendation.

2. **Issue List** organized by severity:
   - **Critical** — Problems that break the reader's experience. Must fix.
   - **Important** — Problems that weaken the story significantly. Should fix.
   - **Minor** — Opportunities for improvement. Nice to fix.

3. **Strengths** — Specific things the author is doing well that must be preserved during editing.

Each issue should include: chapter/page reference, clear description, why it matters to the reader, and a **suggested approach** to fixing it.

**Remember: Suggestions only. Do not apply any changes to the manuscript. The developmental assessment is an editorial letter — the author's creative decisions are theirs to make.**
