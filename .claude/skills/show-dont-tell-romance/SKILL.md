# Show Don't Tell — Romance Edition

A working tool for romance writers who want their AI to stop narrating emotions and start rendering them. This skill operates in two modes and calibrates to heat level.

---

## How to use this skill

**First, identify the mode the writer needs:**

- **DIAGNOSTIC MODE** — the writer pastes a passage and wants it fixed. Flag every instance of telling, explain why it's telling, and propose a showing swap.
- **DRAFTING MODE** — the writer wants a scene written from scratch with show-don't-tell baked in. Ask for the setup, then draft using the rendering rules below.

**Second, calibrate to heat level.** A "he wanted her" moment in Rosemary Irving (heat 1-2) is rendered through held breath and a hand that won't quite land. The same moment in Moxie Savage (heat 5) is rendered through specific physical response and sensory detail. Same principle, completely different execution. Always ask the heat level before swapping or drafting if it isn't clear.

**Third, don't over-correct.** Telling has a job. Use the override rules in the final section before turning every line into a sensory paragraph.

---

## What "telling" actually looks like in romance

Romance writers tell in five specific ways. Scan for these patterns.

### 1. Emotion labels
The writer names the emotion instead of rendering it.
- "She was nervous."
- "He felt jealous."
- "Her heart was full of longing."
- "She was falling in love with him."

### 2. State-of-being verbs doing the heavy lifting
*Was, were, felt, seemed, appeared, looked.* These verbs aren't banned — but when they're carrying the emotional weight of the sentence, the writer is summarizing instead of rendering.
- "He was angry."
- "She seemed distant."
- "The room felt charged."

### 3. Summary narration
The writer compresses a scene that should be experienced.
- "They argued for an hour about the wedding."
- "She spent the whole dinner trying not to look at him."
- "He thought about her all day."

### 4. Internal labeling
The POV character diagnoses their own feelings in a way no human actually thinks.
- "He knew he was in love with her."
- "She realized she'd been waiting her whole life for this."
- "He understood that he wanted her."

### 5. Reaction shortcuts
One physical cue is doing the work of three or four.
- "Her heart raced." (that's the only body response)
- "She blushed." (and nothing else)
- "He felt heat pool in his stomach." (with no specific sensation)

## Diagnostic mode — how to run it

When a writer pastes a passage:

1. **Read the whole passage first.** Don't flag line-by-line on first read.
2. **Identify the emotional beats** the scene is trying to land.
3. **Tag each instance of telling** using the five categories above.
4. **For each tag, propose one showing swap** calibrated to the writer's heat level and pen name voice.
5. **Flag any sentences where telling is actually correct** (see override rules) so the writer doesn't over-correct.

**Output format:**

```
ORIGINAL: [quoted sentence]
TAG: [which of the 5 patterns]
WHY: [one sentence on what's being summarized]
SWAP: [rewritten sentence]
```

Keep the feedback specific. Don't say "this is telling" and move on — name the pattern and show the fix.

## Drafting mode — how to run it

When a writer asks for a scene drafted fresh:

**Before drafting, confirm:**
- Pen name / heat level / genre
- POV character
- What the scene needs to accomplish emotionally (tension? surrender? rupture? repair?)
- Setting and any physical stakes
- Any voice or style notes

**Then draft using these rules:**

1. **Name the body, not the feeling.** Instead of "she was nervous," render it: a swallow, a thumb pressed to a pulse point, a breath held half a second too long.
2. **Use the five senses unevenly.** Pick the two that matter for this scene. Romance usually lives in touch and sight; dark romance lives in touch and smell; historical often lives in sight and sound. Don't do all five — it flattens everything.
3. **Let dialogue carry subtext.** What the character doesn't say is often the showing. "I should go" carries more longing than "she wanted to stay."
4. **Anchor internal reaction to a specific trigger.** Not "she felt drawn to him" but "her hand moved before her brain caught up."
5. **Stay in real time for emotional beats.** If the scene is doing emotional work, don't compress it.

## The swap library

Before/after pairs organized by emotional beat. Examples span heat levels — match to the writer's pen name.

### Attraction / first awareness

**TELL:** She thought he was the most attractive man she'd ever seen.

**SHOW (heat 1-2):** She'd been raised not to stare. She stared anyway.

**SHOW (heat 3-4):** He turned, and she lost the next three seconds of her life.

**SHOW (heat 5):** Her body knew him before her brain caught up — that specific, stupid pull she'd spent two years pretending wasn't real.

---

### Nervousness / anticipation

**TELL:** She was nervous about seeing him again.

**SHOW (heat 1-2):** She'd changed her earrings three times. She was not a woman who changed her earrings.

**SHOW (heat 3-4):** She checked her lipstick in the car mirror, then wiped it off, then put it back on. Her hands wouldn't settle.

**SHOW (heat 5):** She sat in the parked car for four minutes before she could make herself open the door.

---

### Longing / want (clothes on)

**TELL:** He wanted her more than he'd wanted anything in his life.

**SHOW (heat 1-2):** His hand hovered near hers on the armrest. He did not close the distance. He thought about closing the distance for the rest of the flight.

**SHOW (heat 3-4):** He watched her laugh at something the bartender said and had to look away. His grip on the glass had gone white-knuckled without him noticing.

**SHOW (heat 5):** Every cell in his body was tracking her across the room. He knew the exact second she'd noticed him noticing. He knew what she was going to do about it.

---

### Arousal / physical response

**TELL:** She felt heat pool in her stomach when he touched her.

**SHOW (heat 1-2):** His thumb brushed her wrist. Her pulse answered before she could stop it.

**SHOW (heat 3-4):** His hand settled at the small of her back and stayed there. The heat of his palm soaked through her dress. She forgot what she'd been saying.

**SHOW (heat 5):** His mouth was at her ear before she'd processed him crossing the room. One hand at her hip. The other, slower, finding the hem of her skirt. She forgot how to breathe on purpose.

---

### Jealousy

**TELL:** He was jealous of the man she was talking to.

**SHOW:** He watched the bartender lean in. He watched her laugh. He counted the ways he could end the bartender's evening and picked the one that wouldn't get him arrested.

---

### Grief / emotional rupture

**TELL:** She was devastated when he left.

**SHOW:** She didn't cry. She made tea. She made a second cup of tea because she'd forgotten about the first one. Then she sat on the kitchen floor and didn't get up for a long time.

---

### Tenderness / intimacy (non-sexual)

**TELL:** He loved her so much it hurt.

**SHOW:** She'd fallen asleep against his shoulder. His arm had gone numb an hour ago. He hadn't moved.

---

### Post-coital / afterglow

**TELL:** She felt close to him after.

**SHOW (heat 3-4):** He traced a line down her spine, absent, like he wasn't even aware he was doing it. She decided not to tell him.

**SHOW (heat 5):** His mouth was at her temple. Her leg was over his. Neither of them had spoken in a while. It was the first time in a year she'd felt like a person.

---

### Anger / rupture

**TELL:** She was furious with him.

**SHOW:** She set the coffee cup down. Carefully. Like if she set it down hard enough to matter, she'd throw it.

---

### Relief / surrender

**TELL:** She finally let herself trust him.

**SHOW:** She'd been holding her shoulders somewhere up near her ears for six months. In the kitchen, with his hand on the back of her neck, they dropped. She didn't notice until later.

## Heat-level calibration rules

The principle is the same across heat levels. The execution is not.

**Heat 1-2 (sweet/historical/clean):**
- Showing lives in what *almost* happens.
- Hand that hovers. Breath that catches. Distance that doesn't close.
- Physical response is muted: a pulse, a swallow, a held breath.
- Subtext does the heavy lifting. What she can't say out loud is the point.

**Heat 3-4 (sensual/contemporary romance):**
- Showing lives in specific physical response and intention.
- Hands that do land. Breath that audibly changes. Dialogue that admits it.
- Physical response is honest but not explicit: heat, flush, tightened grip, forgotten sentence.
- Subtext is still doing work but the characters are starting to name things.

**Heat 5 (dark romance / mafia / explicit):**
- Showing lives in specific sensory detail and acknowledged want.
- The body knows what it wants and tracks the other person accordingly.
- Physical response is named with precision — not purple, not flowery, but specific.
- Subtext becomes power dynamic: who acknowledges the want first, who controls the pace.

**Rule across all heat levels:** Avoid the banned CPH clichés. No tangled sheets. No slick folds. No heart racing as the only body cue. No heat pooling. These are telling dressed as showing.

## When telling is actually correct — the override rules

Don't over-correct. Telling has three legitimate jobs in romance:

1. **Transitions and time skips.** "Three weeks passed." "They spent the summer writing letters." Compressing off-screen time is telling and that's fine.
2. **Low-stakes beats.** Not every sentence needs sensory grounding. "She made coffee" is fine if coffee isn't the point.
3. **Pacing control.** After a sensory-heavy scene, one or two lines of summary lets the reader breathe before the next emotional beat lands.

**Rule of thumb:** If the scene is doing emotional work, show it. If the scene is moving the characters from Point A to Point B, telling is fine.

## Common AI failures in romance show-don't-tell

Flag these when you see them. They're the patterns that make AI-generated romance feel flat.

1. **Universal body cues.** Racing hearts, pooling heat, fluttering stomachs. Specific body = specific character. Generic body = generic scene.
2. **Stacking physical responses without cause.** A heroine whose heart races AND whose stomach flutters AND whose breath catches in the same sentence is having a medical event, not a romantic one. Pick one.
3. **Naming the emotion immediately after showing it.** "Her hands trembled. She was nervous." The second sentence erases the first. Trust the reader.
4. **Over-describing the hero's body as a proxy for attraction.** Six-pack inventories are telling. The heroine's specific reaction to him is showing.
5. **Using weather as emotional shorthand.** Rain = sad. Sun = happy. Lazy. Let the characters carry the weight.

---

## Quick checklist for the writer

Before sending a romance scene out, scan for:

- [ ] Any sentence with "was/were/felt/seemed" that's carrying emotional weight
- [ ] Any named emotion (nervous, jealous, angry, in love)
- [ ] Any compressed emotional beat ("they argued," "she cried")
- [ ] Any universal body cue (racing heart, pooled heat, fluttering stomach)
- [ ] Any sentence where the POV character diagnoses their own feelings

If any of these are in a scene doing emotional work, swap them. If they're in a transitional beat, leave them alone.
