# Prose Craft Rules for Thrillers

These rules apply during WRITING, not just revision.

---

## Rule 1: Tension Through Implication, Not Declaration

Never write "a chill ran down her spine" or "he felt a growing sense of dread." Create dread through what the character NOTICES — the detail that's wrong, the silence where sound should be, the thing that doesn't belong.

**BAD:** "I felt a sudden wave of fear when I saw the open door."
**GOOD:** "The front door was open. I'd locked it. I'd checked it twice because I always check it twice."

**BAD:** "Something about the room felt wrong, filling me with unease."
**GOOD:** "The coffee mug was on the left side of the desk. I'm right-handed. I've never put it on the left."

**BAD:** "A sense of danger hung in the air."
**GOOD:** "The neighbor's dog wasn't barking. That dog barked at everything — mail carriers, squirrels, wind. It wasn't barking."

**Self-check:** Delete the fear/dread/unease label. If the reader still feels unsettled from the details alone, you didn't need it.

---

## Rule 2: Dialogue Is a Weapon

In thrillers, every conversation is a negotiation. Characters are extracting information, concealing information, testing loyalty, or establishing dominance. No conversation is casual — even casual conversations reveal something.

**BAD:**
"Did you know the victim?"
"We were acquaintances."
"How well did you know her?"
"Not that well."

**GOOD:**
"Did you know her?"
"Everyone knew Diane." He picked at the label on his beer. "She was hard to miss."
"That's not what I asked."
The label came off in one piece. He lined it up on the bar, smoothing the wrinkles. "We talked a few times. At fundraisers. She was always at fundraisers."
"You remember what she was always at."
He looked at me then. "I remember what everybody's always at. It's a small town."

### Dialogue Tells for Liars
Characters who are lying or hiding something should exhibit specific patterns:
- Over-specificity (too much detail about an alibi)
- Deflection (answering a question with a question, or answering a different question)
- Rehearsed quality (the words sound prepared)
- Physical tells (but NOT obvious ones like "he looked away" — subtler: adjusting a watch, reorganizing items, becoming very still)
- Contrasting registers (formal language where casual would be natural, or vice versa)

### Dialogue Tells for Truth
When a character finally tells the truth, it should FEEL different:
- Simpler language (lies are complex, truth is plain)
- Shorter sentences
- Unexpected admissions (they say something against their own interest)
- Physical release (a breath, a slump, hands unclenching)

## Rule 3: Pacing Through Sentence Structure

Thriller pacing is controlled at the sentence level, not just the chapter level.

### When Tension Is Rising
Short sentences. Fragments. The rhythm accelerates.

"I checked the hallway. Empty. The stairwell. Empty. The office at the end — the door was closed. It shouldn't have been closed."

### When Dread Is Building
Longer sentences that carry the reader through unease without letting them pause.

"I stood in the parking garage for eleven minutes, watching the elevator indicator cycle through floors — three, five, nine, five, three, ground — and I couldn't tell if someone was coming down or if the elevator was simply moving through its nightly routine, the kind of mechanical pattern that means nothing until the night it means everything."

### During Revelation
One clean, devastating sentence after buildup.

"The files went back three years. She'd been watching me since before we met."

### During Action
Ultra-short. Present tense if the book allows. Sensory fragments.

"Glass. Floor. My hands on tile. Ringing in my left ear. Movement in the doorway — I roll before I think."

## Rule 4: The Unreliable Narrator Requires Precision

If the POV character is unreliable, every lie or omission must follow rules:

### Types of Unreliability
- **Lying to the reader**: The narrator directly states something false. (Most difficult to execute well.)
- **Omitting**: The narrator doesn't mention something crucial. The reader doesn't notice the absence.
- **Misinterpreting**: The narrator describes events accurately but draws wrong conclusions. The reader can see the truth if they look past the narrator's interpretation.
- **Self-deceiving**: The narrator genuinely believes something false about themselves. Their actions contradict their self-description.

### The Fairness Rule
An unreliable narrator must play fair:
- They can omit, but they can't directly lie about something the reader could verify from context
- Their unreliability must be CONSISTENT (if they misinterpret one type of situation, they should misinterpret similar situations)
- There must be moments where the mask slips — where attentive readers can catch the discrepancy
- The reveal must recontextualize, not contradict — everything the narrator said should mean something different, not be proven false

## Rule 5: Settings Create Claustrophobia

Every setting should make the reader feel slightly trapped — even if it's an open field.

**BAD:** "The office was a standard corporate space — fluorescent lights, cubicles, the hum of computers."
**GOOD:** "The office was the kind of quiet that means either everyone's gone home or everyone's pretending to work. I counted six people at their desks. Four of them weren't typing."

**BAD:** "The house was isolated, surrounded by woods."
**GOOD:** "The nearest neighbor was a fourteen-minute drive. I knew because I'd timed it, twice, wondering how fast I could get there if I had to."

### Setting as Threat
- Rooms should have exits the reader (and protagonist) are aware of
- Travel times between locations should feel calibrated (the protagonist knows how long it takes to get to safety)
- "Safe" spaces should have one unsettling detail (the lock that doesn't work well, the window that faces the wrong direction, the neighbor who's always watching)
- Weather and time of day are tools — darkness limits information, rain covers sound, crowds create anonymity or exposure

---

## Rule 6: The Clock Is Always Audible

Even without a literal ticking clock, the reader should always feel that time matters.

**Explicit clock:** "The trial started Monday. I had four days."
**Implicit clock:** "The pattern was every eleven days. It had been nine."
**Personal clock:** "My daughter's school let out at 3:15. It was 2:48."

Make time specific. Exact numbers create urgency. "A few days" creates nothing.

---

## Rule 7: Chapter Endings Are Hooks, Never Resolutions

Every chapter must end on one of:
- **A revelation** (new information that changes things)
- **A question** (something the reader needs answered)
- **A dread beat** (a detail that implies danger)
- **A decision** (the protagonist commits to an action that puts them at risk)
- **A cliffhanger** (an interrupted action)

NEVER end a chapter on:
- Resolution (the problem is solved — even temporarily)
- Reflection (the protagonist processes what happened)
- Calm (things settle down)
- Summary (the narrator wraps up what just happened)

The chapter ending is the engine of "one more chapter." If the reader can put the book down at the end of a chapter, the ending failed.

---

## Rule 8: Violence Has Weight

Whether the book is clean suspense or extreme dark, violence must never feel casual.

### Clean Suspense
Violence is implied, threatened, or happens off-page. The THREAT of violence is the tool. A gun in a drawer. A scar the protagonist doesn't explain. A news report about someone who wasn't so lucky.

### Moderate
Violence is present but not lingered on. The impact matters more than the mechanics. "The blow caught me behind the ear and the world tilted" — not a blow-by-blow description.

### Dark
Violence is visceral and specific. The sensory experience is unflinching. But it must serve the story — every violent scene changes something (a relationship, the stakes, the protagonist's psychology). Gratuitous violence numbs the reader.

### The Aftermath Rule
The best thrillers spend more time on the AFTERMATH of violence than the violence itself. The shaking hands. The inability to eat. The way a door that used to be just a door is now a threat. Violence that has no aftermath has no weight.

## Rule 9: Subtext Carries the Real Conversation

In thrillers, what characters DON'T say is more important than what they do say. Every important conversation should have a subtext layer.

**Surface:** Two colleagues discussing a project.
**Subtext:** One knows the other is compromised. The other suspects they know.

The reader should be able to read the scene twice — once for surface, once for subtext — and get a different experience each time.

### How to Write Subtext
- Characters talk AROUND the real subject
- Pauses and redirections carry meaning
- Physical behavior contradicts verbal behavior
- Questions are answered with questions
- One character pushes; the other deflects — the pattern itself is the communication

---

## Rule 10: The Ending Earns Its Impact

The climax and resolution of a thriller must feel INEVITABLE — not just surprising.

**Clean Resolution:** The protagonist defeats the threat using the specific skill/insight they developed through the story. Justice is clear.

**Ambiguous Ending:** The threat is handled but something remains unresolved — a final image or detail that suggests the world isn't fully safe. The reader is left thinking.

**Twist Ending:** A final revelation that recontextualizes the entire story. Must survive the "does this hold up if I re-read the book?" test.

Whatever the ending type — the reader should close the book and sit with it. The best thriller endings create a silence after the last page.
