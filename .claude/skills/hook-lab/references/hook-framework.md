# Hook Framework — FB Ad Hooks (reference)

The hook is the first text a reader processes on a book ad. It exists anywhere
you compete for attention (Reels, YouTube, email subject lines). On an ad it can
live in the **image text**, the **video text**, the **headline**, and the
**primary text** — but the primary text is read last, so the hook that matters
most is the one on the creative itself.

You can **layer** hooks across these slots, and an ad hook should be much shorter
than a back-cover blurb because you have far less room and far less attention.

---

## The four hook types ("the four horsemen")

Nearly every book/ad hook is one of these — or a deliberate blend.

### 1. Curiosity gap
An open or implied question the reader wants answered. The best ones have **no
obvious, trivial answer**.
- *"What would you sacrifice to remember?"*
- *"Twenty students. One didn't exist."*

### 2. Emotional resonance
Triggers a feeling the audience **instantly recognizes**. People buy on emotion,
especially with fiction.
- *"She never expected to miss a place she'd never been."*
- *"He'd been gone for three years — and then the letter arrived."*

### 3. Provocative statement
A bold, controversial, or arresting claim that halts the scroll. Doesn't have to
enrage — just stop the thumb.
- *"Humanity was a virus."*
- The **outrage** sub-type makes the reader indignant on a character's behalf so
  they want to see justice. Effective but use carefully; frame it clearly as on
  behalf of the character.

### 4. Story fragment
A snippet that implies a much larger story. Great for mystery/thriller/suspense.
- *"The door was open — and it was never supposed to be."*
- *"The signal came from a dead planet."*

**Default pick:** if you can only run one, the **curiosity gap** generally
converts best — but only when it matches the book. You may blend types (a line
can be both emotionally resonant and provocative).

## Match the hook to the book: character vs. plot focus

Decide first whether the ad is **character-focused** or **plot-focused**. Every
image and every line lands in one bucket. Genre usually decides:

- **Plot-focused** (e.g. military sci-fi): you basically can't run the ad without
  referencing the plot — external forces drive every choice. Lead with plot.
- **Character-focused** (e.g. slow-burn / contemporary romance): you can run most
  of it on who the characters are and what they need vs. what they're offered.
  Hinting at conflict still helps.

Match the **promise** of the hook to the actual reading experience. A high-octane
curiosity hook on a quiet slice-of-life book draws readers who bounce — and leave
one-star reviews. The hook must "jive" with the book.

---

## What makes a hook NOT work (failure modes)

1. **It describes the picture or the book (telling, not showing).**
   "Show, don't tell" applies to ads. Bad: *"Join the adventure in this sci-fi
   epic set in the year 3000."* The **image** should already say "far-future
   sci-fi"; the text should add intrigue the image can't.

2. **It reads like a sales pitch.**
   People pattern-match "ad" and scroll past. (Real data point from the class: a
   well-known creator's video that looked sponsored lost ~75% of the audience who
   would normally have watched.) **Hook first, sell second** — never lead with
   the pitch.

3. **It's generic review copy.**
   "Couldn't put it down," "everything changes," "this book delivered" fit any
   book, so they say nothing. Use only specific, surprising call-outs.

4. **It requires context to understand.**
   Don't pack in unfamiliar character/place/kingdom names. Readers won't decode
   five unknown proper nouns. Use **roles** — the princess, the prince, the
   duchess, the cartel. (Most common in sci-fi and urban fantasy.)

5. **It leans on insider jargon.**
   Deep niche terms alienate non-experts. Exception: if you're deliberately
   filtering for super-fans, jargon can work as a qualifier.

## Format: static image vs. video

### Static image (most forgiving)
- ~**1080 × 1080** (1:1). Lots of room; easy to use **negative space**.
- Text often **centered**; if the character is on one side, put text on the other.
- More usable text room than you'd expect.

### Video (tighter)
- Larger canvas (~**1920 × 1080**) but **only the center square is reliably
  safe** — top/bottom get cropped per placement. Effectively *less* room.
- Subject usually **centered**, and things **move** — so use **bigger text**, keep
  it tight, and lean on the one-two punch.
- Upside: motion itself grabs attention.

## Craft rules

- **One-breath rule:** the hook must be readable in a single breath. Need a second
  breath? It's too long. (The big one.)
- **Make text BIG:** start as large as fits; only shrink if you must. High
  contrast, readable in a fraction of a second. Cut words before shrinking font.
- **Eye flow (Z-pattern):** image stops the scroll → eye reads image text →
  headline → CTA button → then circles back to primary text. The image stops the
  scroll; primary text is read last.
- **Character eyeline:** we read left→right, top→down. The character should look
  **toward** the text. Flip the image if their gaze points away.
- **Don't duplicate:** the image hook, headline, and primary text should support
  one another, not repeat the same words.
- **Punctuation discipline:** ration exclamation points; over-punctuation reads as
  hype.
- **Legibility trick:** put a **semi-transparent black shape behind the text** and
  send it backward; pure white text on the darkened area. Fast and makes big text
  pop.

## The one-two punch (video reveal)

Reveal the hook in two beats: **setup card → payoff card**.

- **Timing is critical:** ~**2 seconds** to hold attention, so the first beat
  must land — and the transition fire — **before ~2 seconds**. Moving video buys
  a little forgiveness; a still-background video needs the transition even faster.
- **Bridge words** ("but," "and," "so," "until") connect the cards — but **never**
  on the opening line; the first beat must hook alone.
- **Read it aloud as you watch** to confirm each beat is readable in its window.

Example: **"One summer. One promise."** → **"One reason she never went back."**

## Show-don't-tell conversion bank

| Telling (weak) | Showing (strong) | Type |
|---|---|---|
| "Set aboard a starship in deep space." | "The signal came from a dead planet." | story fragment / curiosity |
| "A story of love and loss." | "He'd been gone for three years — and then the letter arrived." | emotional |
| "A magic sword with a curse." | "The blade reverses every hand that's held it." | curiosity |
| "A suspenseful story set in a school." | "Twenty students. One didn't exist." | curiosity |
| "She has to decide whether to go home." | "She promised she'd never go back." | emotional / story fragment |

Pattern: take the telling line and rework until it triggers curiosity, emotion,
provocation, or a story fragment. If you can't name which of the four it's doing,
it's still telling.

## Beyond the hook (mention when asked)

- **Headlines:** don't duplicate the image hook; make the headline *flow from*
  it. Facebook usually shows 1–2; test 2–3, not 5. Use the headline to add social
  proof or an offer ("Free in Kindle Unlimited," "5,000 five-star ratings") when
  the image hook is already strong.
- **Book cover on the ad:** trade-off. Putting the cover on signals "book" and
  helps target readers — but makes the ad look more like an ad (lower stops,
  Facebook may charge more). If you remove it, everything else must scream "book."
  Test both.
- **Display link / "Offer":** the display link can do double duty
  (`amazon.com/kindle` to signal KU). The word "Offer" renders bold and grabs the
  eye — good for box sets, pricing, KU.
- **Primary text:** mobile shows ~125 characters before "…more." Front-load the
  curiosity; the cut-off itself can create it. Keep it from sounding like an
  e-commerce/checkout process. Comp call-outs ("for fans of …") help readers slot
  the book — but the wrong comp draws the wrong readers.
