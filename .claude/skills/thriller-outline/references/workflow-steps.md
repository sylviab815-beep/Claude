# Workflow Steps — Thriller Outline

Detailed instructions for each phase. Complete each step, pause for input.

## IMPORTANT: Names & Places to Avoid

**Names to avoid**: Sarah/Jack/Kate/Tom as "generic thriller protagonist." No Blackwood, Grayson, Hawkins, Sterling, Cross, Stone, Drake, Shaw as surnames meant to sound "tough." No Dr. Marcus/Elena as the brilliant villain. No "Detective [Hard Surname]."

**Place names to avoid**: Blackwood Estates, Ravenwood, Oakbrook, Pinecrest, Willowdale, Westmore. No "[Dark Word]+[Residential Suffix]" formulas. No "the quiet town of [Pleasant Name] where nothing bad ever happens."

**Title words to avoid**: Silent, Dark, Hidden, Twisted, Shattered, Broken, Gone, Missing, Girl, Woman, Wife, Lie, Truth, Secret, Last, Final, Dead, Blood, Night, Stranger, Watch, Behind, Beneath, Locked.

**Instead**: Use real-world naming patterns. Thrillers benefit from specificity — real city neighborhoods, actual job titles, names that feel like the protagonist's demographic, not a casting call. The more grounded the names, the more real the threat feels.

## Step 1: Thriller Subgenre Selection

Ask the user eight questions using AskUserQuestion. ONE AT A TIME.

### Question 1: Thriller Subgenre

**"What kind of thriller are we building?"**

Options:
- "Psychological Thriller — unreliable narrators, mind games, 'is this real?' tension"
- "Domestic Thriller — secrets in relationships, marriages, families, neighbors"
- "Crime Thriller — criminal enterprise, heists, investigations from the criminal's side"
- "Legal Thriller — courtroom drama, legal conspiracy, justice system corruption"
- "Medical/Scientific Thriller — hospital, pharmaceutical, biotech, pandemic, research gone wrong"
- "Political Thriller — government conspiracy, espionage-adjacent, power corruption"
- "Techno Thriller — cyber crime, AI, surveillance, hacking, tech companies"
- "Espionage/Spy Thriller — intelligence agencies, moles, double agents, international stakes"
- "Action Thriller — survival, chase, physical danger, ticking clock"
- "Conspiracy Thriller — layered conspiracy, nobody is who they seem, institutional corruption"
- "Something else — I'll describe"

### Question 2: Protagonist Type

**"Who is your protagonist?"**

Options:
- "Ordinary Person in Extraordinary Situation — wrong place, wrong time, in over their head"
- "Professional Under Pressure — cop, lawyer, doctor, agent doing their job when everything goes wrong"
- "The Outsider / Investigator — someone who stumbles onto something and can't let it go"
- "The Flawed Expert — highly skilled but compromised by addiction, trauma, past mistakes"
- "The Victim Who Fights Back — targeted, hunted, turns the tables"
- "Dual/Multiple Protagonists — shifting perspectives, converging stories"
- "The Antihero — morally compromised protagonist the reader roots for anyway"
- "Custom — I'll describe"

### Question 3: POV Structure

**"How should we handle point of view?"**

Options:
- "Single POV (First Person) — deep inside one head, claustrophobic, unreliable narrator potential"
- "Single POV (Third Person Close) — tight focus on one character, controlled information"
- "Dual POV — protagonist and antagonist (or protagonist and victim), alternating"
- "Multiple POV — 3+ perspectives, puzzle-box structure"
- "Mixed — primarily one POV with occasional shifts for key scenes"

### Question 4: Twist Complexity

**"How twisty should this be?"**

Options:
- "One Major Twist — a single, devastating revelation that reframes the entire story"
- "Two-Layer — a mid-book twist that changes the game, then a final twist that recontextualizes everything"
- "Puzzle-Box — multiple revelations layered throughout, each one shifting what the reader thought they knew"
- "No Major Twist — straight-ahead tension and escalation, the suspense IS the story"

### Question 5: Pacing Style

**"What's the pacing feel?"**

Options:
- "Slow Burn — mounting dread, creeping paranoia, the tension is in what's NOT happening"
- "Relentless — fast-paced from page one, short chapters, constant escalation"
- "Ratcheting — alternates between pressure and release, each pressure peak higher than the last"
- "Dual Timeline — past and present converging, each timeline building tension for the other"

### Question 6: Ticking Clock

**"Is there a deadline driving the story?"**

Options:
- "Yes — a literal ticking clock (trial date, countdown, deadline, event)"
- "Yes — an escalating pattern (the killer strikes again every X days, the disease is spreading)"
- "Soft deadline — a general sense that time is running out, but no specific clock"
- "No clock — the tension comes from escalating stakes, not time pressure"

### Question 7: Trope Selection

**"Which thriller tropes do you want to include? (Pick 2-4)"**

Options:
- "Unreliable Narrator — the reader can't fully trust the person telling the story"
- "The Past Comes Back — a buried event resurfaces and threatens everything"
- "Nobody Is Who They Seem — identities, motivations, and allegiances shift"
- "Gaslighting / Manipulation — one character systematically distorts another's reality"
- "The Wrong Person Is Blamed — protagonist is suspected or framed"
- "Whistleblower / Cover-Up — someone discovers institutional corruption"
- "The Missing Person — a disappearance drives the investigation"
- "Cat and Mouse — protagonist and antagonist actively hunting each other"
- "Trapped / Contained — limited setting, can't escape, pressure builds"
- "The Conspiracy Goes Higher — every answer reveals a bigger, more powerful enemy"
- "Double Cross — an ally betrays the protagonist at a critical moment"
- "The Protagonist Is Complicit — they're not just a victim, they're partly responsible"

### Question 8: Story Length

**"Novella or novel?"**

Options:
- "Novella (~20,000-40,000 words, 16-25 chapters)"
- "Standard Novel (~50,000-70,000 words, 28-35 chapters)"
- "Extended Novel (~80,000-100,000 words, 40-50 chapters — complex thriller)"

## Step 3: Generate 5 Story Ideas

Generate 5 unique thriller ideas based on selections. Each must:

- Match the chosen subgenre with a specific, original scenario
- Feature the chosen protagonist type with a clear vulnerability
- Include the selected tropes working together (not just listed)
- Have a twist structure matching the chosen complexity
- Include a ticking clock if chosen
- Have a title avoiding ALL banned words
- Include a 2-3 sentence premise
- Briefly describe: the protagonist's normal life, the inciting disruption, what they stand to lose, and why they can't just walk away

**Present all 5, use AskUserQuestion to pick.**

---

## Step 4: Hook and Pitch

Generate:
- **Hook**: One sentence that creates instant dread or curiosity. The reader should feel unsafe.
- **Pitch**: 150-250 words establishing the protagonist, the threat, what they stand to lose, why they can't escape, and the ticking clock (if applicable). Should make the reader unable to put it down.

Present and wait.

---

## Step 5: Protagonist Bio

Generate using the Protagonist Bio Format. The protagonist must:
- Have a specific vulnerability the antagonist can exploit
- Have a blind spot that creates complications
- Be capable enough to be credible but limited enough to be in real danger
- Have something specific to lose (not just "their life" — their marriage, their career, their child, their sanity, their freedom)
- Behave in specific ways under pressure (not just "determined")

**Present and ask for feedback.**

---

## Step 6: Antagonist Design

Generate using the Antagonist Bio Format. The antagonist must:
- Have a motive that makes sense (even if it's horrifying)
- Be genuinely formidable — smarter, better-resourced, or more ruthless than the protagonist in specific ways
- Have an information advantage (they know something the protagonist doesn't)
- Also have an information vulnerability (there's something they don't know that will matter)
- Not be "generically evil" — their method should be specific and connected to the subgenre

If psychological/domestic: the antagonist may be hiding in plain sight.
If conspiracy: the antagonist may be an institution, not a person.
If the protagonist doesn't know who the antagonist is yet — plan the reveal.

**Present and ask for feedback.**

---

## Step 7: The Information Architecture

This is where thriller outlining diverges from all other genres. Build the information control map:

### The Truth
State the COMPLETE truth of what's happening in 3-5 sentences. Everything — who did what, why, and what's really going on. This is the answer the reader is working toward.

### The Information Release Schedule
Map what the reader learns and when:

| Timing | What's Revealed | What It Seems to Mean | What It Actually Means |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ch 1-3 | [initial situation] | [surface reading] | [real significance, seen in hindsight] |
| Ch 8-10 | [mid-book revelation] | [the reader recalculates] | [but they're still missing a piece] |
| ... | ... | ... | ... |

### Misdirection Strategy
For each twist, map:
- What the reader is MEANT to believe at this point
- What evidence supports that false belief
- What evidence contradicts it (hidden in plain sight)
- When the truth emerges and how

### Red Herrings
List each red herring:
- What it is
- What false conclusion it supports
- When and how it's debunked
- The real, satisfying explanation for the suspicious thing

Use AskUserQuestion: **"Does this information architecture feel right? Are the reveals satisfying?"**

---

## Step 8: Twist Logic

For each planned twist:

### The Twist
State it clearly.

### The Setup (what the reader sees before)
List 3-5 specific moments that set up the twist — each one must appear in the outline.

### The Logic Test
Answer: "If a reader goes back after the twist, can they find the clues? Does the twist HOLD UP?"

### The Emotional Impact
What does the twist make the reader FEEL? (Betrayal, horror, dread, satisfaction, recontextualization)

### What Changes After
How does the twist change the story going forward? (New stakes, new enemy, new understanding)

**Present and ask for feedback.**

---

## Step 9: Supporting Cast

Generate bios using the Supporting Character Bio Format. Include:

- **The Confidant / Ally**: Someone the protagonist trusts (are they right to?)
- **The Red Herring Suspect**: Someone who looks guilty but isn't
- **The Information Source**: Someone who has a piece of the puzzle
- **The Complication**: Someone whose needs conflict with the protagonist's mission
- **The Victim / Stakes Character**: The person whose safety the protagonist is fighting for (if applicable)

For each, note their **trustworthiness** and what **information they hold**.

**Present and ask for feedback.**

---

## Step 10: Settings & Atmosphere

Create settings that contribute to tension:

### Key Locations (4-6)
For each:
- Name and description (atmosphere, sensory detail)
- Why it's threatening or claustrophobic
- Key scenes set here
- How the setting creates or amplifies tension

Thriller settings should feel like they're closing in. Even "safe" spaces should have escape routes the reader is calculating.

**Present and ask for feedback.**

## Step 11: Detailed Chapter Outline

Use AskUserQuestion for chapter count.

Generate the full outline. **Every chapter must include ALL elements from the Chapter Outline Format in SKILL.md.**

### Thriller Structure Guide

**Act 1 (Chapters 1-20%): The Hook**
- Establish the protagonist's world and what they stand to lose
- The inciting disruption — something is WRONG
- Initial investigation/response — the protagonist engages
- First false lead or misdirection
- The antagonist's presence is felt (even if not seen)
- Tension: 3-5 range, climbing. The unease is building.

**Act 2A (Chapters 20-40%): Down the Rabbit Hole**
- The situation is worse than it seemed
- Each answer reveals a bigger question
- The protagonist's resources start failing — trusted systems, trusted people
- A major revelation shifts the reader's understanding
- If ticking clock: it becomes real
- If dual POV: the antagonist's chapters show competence and planning
- Tension: 5-7, steady climb with brief dips for breathing room

**Midpoint (Chapter ~50%): The Game Changes**
- A revelation, betrayal, or escalation that fundamentally alters the story
- What the protagonist thought was happening is NOT what's happening
- New stakes, new rules, higher danger
- If twist: the first major twist may land here
- Tension: spikes to 8-9

**Act 2B (Chapters 50-75%): The Squeeze**
- The protagonist is increasingly isolated
- Resources depleted, allies gone or compromised
- The antagonist tightens the noose
- Each chapter should feel like the walls closing in
- A moment where the protagonist considers giving up or running
- The key piece of information that will unlock the climax is found — but its significance isn't understood yet
- Tension: 7-9, relentless

**Act 3 (Chapters 75-100%): The Reckoning**
- The protagonist assembles the truth
- Confrontation (physical, psychological, or both)
- The final twist (if applicable) — reframes everything
- The climax: maximum danger, maximum stakes
- Resolution: justice served, truth exposed, or an ambiguous ending that lingers
- Tension: 9-10, then release

**Tension Tracker**:

|---------|---------------|-----------|----------------|

| ... | ... | ... | ... |

**Present the full outline and ask for feedback.**

## Step 12: Epilogue/Final Scene

Generate 3 options:
- **Clean Resolution**: Justice, truth, safety — the threat is neutralized
- **Ambiguous Ending**: The threat is handled but something lingers — a final image or line that suggests it's not fully over
- **Twist Ending**: A final-page revelation that recontextualizes the entire story

Use AskUserQuestion to pick.
