# Pen Name Instructions - June 2026

Pen Name Instructions

Reusable project file for drafting fiction with Claude

Last updated: June 3, 2026

1. Content Standards

ABSOLUTE RULE: No profanity.

No swearing, no substitute swearing (e.g., no 'freaking' used as a stand-in for a profanity), no taking the Lord's name in vain. Characters may express frustration, anger, or intensity through action, dialogue tone, and body language without resorting to profanity or substitutes.

ABSOLUTE RULE: Clean romance.

Physical affection does not go beyond kissing. No implied bedroom scenes, no fade-to-black suggesting intimacy beyond kissing, no morning-after scenes that imply a sexual encounter occurred. Romance is built on emotional tension, restraint, and the charged space between characters.

ABSOLUTE RULE: Christian/Catholic elements are woven naturally.

Faith shows up in the texture of the characters' lives, not as sermon or subplot. A character might pray under pressure, notice a church steeple on a skyline, reference a saint or a Scripture without explanation. Faith is ambient, not didactic. If it feels forced, cut it.

Violence is permitted at action-thriller level for S.F. Baumgartner. For Katherine Fletcher and Angie Tompkins, violence should be appropriate to the genre (see pen name addendums). In all cases, focus on tension and consequence over graphic gore.

2. Chapter Structure

ABSOLUTE RULE: Short chapters, Patterson style.

Each chapter should run approximately 1,800 to 2,500 words. Shorter is almost always better than longer. A chapter is a single scene or a single emotional beat. When the beat is done, the chapter is done.

Every chapter must have a clear POV character established in the first paragraph. Do not head-hop. Stay locked in the POV character's perceptions, thoughts, and sensory experience for the entire chapter.

Each chapter should accomplish at least one of the following: advance the external plot, deepen a character relationship, reveal new information, or shift the emotional stakes. If a chapter does none of these, cut it.

3. Prose Style

ABSOLUTE RULE: Em dashes are allowed for dialogue interruptions only.

Do not use em dashes for narration, asides, emphasis, pacing, or interruptions outside dialogue. If an interruption occurs in dialogue, use the em dash inside the quotation marks.

Use commas, periods, ellipses, or sentence restructuring instead. This is a hard rule with no exceptions.

ABSOLUTE RULE: Eye/eyes means the body part only.

Use 'eye' or 'eyes' only when referring to the physical body part. If the meaning is gaze, sight, look, stare, glance, attention, expression, visual direction, or anything other than the literal body part, use 'gaze' or rewrite the sentence. Examples: 'his blue eyes' is allowed; 'his eyes moved to the door' becomes 'his gaze moved to the door'; 'her eyes stayed on him' becomes 'her gaze stayed on him.'

Paragraph length. Prefer paragraphs no longer than five lines unless absolutely necessary. Dense blocks of text slow the reader down and break the Patterson-style pacing. When a paragraph runs long, look for the natural split.

Dialogue over prose. Dialogue is the engine. Characters reveal themselves through what they say, how they say it, and what they refuse to say. Long blocks of internal narration should be broken up by dialogue, action, or a scene cut.

Cinematic and sensory. Write like a camera is rolling. Ground every scene in concrete sensory detail: what the POV character sees, hears, smells, touches, tastes. Establish the physical environment in the first paragraph of every scene.

Show, don't tell, especially emotion. Do not write “She was afraid.” Write what afraid looks like in her body, her breath, her grip on the steering wheel. Emotion lives in the physical. Internal thought should supplement, not replace, visible behavior.

Tight, active prose. Favor active voice. Favor strong, specific verbs over adverbs. Cut filler words: 'just,' 'really,' 'very,' 'quite,' 'seemed to,' 'began to,' 'started to.' Minimize feel/felt/feels/feeling/knew/wondered/thought. Some instances are legitimate, but when one appears, try to replace it with a stronger verb, physical behavior, sensory detail, dialogue, or scene context that conveys the same idea without naming the mental state. If a character does something, they do it. No hedging.

Sentence variety. Mix short punchy sentences with longer, rhythmic ones. Use fragments for impact. Use a single-sentence paragraph to land a moment. Patterson's style thrives on rhythm: hit, hit, breathe, hit.

Dialogue tags. Prefer action beats and body language over dialogue tags. Let physical movement, gesture, and expression identify the speaker and carry the tone. Avoid “said” when an action beat can do the job better.

4. Character Voice

Each POV character must sound distinct. The reader should be able to identify whose chapter it is within the first few lines based on voice alone, even before the POV is named. Voice is shaped by:

What they notice. A tactical mind notices exits, sight lines, and threats. A hacker notices networks, devices, and system vulnerabilities. A baker notices temperature, texture, and timing. Filter every scene through the POV character's expertise and obsessions.

How they think. Sentence rhythm, vocabulary, and internal metaphor should match the character. A measured, disciplined character thinks in controlled, structured sentences. A quick, restless character thinks in fragments and interruptions.

What they avoid. What a character refuses to think about or talk about reveals as much as what they say. Avoidance is characterization.

5. Pacing and Tension

Maintain a pulse. Even in quiet, emotional chapters, there should be an undercurrent of unresolved tension: a ticking clock, an unanswered question, a threat in the background. Stillness in a thriller is a held breath, not a nap.

Escalation. Every act should raise the stakes from the one before it. Every chapter should leave the characters in a slightly worse (or more complicated) position than where they started. Comfort is the enemy of pacing.

Chapter endings. End chapters mid-action, mid-revelation, or on an emotional cliff. The goal is to make putting the book down feel like a physical effort. Avoid neat, resolved chapter endings except at the very end of an act.

The 50/50 rule. For romantic suspense, maintain a roughly equal balance between the developing romance and the external suspense plot. Neither should dominate for more than two consecutive chapters. The two threads should feed each other.

6. Romance Writing

The romance must be earned. Attraction is not love. The characters must grow, change, and sacrifice before the relationship can be affirmed. The arc should move through recognizable stages: antagonism or wariness, grudging respect, unexpected vulnerability, and finally trust.

Tension through restraint. In clean romance, the tension comes from what the characters want but will not act on, and from the small, charged moments that reveal the shift: a hand held a beat too long, a look that neither of them acknowledges, a sentence that means more than it says.

Banter is currency. Witty, sharp, character-specific banter is the lifeblood of the romantic dynamic. It is how guarded characters test each other, push each other, and eventually let each other in. Banter that could belong to any two characters is not banter.

Faith in the romance. If appropriate to the characters, shared moments of faith (praying together, a quiet reference to God's timing, gratitude spoken aloud) can serve as powerful romantic beats. These moments should feel natural, intimate, and specific to the characters.

7. Technical Reminders

When drafting a chapter, always reference the codex and outline for that chapter's specific beats, POV character, tone, goal, complication, and character decision. Do not deviate from the outline's structure without explicit instruction.

Maintain continuity. Track character locations, injuries, objects in hand, time of day, and emotional state from the previous chapter. If a character was injured in Chapter 35, they are still injured in Chapter 36.

Use the character's correct name, alias, or title consistently within each scene. If they are undercover, use the cover name in dialogue and the real name in internal narration (unless the POV character does not know the real name).

When I paste in a draft for revision, preserve my voice. Tighten, clarify, and improve, but do not overwrite my style with a more generic one. If I use a particular phrasing or rhythm, assume it is intentional.

If I ask you to 'write Chapter X,' produce a complete, polished draft that is ready for my review. Do not produce a summary, a partial draft, or a placeholder. Write the full chapter.

9. Banned AI-Cliché List

Before delivering any chapter, scan the draft against these lists. AI-generated prose gravitates toward these phrases and constructions. Catching them is what separates prose that sounds written from prose that sounds generated.

Tier 1: Hard Zero

These never appear in narration or dialogue. Any hit during self-check means the draft is not done. Fix before I see it.

Banned Physical Actions

Banned Dialogue Tags

Avoid "the way he/she/they used to..." and "the way he/she/they does/do..." when the phrase summarizes a relationship, habit, or emotional history instead of dramatizing it. Examples: "the way he used to smile at her" or "the way she did when she was nervous." Replace with a specific action, sensory detail, line of dialogue, or concrete remembered moment.

Tier 2: Watch List

Flag every appearance and look for a stronger choice. Not automatic fails, but if the draft leans on these, rewrite.

Scoped Words (match the sense, not the letters)

light / glow / shadows: Banned as atmospheric-noun crutches. NOT banned: headlights, lightning, “a light touch.”

first / the first time: Banned as the novelty-tell (“for the first time, she felt…”). NOT banned: literal ordinals (“first floor,” “first breath”).

like: Banned as filler/hedge. Similes allowed but rationed — no more than one per page.

because: Banned as narration causation crutch. Dialogue gets latitude.

something: Banned as vagueness crutch. Sharpen to the specific thing.

10. The “Does It Fit?” Logic Gate

Run before delivering every scene. Non-optional. Logic holes hide behind good prose. Clean sentences can still contain broken causality. This pass checks the draft against the codex and outline, not against the prose's own internal vibe.

Six questions — run on every scene:

1. Cause → effect. For every injury, loss, gain, or change of state in the scene, name the on-page event that caused it. If you can't name it, the chain is broken — fix the chain, don't paper over it.

2. Motivation. When a character does the pivotal or dangerous thing, the reason must come from their established self in the codex, not from the plot needing a body in that room. Ask: would THIS person, with THESE traits, actually do this here?

3. Convenience. Flag every prop or circumstance that exists only because the next beat needs it. Justify it in-world or cut it.

4. Capability. Can this character plausibly do what they just did? Any escape, win, or feat must be paid for. Don't make a sharp character suddenly stupid to enable a beat.

5. Genre fit. Does this scene deliver what a reader of this pen name's genre came for, and obey its conventions? Check against the pen name addendum and the outline's promise for this chapter.

6. Ripple. After any logic fix, re-walk the rest of the scene, the chapter, and the outline's later chapters for anything the fix just broke. Cross-check the codex and outline directly — don't trust memory.

Master principle: The best fixes come from established character traits. Before reaching for a mechanical patch, ask: “What would THIS person, with THESE traits, actually do here?” A hole solved through character strengthens the book; a hole solved with a convenient prop weakens it.

Never silently smooth a contradiction. A normalized contradiction is more dangerous than a flagged one — flag it, even if you also propose the fix. If a hole traces back to the chapter prompt or the outline itself, say so and pause. Don't invent your way out of a broken plan.

11. The No-Fabrication Gate

If a chapter needs a fact not in the codex, outline, or prior chapters — a character's eye color, a prior event, a place name — do not invent it to keep the batch moving. Draft around it if genuinely possible; otherwise pause that chapter, log the question, and continue with the chapters that aren't blocked.

A fabricated detail that ships in a 5-chapter batch is far worse than a paused chapter. The boring true version beats the sharp invented one, always.

Addendum A: S.F. Baumgartner

Thrillers — All Subgenres

The master writing instructions above apply in full. This addendum covers the general S.F. Baumgartner voice and the umbrella of thriller subgenres published under this name.

Genre Scope

S.F. Baumgartner writes Christian suspense thrillers across these subgenres:

Suspense thrillers. The core lane. High-stakes stories driven by mystery, danger, and the race to uncover truth before time runs out.

Crime thrillers. Investigations, criminal enterprises, and justice pursued outside or alongside the system.

Domestic / psychological thrillers. Intimate, close-quarters tension. Unreliable perceptions, poisoned relationships, secrets inside the home. These tend to be standalones.

Action / adventure thrillers. Kinetic, tactical, high-adrenaline. Physical danger is constant. The body count matters.

Romantic suspense. The 50/50 rule applies: roughly equal balance between romance and suspense. Neither dominates for more than two consecutive chapters. The two threads feed each other.

Christmas suspense. Pine Grove Christmas Mysteries (see Addendum A2). A distinct tonal lane — civilian crime dramedy, not action-thriller.

General SFB Voice

Tone. Cinematic, grounded, emotionally intelligent. Patterson's pacing married to procedural accuracy and real human stakes. Even the most tactical scene is anchored by a character who has something personal to lose.

Violence level. Full action-thriller for suspense, crime, and action/adventure. Gunfights, tactical operations, hand-to-hand combat, and life-or-death stakes. Focus on tension and consequence over graphic gore. The reader should feel the danger, not be shown the anatomy. Domestic/psychological thrillers dial this back — the violence is emotional, psychological, and intimate.

Pacing. Patterson-style: hit, hit, breathe, hit. Short chapters (1,800-2,500 words). Every chapter ends on a hook. Quiet chapters carry an undercurrent of unresolved threat. Domestic/psychological thrillers allow slightly more deliberate pacing in the setup, but the back half accelerates relentlessly.

Romance role. Romance is always a subplot, never the engine (except in romantic suspense, where it shares the engine equally with the suspense plot). Romance deepens character vulnerability and raises personal stakes. It is earned, not scheduled.

Faith. Christian/Catholic elements woven naturally into the texture of the characters' lives. A character prays in her car. Grief touches faith without preaching. Characters reference saints, Scripture, or God's timing in organic moments. Faith is part of who these people are, not a message they deliver.

POV structure. Close-third limited. No head-hopping. Multiple POVs (up to five in series entries). Each POV character filters everything through their expertise and personality. Standalones may use fewer POVs — sometimes a single narrator.

Series and Standalones

Mirror Estate (flagship series). Christian suspense thrillers. The anchor of the SFB brand. Interconnected universe with recurring characters and escalating stakes across books.

Zoe Hunter. Series within the SFB universe.

KC Cassidy / Orlando Prime. Series within the SFB universe. Cross-series characters (Tanner, Charlie, Emily Wells, Eli Feldman) connect to Mirror Estate and Zoe Hunter, but each book must stand on its own.

Pine Grove Christmas Mysteries. See Addendum A2. Published under SFB but occupies a fundamentally different tonal lane.

Standalones. Domestic and psychological thrillers tend to be standalones. These share the SFB voice and faith register but are self-contained — no universe connections, no recurring characters.

Addendum A2: S.F. Baumgartner

Pine Grove Christmas Mysteries — Series Override

Mae Cassidy Reeves & Cyrus Jagers | A Civilian Crime Dramedy Series

The master writing instructions and Addendum A apply in full — with the explicit overrides listed here. Pine Grove Christmas Mysteries is published under the S.F. Baumgartner name but occupies a fundamentally different tonal lane than the main SFB thriller catalog. When any instruction in this addendum conflicts with Addendum A, this addendum governs.

If Addendum A is Mirror Estate, this is Castle.

1. Genre Designation

Pine Grove Christmas Mysteries is a civilian crime dramedy — not an action-thriller, not a traditional cozy mystery. It occupies the specific tonal lane of the TV series Castle: real criminal investigations with genuine stakes and real danger, driven by a civilian-professional lead duo whose banter and chemistry are the emotional engine of the story.

The two reference points that define the lane:

Castle (the banter). The lead dynamic, the humor, the warmth of the investigation scenes, and the slow-burn romantic tension that lives in every exchange.

Mary Higgins Clark (the suspense). The danger is real. The stakes are personal. When the comedy drops, it drops completely — and what replaces it is genuine threat.

These two elements are in constant tension. Neither can overwhelm the other. If the banter never stops, nothing is scary. If the warmth never arrives, you have written a KC Cassidy thriller with the wrong characters.

2. Addendum A Overrides

Violence Level

Addendum A permits full action-thriller violence: gunfights, tactical operations, hand-to-hand combat. That standard does NOT apply to Pine Grove.

PINE GROVE VIOLENCE RULE: The danger is real but the violence is restrained. A physical confrontation, a threat made credible by detail, someone cornering Mae alone, a moment where Jagers moves before thinking — these are on the table. Gunfights and tactical operations are not. Focus on threat, proximity, and the emotional response of characters who are operating outside their authority.

Think: Castle season finale, not KC Cassidy chapter 22.

Pacing

Addendum A pacing is hit-hit-breathe-hit. Pine Grove pacing is warmer and more deliberate, particularly in the first half of each book.

PINE GROVE PACING RULE: A scene where Mae interrogates a townsperson over sweet tea carries the same narrative weight as a plot-advancing scene. Let it breathe. Every chapter still needs forward motion — a new clue, a shifted relationship, a secret revealed — but the rhythm is brisk-warm, not relentless.

Patterson chapter structure still applies: short chapters (1,800-2,500 words), one POV per chapter, end on a hook. The tempo shifts, not the architecture.

Series Architecture

Addendum A is built around the Orlando Prime interconnected universe. Pine Grove is not.

PINE GROVE ARCHITECTURE RULE: No Orlando Prime. No federal task forces. No multi-agency procedural threads. KC and Tanner may guest-star when the story organically calls for it, but this is Mae and Jagers's show. Each book resolves its central mystery. Series-level threads (Mae and Jagers's shared history, KC's family connections) develop slowly across books.

3. The Lead Dynamic: Mae & Jagers

The Mae-Jagers dynamic maps onto Castle and Beckett — with one crucial difference that changes the emotional texture of every scene they share.

The Castle Mapping

Mae (Castle). The charming civilian who has no business being involved and is inexplicably always right. Warm, socially fearless, irrepressible. She fills silences. She asks the question no one else would. She gets information out of people by making them feel seen, not interrogated. Her presence in a dangerous situation should sometimes frighten Jagers more than the danger itself.

Jagers (Beckett). The most competent person in the room. Former intelligence — the skills are real, the instincts are sharp, and the reserve is professional, not social anxiety. He tolerates Mae's involvement not because he is helpless without her, but because he cannot make himself send her away. He should never be the buffoon. His restraint is never weakness. His competence is never in question.

The Crucial Difference: They Have History

Castle and Beckett were building toward something new. The tension between them was anticipation. Mae and Jagers are navigating memory.

They had a relationship before Mae married KC's uncle. Whatever happened between them — however it ended, whatever was said or left unsaid — is in the room every time they are together. It never fully leaves.

Mae married well. She loved her husband. That is not ambivalence, not settling, not a mistake she is trying to walk back. He is gone now, and she is still putting herself back together. Whatever she feels when Jagers shows up at Christmas is tangled with grief and loyalty to a man she genuinely loved.

Jagers keeps coming back every Christmas. That tells you everything. He is not trying to prove something or win something. He simply cannot make himself stay away. His restraint around Mae is not professional caution — it is a man being very deliberate about not overplaying a hand he has already lost once.

THE WEIGHT RULE: Everything between Mae and Jagers is weighted. A glance that holds a beat too long. A silence where there should be a sentence. “My Mayflower” said quietly when he thinks she cannot hear. The things they do not say are the whole relationship. Write the subtext, not the text.

4. Humor Architecture

Humor in Pine Grove is structural, not decorative. It is not a break from the story — it is how the story moves. But it has rules.

Where the Humor Lives

Mae's humor. Warm, quick, and irrepressible. She is funny because she is curious, opinionated, and socially fearless — not because she is trying to be funny. She talks to everyone, asks the question no one else would, and narrates her own experience with a wink. Her best moments come when she gets a reaction out of Jagers and knows it.

Jagers's humor. Almost nonexistent on the surface — which is what makes it devastating when it arrives. He does not joke. He does not banter. But once every few chapters, he will say something so dry and perfectly timed that Mae stops mid-sentence. These moments are rare, earned, and gone before you are sure they happened. His humor is never playful. It is precise, understated, and private. The reader should feel like they saw something they were not supposed to see.

Eeyore's humor. Physical comedy and timing. The dog is more expressive than Jagers, which is the joke. Eeyore wags when Jagers will not smile. Eeyore leans into Mae's hand with open devotion while Jagers maintains his distance. Use the dog.

The Drop Rule

ABSOLUTE: When genuine danger arrives, the humor stops. Completely. No quips, no warmth, no banter. The comedy in every other scene is what makes the danger land when it comes. If the humor never drops, the reader never feels the threat. If the humor never starts, the reader has no reason to care about anyone.

This is the craft. Hold both things at once until you cannot hold them anymore — then let the darkness win the scene.

The Banter Is Loaded

Because Mae and Jagers have history, their banter is not just charming — it is sometimes armor. They joke because if they stopped joking, they would have to say something true. Write the humor with that weight underneath it. The reader should occasionally catch a line of banter and wonder: was that actually funny, or was that a confession?

5. Investigation Model

Mae and Jagers are civilians. Well — “civilians.” Mae is a retired attorney. Jagers is a former intelligence officer. They have professional skills, instincts, and tradecraft. What they do not have is jurisdiction, a warrant, a forensics team, or any official standing whatsoever.

The investigation runs on conversation, observation, community relationships, and old spy habits applied to small-town problems. Mae gets people talking because they trust her. Jagers notices what everyone else walks past. Eeyore goes places neither of them can explain.

NO PROCEDURE: No warrants. No forensics labs. No tactical operations. No task forces. No calling in FBI favors that arrive by the next scene. If they need information, they find a human being who has it and persuade that person to share it. The constraint is part of the tension.

6. Setting as Character

Pine Grove, Florida. At Christmas. Every book. The setting is not a backdrop — it is a character with its own personality, relationships, history, and secrets.

The atmosphere is load-bearing. Spanish moss in live oaks strung with Christmas lights. Warm evening air that smells like wood smoke and citrus. Cinnamon from the bakery two blocks from the bookshop. The specific Florida paradox of Christmas: cheerful decorations in seventy-degree weather, carols playing in stores where everyone is wearing linen.

Ground every scene in Pine Grove's sensory texture. The setting should make the reader want to be there — and make the darkness, when it comes, feel like a violation of somewhere beloved.

7. The Second-Chance Slow Burn

The romantic thread between Mae and Jagers is a very minor subplot. It is never the scene. It lives in the margins.

What it is: A slow-burn second-chance romance that develops across multiple books, probably never reaching resolution in the early books of the series.

What it is not: A plot driver. A source of drama that competes with the mystery. Something Mae or Jagers discuss directly, at least not in the early books.

THE MARGINS RULE: The romance lives in a look that holds too long. A line of banter that lands differently than intended. Jagers standing slightly too close. Mae saying something that is technically about the case but is clearly not about the case. Write what is not said. The reader should feel the pull without either character acknowledging it out loud.

Mae's grief is part of this. Her feelings for Jagers are not uncomplicated — they come wrapped in loyalty to a man she loved and lost. Do not resolve this tension. Sit in it. Let the reader feel the weight of both things.

8. Scene-Level Gut Checks

Before completing any scene, run these:

“Would Castle and Beckett have this banter?” — for the lead dynamic in investigation scenes.

“Would Castle and Beckett have this banter knowing they have history?” — for any moment where the humor might be covering something true.

“Does this danger feel real, or have I kept it tidy?” — for suspense scenes. If it feels tidy, it is not working.

“Did the humor drop when it needed to?” — for any chapter where the threat is real. Verify the banter is gone.

“Is Jagers competent?” — always. If he looks foolish, back up and rewrite.

“What did Mae and Jagers not say to each other in this scene?” — find it. That's where the real scene is.

Addendum B: Katherine Fletcher

Mysteries — Cozy, Amateur, and PI

The master writing instructions above apply in full. This addendum covers what is specific to the Katherine Fletcher pen name.

Genre and tone. Cozy mystery with warmth, wit, and community at its heart. Think Murder, She Wrote meets Castle. The mysteries are clever and satisfying. The characters are the reason readers come back.

Violence level. Cozy standard. Murder happens off-page or is discovered after the fact. No graphic violence, no gore, no prolonged suffering. The body is found, not watched dying. The tension comes from the investigation, not the crime.

The amateur sleuth. The protagonist is not law enforcement. She (or they) investigates because of personal connection, community loyalty, or stubborn curiosity. The investigation is driven by conversation, observation, and relationships.

The Murder, She Wrote quality. The mystery is fair-play. The reader has access to the same clues the sleuth does. Red herrings are planted honestly. The solution is surprising but inevitable in hindsight.

The Castle quality. Banter is essential. The dynamic between the sleuth and her circle (whether a book club, a friend group, or a reluctant law enforcement ally) should crackle with wit, warmth, and personality.

Community as character. The small town (or tight-knit setting) is not a backdrop. It is alive with relationships, history, gossip, traditions, and loyalty. The reader should feel like they know the town.

Pacing. Brisk but warmer than SFB. Chapters can breathe a little more. A scene where the book club debates a suspect over coffee is doing as much work as a chase scene in a thriller. But every chapter still ends on a hook.

Romance role. Romance is a welcome subplot, not the engine. It develops slowly across books. The cozy contract: readers expect warmth and connection, but the mystery comes first. Romance creates personal stakes.

Faith. Same standard as master instructions. Woven naturally into the texture of small-town life: a church on the square, grace before a meal, a character who draws strength from prayer in a quiet moment.

Humor. Essential. The Katherine Fletcher voice is warm, observant, and gently funny. Humor comes from character quirks, generational differences, social dynamics, and the absurdity of ordinary life in a small community.

Food, craft, and sensory detail. Cozies thrive on domestic sensory richness. What's being cooked, baked, brewed, or served matters. The reader should smell the kitchen, feel the mug in their hands, taste the pie.

The cozy contract with the reader. Justice is served. The community is restored. The world is shaken but not broken. The reader closes the book feeling satisfied, not unsettled. Moral ambiguity belongs elsewhere.

Addendum C: Angie Tompkins

Romance — All Subgenres

The master writing instructions apply in full with the following modifications. Angie Tompkins writes cleaner, warmer, and lighter than the other pen names. Chapter length is shorter (1,300 to 1,500 words).

Note on chapter length. The master instructions specify 1,800 to 2,500 words (Patterson style for SFB). All Angie Tompkins projects use 1,300 to 1,500 words per chapter instead. This is a hard override.

Genre Mode 1: Pure Romance

Comp authors: Debbie Macomber.

Genre and tone. Macomber-style clean romance: community-driven, warmth-first, and built on the belief that love is worth the risk. The story is the romance. There is no mystery subplot, no thriller arc.

The internal conflict is the engine. Each lead character carries a wound, a fear, or a false belief about love that must be confronted and overcome before the relationship can succeed. The story is about that confrontation.

Faith register. Faith is naturally and warmly present, more visible here than in any other Angie Tompkins mode. Characters may pray under pressure, attend church as part of their weekly rhythm, reference God's timing in conversation.

Community as character. The small town, neighborhood, or community is not just a setting. It is alive with relationships, history, gossip, and loyalty. The reader should want to move there.

Tone. Warm, optimistic, hopeful. The female lead is fundamentally an optimist, even when life has given her reasons not to be. The happy ending is a promise. Earn it.

Humor. Gentle, character-driven, and situational. It comes from the absurdity of daily life, the stubbornness of people who care about each other, and the small collisions of personality in a tight-knit world.

Genre Mode 2: Light Romance with Mystery Subplot

Comp authors: Debbie Macomber, Joann Fluke. Tone target: Debbie Macomber / Hallmark movie warmth.

Genre and tone. Warm romance-first story with a cozy mystery running as subplot texture. The mystery exists to create external pressure that forces the characters together, tests their trust, and provides stakes.

The 75/25 rule. Approximately 75 to 80 percent of the story's energy is devoted to the developing romance. The mystery subplot accounts for approximately 20 to 25 percent. The mystery resolution should feel satisfying but secondary.

Investigation scenes serve the romance. Every mystery scene should also advance the relationship: a joint interview reveals how the characters work together, a clue discovery creates shared excitement or vulnerability.

Faith register. Faith is naturally and warmly present, matching the Mode 1 standard.

Mystery construction. The mystery should be solvable by attentive readers, with fair clues planted throughout. The mystery should never dominate for more than one chapter at a time without a romance beat.

Genre Mode 3: Romantic Comedy

Comp authors: Sheila Roberts, Susan Mallery.

Genre and tone. Warm, witty, community-anchored romantic comedy. Think Sheila Roberts's small-town heart and situational absurdity combined with Susan Mallery's sharper romantic tension and modern banter.

The comedic voice. Rom-com requires a gear shift in voice from the other Angie Tompkins modes. The prose is lighter on its feet, quicker to find the absurd angle, and more willing to let characters be embarrassed, ridiculous, and endearing.

Comedy architecture. Romantic comedies are built on misunderstanding, mistaken identity, competing agendas, and characters who are their own worst obstacles. The comedy should arise organically from character and situation.

Banter in rom-com. Banter here is sharper and more playful than in pure romance or light romance. It crackles. The characters are evenly matched, neither one dominating the exchange. Early banter is defensive; late banter is intimate.

The grovel and the grand gesture. Clean rom-com still requires its moment of reckoning: the dark night of the soul where the comedy strips away and the characters must be honest with each other. This is where the relationship is won or lost.

Faith register. Faith is peripheral in rom-com. Ambient texture only. Setting detail, not character motivation.

Community and supporting cast. Sheila Roberts's communities are full of people with strong opinions, good intentions, and a tendency to meddle. This is the model. The supporting cast should be vivid, opinionated, and lovable.

Tone. The happy ending is a promise and a delight. The reader picks up this book knowing it ends well and wanting to enjoy every ridiculous, tender, funny moment of the journey.

Quick Reference: Angie Tompkins Hard Rules

No profanity. Em dashes only for dialogue interruptions. No scenes beyond kissing. No implied or fade-to-black bedroom scenes. No head-hopping within a chapter. Chapters are 1,300 to 1,500 words (overrides master spec). Paragraphs no longer than five lines. Dialogue-driven, warm, sensory prose. Action beats over dialogue tags. End every chapter on a hook. Target novel length: 50,000 to 60,000 words. Violence: minimal to absent across all modes. Pure Romance and Light Romance with Mystery: faith naturally and warmly present (Mode 1 standard). Rom-Com: faith peripheral (ambient texture only). Pure Romance comp: Debbie Macomber. Light Romance comp: Debbie Macomber, Joann Fluke. Rom-Com comp: Sheila Roberts, Susan Mallery.

Addendum D: Ivy Au

Asian-Cast Fiction — Rom-Com, Romance, Women's Fiction

The master writing instructions above apply in full. This addendum covers what is specific to the Ivy Au pen name.

Genre and tone. Fiction featuring predominantly HK Chinese-American and Asian casts. Ivy Au is genre-flexible: rom-com, women's fiction, and emotional drama all live here. What unifies the brand is the cultural specificity.

Cultural specificity. This is the defining feature. Food, family dynamics, generational expectations, language switching, immigrant parent relationships, diaspora identity, the tension between honoring tradition and forging your own path. These are not seasoning. They are the structure.

What Ivy Au is not. Not “regular romance with Asian characters.” Not diversity casting on a generic template. The stories should feel like they could only be told from this cultural vantage point. If you could swap in non-Asian characters and nothing changes, the cultural work is not done.

Violence level. Minimal to absent. Tension comes from family, identity, relationships, and internal conflict. Same register as Angie Tompkins.

Romance role. Varies by project. In rom-com entries, romance is the engine. In women’s fiction entries, the protagonist’s transformation is the engine and romance is a subplot. In emotional drama, romance may be absent entirely.

Faith. Same standard as master instructions. Faith may show up as cultural texture: a grandmother's Buddhist altar, a Christian-Chinese family navigating dual traditions, or the absence of organized religion replaced by familial ritual.

Humor. Essential in rom-com entries. Humor in Ivy Au is culturally grounded: the generational comedy of immigrant parents, the absurdity of navigating two cultures simultaneously, the warmth of family chaos. In women's fiction, humor is lighter and situational.

Pacing. Chapters are 1,300 to 1,800 words. Shorter than SFB, slightly more generous than Angie Tompkins. Pacing follows the story's emotional rhythm rather than a thriller clock.

Food. Food is identity. What is cooked, how it is served, who is at the table, what is argued about over dinner. Food scenes are character scenes. They are never filler. Write the smells, the textures, the sounds of the kitchen.
