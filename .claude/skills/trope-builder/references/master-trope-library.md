# Master Trope Library

Reference file for the Trope Builder skill. Organized by genre, then by category. Each trope includes a 1-2 sentence execution note covering what makes it work and what makes it fail.

---

## ROMANCE TROPES

### Relationship Dynamics

**Enemies to Lovers**
The opposition must be genuine and rooted in something real — conflicting values, competing goals, genuine harm done. Works when the reader can see both sides and the transformation from enemy to lover is earned through changed understanding, not just proximity. Fails when the "enemies" are just mildly annoyed by each other or when the reason for enmity is a misunderstanding that could be solved with one conversation.

**Friends to Lovers**
The tension here is the fear of ruining what they already have. Works when the friendship feels real and valuable — readers need to understand the stakes of confessing. Fails when the friendship is told, not shown, or when there's no clear moment of shift from platonic to romantic awareness.

**Strangers to Lovers**
The blank-slate dynamic where attraction and connection are built from scratch. Works when the initial meeting is memorable and the chemistry is immediate but the relationship has genuine obstacles. Fails when the characters have no reason to keep interacting after the first meeting, or when the pacing is too fast to feel earned.

**Second Chance Romance**
Past relationship, new opportunity. Works when the original breakup was painful, understandable, and caused by something that has genuinely changed. Fails when the original reason for breaking up was trivial (making readers wonder why they split), or when neither character has grown in the interim.

**Forbidden Love**
Something external makes the relationship off-limits — family loyalty, social rules, professional ethics, laws. Works when the prohibition is real and has consequences, so every stolen moment carries weight. Fails when the "forbidden" element is easily overcome or when the characters face no real consequences for pursuing each other.

**Marriage of Convenience**
Two people marry for practical reasons and fall in love. Works when both characters have compelling, non-romantic reasons to enter the marriage AND when the domestic intimacy of shared life creates genuine vulnerability. Fails when the "convenience" is flimsy, when either character could easily solve their problem another way, or when the marriage stays emotionally distant for too long.

**Fake Dating / Fake Relationship**
Pretending to be together for an external audience, then catching real feelings. Works when the stakes of the performance are high (family pressure, professional necessity, public image) and when the line between performance and reality blurs gradually. Fails when there's no convincing reason to fake it, when the fake-out is transparent to everyone, or when the "real feelings" confession comes without sufficient build.

**Arranged Marriage**
Marriage orchestrated by others — family, duty, political alliance. Works when both characters feel trapped and find unexpected connection within the constraint. Differs from marriage of convenience in that neither party chose this. Fails when one character is enthusiastic from the start (removes the tension) or when the arranger's power is never challenged.

**Love Triangle**
Handle with extreme care. Reader opinion is deeply divided on love triangles. Works when both potential love interests are genuinely viable and the choice reflects something meaningful about the protagonist's identity and values. Fails when one option is obviously wrong (making the protagonist look stupid for deliberating), when the protagonist strings both parties along cruelly, or when the resolution feels arbitrary. If using: commit to making BOTH options compelling.

**Slow Burn**
The relationship builds gradually over a long stretch of the book. Works when the tension escalates consistently — every interaction adds a new layer of awareness, longing, or complication. The delayed payoff must be worth the wait. Fails when slow burn becomes "nothing happens" — there must be forward movement in every scene, even if consummation is delayed.

**Instalove**
Immediate, powerful attraction that feels fated or overwhelming. Reader-divisive: some love it, many are skeptical. Works in paranormal (fated mates justifies it), in short fiction (compressed timeline needs it), and when paired with significant external obstacles that test the instant connection. Fails in contemporary realistic fiction when characters declare love with no foundation, or when the instant attraction substitutes for actual relationship development.

### Proximity Tropes

**Forced Proximity**
Characters are compelled to be in close physical space. Works because proximity creates unavoidable interaction, which creates friction, which creates intimacy. The key is that the proximity must feel genuinely forced — if either character could easily leave, the tension collapses. Fails when the "forced" element is weak or when the characters don't use the proximity to learn uncomfortable truths about each other.

**One Bed**
A subset of forced proximity with specific physical intimacy implications. Works because it's a pressure cooker for physical awareness — every shift, every breath matters. Best when it's genuinely the only option and when the characters' attempts to maintain boundaries create both humor and heat. Fails when it's contrived (a hotel with no other rooms in a major city) or when the bed-sharing has no emotional consequences.

**Stuck Together**
Broader than forced proximity — characters are trapped by circumstance (snowstorm, elevator, island, etc.). Works when the being-stuck forces conversations and revelations that wouldn't happen otherwise. The confinement becomes a crucible. Fails when the "stuck" period is too short to matter or when the characters just wait it out without meaningful interaction.

**Roommates**
Shared living space creating daily domestic intimacy. Works because domestic routines reveal character — how someone loads a dishwasher says more than a date. Creates a slow-drip intimacy that's hard to resist. Fails when the roommate situation is too easily escaped or when the domestic details are skipped in favor of standard romance beats.

**Neighbors**
Proximity without cohabitation — close enough to observe, not close enough to ignore. Works when the proximity creates accidental intimacy (thin walls, shared spaces, seeing each other in vulnerable moments). Fails when the neighbor dynamic is just "they live near each other" without the proximity creating specific scenes and complications.

**Coworkers**
Professional proximity with personal complications. Works when the professional stakes are real — both characters care about their jobs and the relationship threatens that. The workplace creates forced daily interaction and performance pressure. Fails when the workplace is just a backdrop and could be swapped for any setting without changing the story.

**Boss/Employee**
Power dynamic trope — see Power Dynamics section for full treatment. As a proximity trope, it works because the professional hierarchy creates forced deference that contrasts with private equality or attraction. Fails when the power imbalance is ignored or handwaved rather than addressed as a genuine complication.

**Stranded**
Cut off from normal life and support systems. Works when the stranding strips away social masks and forces characters to rely on each other in ways that reveal their true selves. Creates urgency and survival stakes that accelerate emotional bonding. Fails when the stranding feels like a vacation rather than a genuine challenge, or when rescue comes before the relationship has been properly tested.

**Road Trip**
Journey as relationship metaphor. Works because the physical movement mirrors emotional progress, every stop is a potential turning point, and the enclosed space of a vehicle forces conversation. Fails when the road trip is just a series of cute stops without escalating tension, or when the journey has no meaningful destination driving urgency.

**Small Town (Everyone Knows Everyone)**
A proximity trope at community scale. Works because there's no anonymity — every interaction is witnessed, every history is known, and the community itself becomes a character with opinions. Creates organic complications and a built-in support/interference system. Fails when the small town is just a cute backdrop without the gossip, pressure, and claustrophobia that real small-town life involves.

### Identity and Secret Tropes

**Secret Identity**
One character is not who they appear to be. Works when the secret creates a genuine moral dilemma — the character grows closer to someone while deceiving them, and the reader feels both the sweetness of connection and the dread of discovery. Fails when the secret is trivial (why hide it?), when discovery has no real consequences, or when the deception goes on so long that the deceived character looks foolish.

**Secret Royal / Secret Billionaire**
A character hiding wealth or status. Works when the disguise serves a real purpose (testing whether they're loved for themselves) and when the reveal creates genuine conflict rather than just "oh, you're rich, great." Fails when the secret wealth solves all problems too easily or when the "regular person" persona is unconvincing.

**Amnesia**
Memory loss creating a blank slate. Works when the amnesia forces characters to fall in love again without the baggage of their history, creating a parallel romance that comments on the original. Requires careful handling of consent — the amnesiac character cannot consent to a relationship they don't remember agreeing to. Fails when the amnesia is used as a cheap reset button or when memory returns too conveniently.

**Mistaken Identity**
Characters incorrectly identified as someone else. Works best in comedic or lighter tones where the escalating misunderstanding creates increasingly absurd situations. Fails when the mistake could be corrected with a simple sentence and the characters just... don't.

**Hidden Past**
A character concealing their history — criminal record, previous identity, trauma, secret child. Works when the hidden past is genuinely threatening to the present relationship and when the reveal forces both characters to reckon with who they really are. Fails when the hidden past is mild (why hide it?) or when the other character's reaction to the reveal is disproportionate.

**Undercover**
Professional deception — law enforcement, espionage, investigation. Works when the character develops genuine feelings while maintaining a false identity, creating an impossible tension between duty and heart. The professional stakes add external pressure to the personal conflict. Fails when the undercover mission is forgotten for chapters at a time or when the love interest has no agency in the resolution.

**Double Life**
A character maintaining two separate existences. Works when both lives are essential and losing either one would cost something real. The tension of maintaining the divide, and the inevitable collision, drives the plot. Fails when one life is clearly less important (just drop it) or when the double life is sustained beyond believability.

**Pen Pal / Online Romance**
Connection built through words before meeting in person. Works when the written/digital intimacy creates a genuine emotional bond that physical meeting then complicates (what if they don't match in person?). Fails when the digital phase is rushed or when the identity behind the screen is obvious to readers from page one.

### Power Dynamic Tropes

**Note on all power dynamic tropes:** These require intentional handling of consent, agency, and the imbalance itself. The power dynamic must be addressed as a real complication within the story, not ignored. Readers who love these tropes want the friction the imbalance creates. Readers who dislike them want to see the imbalance resolved. Both require the author to be AWARE of the dynamic and handle it deliberately.

**Age Gap**
Significant age difference between romantic leads. Both characters must be adults. Works when the age difference creates genuine differences in life experience, perspective, and worldview that enrich the relationship AND create friction. The gap should be a source of both attraction and conflict. Fails when the age gap is purely cosmetic (they act the same age) or when it creates a parental dynamic that's uncomfortable rather than compelling. Reader sensitivity varies by subgenre — dark romance readers accept wider gaps than contemporary romance readers.

**Boss/Employee**
Professional hierarchy as romantic obstacle. Works when both the professional and personal stakes are high, when the power dynamic is explicitly addressed (not ignored), and when the resolution doesn't require one character to sacrifice their career. Fails when the boss character uses their position to pursue the relationship (this reads as coercive, not romantic), or when the professional consequences are never a real threat.

**Teacher/Student (Adults Only)**
Intellectual authority dynamic. ALL characters must be adults. Works in university/adult education settings where the knowledge gap creates admiration that transforms into mutual respect and attraction. The taboo element adds heat. Fails when the authority figure initiates the relationship (power abuse), when the student has no agency, or when the setting makes the characters' ages ambiguous.

**Bodyguard**
Protector/protected dynamic. Works because the bodyguard's professional duty to keep someone alive creates enforced intimacy, physical closeness, and a built-in reason to be hyperaware of the other person's body and safety. Fails when the protectee is passive (the bodyguard does everything) or when the threat level is too low to justify the protection.

**Celebrity / Normal Person**
Fame gap creating different life experiences and access. Works when the celebrity's world is genuinely disorienting for the normal person AND the normal person's world offers the celebrity something real they can't get elsewhere. Fails when the "normal person" is actually gorgeous, wealthy, and well-connected (they're not actually normal) or when fame is all upside with no real cost.

**Royal / Commoner**
Status gap with institutional stakes. Works when the monarchy/aristocracy has real power and the relationship genuinely threatens political stability or family expectations. The weight of duty makes the romance feel consequential. Fails when the royal duties are vague, when the royal can just abdicate without consequence, or when the commoner is too awed to have a real personality.

**Billionaire / Regular Person**
Wealth gap as both attraction and obstacle. Works when the money creates genuine power imbalance that must be addressed, when the billionaire's wealth comes with real costs (isolation, distrust, control issues), and when the regular person maintains their identity and agency. Fails when the billionaire is just a regular nice guy who happens to be rich, when the money solves all problems, or when the regular person's financial struggles are trivialized.

### Personality Pairings

**Grumpy/Sunshine**
One character is warm, optimistic, and open; the other is closed-off, guarded, and prickly. Works when the grumpy character's exterior is a defense mechanism protecting something tender, and the sunshine character's warmth is genuine (not performative). The magic moment is when the grumpy character is soft for the sunshine character ONLY. Fails when the grumpy character is genuinely mean (not endearing) or when the sunshine character is annoying rather than warm.

**Opposites Attract**
Characters with contrasting personalities, values, or lifestyles who complement each other. Works when the differences create genuine friction in daily life but also genuine admiration — each character has something the other needs. Fails when the differences are superficial (one likes jazz, one likes rock) rather than structural, or when the characters have no common ground at all.

**Ice King/Queen Thaws**
A cold, controlled character slowly warmed by the love interest. Works when the coldness is a survival mechanism and the thawing is gradual and hard-won — each crack in the armor is an event. Fails when the cold character thaws too quickly (robbing the reader of the slow melt) or when the warmth character does all the emotional labor.

**Nerd/Jock**
Intellectual meets physical/athletic. Works when both characters' domains are respected — the nerd isn't just waiting to be socialized, and the jock isn't just waiting to be educated. Each is accomplished in their sphere and humbled by the other's. Fails when one character type is treated as inherently superior.

**Introvert/Extrovert**
Different social energy needs creating friction and accommodation. Works when both characters' needs are valid and the relationship requires genuine compromise. The introvert isn't broken, and the extrovert isn't shallow. Fails when introversion is treated as a problem to be fixed or when the extrovert just bulldozes the introvert's boundaries.

**Cinnamon Roll Hero**
A male romantic lead who is genuinely kind, soft, emotionally available, and sweet. Works when the kindness is paired with an unexpected strength — he's soft but not weak, sweet but not boring. Give him a spine. Fails when "cinnamon roll" means "has no personality beyond being nice" or when he has no conflict of his own.

**Alpha Male**
A dominant, confident, take-charge male lead. Works when the dominance is paired with genuine respect for the other character's autonomy, when the alpha energy is channeled into protection/provision without control/coercion. The alpha shows vulnerability to the love interest ONLY. Fails when alpha tips into controlling, when possessiveness is presented as romantic without pushback, or when the love interest has no voice in the relationship.

**Strong Female Lead**
A female protagonist with agency, competence, and backbone. Works when "strong" means having clear goals, making active choices, and having an inner life that exists independent of the romance. Strength can be quiet or loud, physical or emotional. Fails when "strong" means "never shows vulnerability," "is rude to everyone," or "can do everything better than every man." Strength without softness is armor, not character.

### Situation Tropes

**Fish Out of Water**
Character in an unfamiliar environment. Works when the displacement creates genuine discomfort, humorous misunderstandings, AND reveals something about the character's core identity that transcends setting. The fish must eventually find their footing — adaptation is the arc. Fails when the character is so adaptable that the "fish out of water" element disappears by chapter three, or when the unfamiliar setting is played purely for laughs without depth.

**Inheritance Condition**
A will or legal requirement forces a situation — often marriage, cohabitation, or running a business. Works when the condition creates a genuine bind that can't be easily circumvented, and when meeting the condition reveals something the character needed anyway. Fails when the legal premise is absurd even by romance standards, or when the inheritance is so large it removes all other stakes.

**Revenge Plot**
One character pursuing revenge against someone connected to the love interest, or the love interest themselves. Works when the desire for revenge is understandable and the growing conflict between vengeance and love creates genuine moral tension. Dark, propulsive, and full of dramatic irony. Fails when the revenge feels petty, when the love interest has no agency in the resolution, or when the character just... forgives without processing.

**Bet/Dare**
The relationship starts as a game, wager, or challenge. Works when the original bet is believable within the social context and when the moment the bet is revealed creates genuine devastation — because by then, everything is real. Fails when the bet is cruel without context (the hero just seems mean), when the reveal has no consequences, or when the forgiveness comes too quickly.

**Matchmaker Falls**
The character playing cupid for others falls in love themselves. Works when the irony is delicious — they know all the rules but can't apply them to their own heart. Fails when the matchmaking subplot overwhelms the actual romance or when the matchmaker's own love story isn't given enough development.

**Only One Bed**
See Proximity Tropes above. As a situation trope, note that this works best as a catalyst rather than the entire premise — the one bed creates a key scene or sequence, but the book needs more than this to sustain a full plot.

**Snowbound / Stranded by Weather**
Weather as plot device forcing proximity. Works when the weather creates genuine danger (not just inconvenience) and the isolation strips away social performance. Fails when the snowbound period is a cozy vacation rather than a genuine trial, or when the weather conveniently clears the moment the couple resolves their issues.

**Wedding Date Needed**
One character needs a date for a wedding and recruits the other. Works because weddings create an atmosphere of romance, public performance of coupledom (fake dating element), and forced interaction with family/community who ask hard questions. Fails when there's no real consequence to showing up alone (the "need" is flimsy) or when the wedding itself is generic rather than a source of specific complications.

**Return to Hometown**
A character goes back to where they grew up, confronting their past. Works when the hometown represents something the character ran from and coming back forces them to face it. The love interest is often someone from the past or someone who represents the life the character rejected. Fails when the hometown is purely nostalgic with no real tension, or when the "city vs. small town" dichotomy is too simplistic.

**Summer Fling**
A romance with a built-in expiration date. Works when the ticking clock creates urgency and when both characters must decide whether to extend beyond the original terms. The temporariness makes every moment more intense. Fails when the "fling" label is dropped too early (removing the clock tension) or when the ending feels forced to create a third-act breakup.

### Family and Community Tropes

**Found Family**
Characters building a chosen family unit. Works when the found family fills a specific void — these characters need each other because their biological families or original support systems failed them. The warmth is earned through shared struggle. Fails when the found family assembles too easily (no conflict, no resistance) or when the group dynamic is told rather than shown through specific, earned moments of loyalty.

**Single Parent**
A character raising a child alone. Works when the child is a real character (not a prop), when parenting is shown as genuinely hard and rewarding, and when the love interest must earn the child's trust separately from earning the parent's love. Fails when the child exists only to be cute, when parenting never conflicts with the romance, or when the child conveniently disappears during romantic scenes.

**Sibling's Best Friend / Sibling's Ex**
The love interest is connected through a family member, making the relationship a betrayal of loyalty. Works when the sibling relationship is important to both characters and the romance genuinely threatens it. The "what would they think" tension should be present in every interaction. Fails when the sibling wouldn't actually care (no real stakes) or when the sibling is absent enough that the connection feels irrelevant.

**Family Business**
Work and family intertwined. Works when the business creates both proximity and obligation, when family expectations conflict with personal desires, and when the love interest disrupts the family dynamic in a way that forces growth. Fails when the family business is just a setting without creating specific conflicts.

**Small Town Dynamics**
The community as character. Works when the small town has its own personality — gossip networks, traditions, rivalries, and a collective opinion on the romance that creates external pressure. Fails when the small town is uniformly charming and supportive, or when it's uniformly hostile and narrow-minded. Real small towns are complicated.

**Workplace Ensemble**
A cast of coworkers who function as community. Works when the supporting characters have their own dynamics and the workplace culture shapes the romance in specific ways. Fails when the coworkers exist only to comment on the main couple or set up sequels.

## THRILLER / MYSTERY TROPES

### Structure Tropes

**Whodunit**
The classic mystery structure — a crime has been committed, and the reader follows the investigation to identify the perpetrator. Works when the suspect pool is genuinely viable (each suspect has means, motive, and opportunity), when the clues are fair (the reader could theoretically solve it), and when the reveal is surprising yet inevitable in retrospect. Fails when the killer is obvious from page one, when the solution depends on information withheld from the reader, or when the detective makes leaps of logic that aren't supported.

**Howcatchem / Inverted Mystery**
The reader knows who did it from the start — the tension is in watching the investigator close in. Works when the criminal's attempts to evade detection are clever and the investigator's process is methodical and satisfying. Requires a compelling cat-and-mouse dynamic. Fails when the criminal makes stupid mistakes to speed the plot or when the investigation is tedious rather than satisfying.

**Locked Room / Impossible Crime**
A crime committed under seemingly impossible circumstances. Works when the solution is ingenious but logical, and when the impossibility is genuinely baffling. The puzzle is the point. Fails when the solution is anticlimactic, overly complex, or relies on improbable coincidence.

**Cold Case**
Investigating an old, unsolved crime. Works when the passage of time has created complications — witnesses have died, evidence is degraded, memories are unreliable, and someone has built a life on the secret. Fails when the cold case is easily solved with one interview, or when there's no reason it wasn't solved originally.

**Unreliable Narrator**
The person telling the story cannot be trusted. Works when the unreliability is purposeful and the moment of revelation recontextualizes everything the reader thought they knew. The reader should feel complicit, not cheated. Fails when the unreliability is random rather than strategic, when the true version of events is less interesting than the false one, or when the narrator's lies serve the plot rather than the character.

**Dual Timeline**
Past and present narratives running simultaneously. Works when both timelines are compelling independently AND when the connections between them create escalating reveals. Each timeline should illuminate the other. Fails when one timeline is clearly more interesting (readers skim the other), when the connections are superficial, or when the past timeline spoils the present one's tension too early.

**Race Against the Clock**
A ticking deadline creating escalating urgency. Works when the deadline is concrete, the consequences of failure are clear, and the clock creates increasingly desperate choices. Fails when the deadline is artificial, when it's extended conveniently, or when the urgency doesn't translate to pacing (the characters still have time for long conversations and meals).

**Heist**
A planned theft or operation requiring a team and a plan. Works when the plan is clever, the team members are distinct and necessary, and the inevitable complications force improvisation. The reader should feel both the elegance of the plan and the chaos of execution. Fails when the plan is too simple, when team members are interchangeable, or when the "twist" is that the real plan was never shown to the reader (a cheat).

### Character Tropes

**Reluctant Detective**
An investigator who doesn't want the case. Works when the reluctance is genuine and rooted in something real (trauma, retirement, personal connection to the crime), and when the thing that pulls them in is compelling enough to overcome that reluctance. Fails when the reluctance is performative — they say they don't want to investigate but immediately start investigating without internal conflict.

**Damaged Investigator**
The detective with personal demons — addiction, trauma, broken relationships. Works when the damage is specific (not generic brooding), when it creates real professional consequences, and when the case intersects with the damage in ways that force growth or reckoning. Fails when the damage is romanticized, when it makes the character merely unpleasant rather than sympathetic, or when the drinking/pill-popping is aesthetic rather than consequential.

**Amateur Sleuth**
A non-professional who investigates. Works when the character has a plausible reason to be involved, a specific skill set that aids investigation (and leaves gaps), and when their amateur status creates genuine danger and obstacles that a professional wouldn't face. Fails when the amateur outperforms trained investigators without explanation, when law enforcement is inexplicably absent, or when the amateur repeatedly enters dangerous situations with no consequences.

**Vigilante**
A character operating outside the law to achieve justice. Works when the legal system has demonstrably failed, when the vigilante's methods have real moral costs, and when the story doesn't shy away from the ethical complexity. Fails when vigilante justice is presented as uncomplicated heroism, or when the vigilante faces no consequences and no moral reckoning.

**The Insider**
The investigator who is part of the world they're investigating. Works when the insider knowledge creates both advantages and conflicts of loyalty. They know where the bodies are buried because they helped bury some. Fails when the insider has no genuine loyalty to the group they're investigating, removing the moral tension.

**The Outsider**
An investigator entering an unfamiliar world. Works when the outsider's ignorance creates both vulnerability and fresh perspective. They see things insiders have normalized. Fails when the outsider instantly understands complex social dynamics, or when the community inexplicably opens up to a stranger.

**The Wrong Man**
An innocent person accused or pursued. Works when the evidence against them is genuinely damning, when their efforts to prove innocence create new complications, and when the reader feels the claustrophobia of being falsely accused. Fails when the evidence is flimsy (why would anyone believe it?) or when the real culprit is obvious.

### Situation Tropes

**Missing Person**
Someone has disappeared. Works when the missing person is complex (not just a victim-in-waiting), when the search reveals uncomfortable truths about the missing person and those who knew them, and when the disappearance has ripple effects on the community. Fails when the missing person is generic or when the search is straightforward.

**Revenge Narrative**
A character seeking payback for a past wrong. Works when the original wrong is viscerally felt, when the revenge plan is specific and escalating, and when the pursuit of revenge transforms the seeker in ways they didn't anticipate. Fails when the wrong is trivial, when the revenge is easily achieved, or when the moral cost is never examined.

**Conspiracy**
A hidden group controlling events from the shadows. Works when the conspiracy is layered — each revelation leads to a bigger truth — and when the protagonist's growing awareness puts them in increasing danger. Fails when the conspiracy is too large to be believable, when the explanation is delivered in a monologue, or when the conspiracy has no clear motivation.

**Betrayal**
A trusted figure revealed as an enemy. Works when the trust was genuine and specific (the reader feels the betrayal too), when the betrayal serves a clear purpose for the betrayer, and when the aftermath explores the emotional damage, not just the plot implications. Fails when the betrayer was never well-developed enough for the betrayal to sting, or when the betrayal is predictable.

**Witness Protection / New Identity**
A character living under an assumed identity for safety. Works when the old life keeps intruding, when the new identity creates specific conflicts, and when the tension between safety and truth drives decisions. Fails when the new identity is boring and the character just wants their old life back without complication.

**Gaslighting**
A character systematically made to doubt their own reality. Works when the gaslighting is insidious and gradual, when the reader isn't sure what's real either (or is screaming at the page because they can see it), and when the moment of clarity is powerful. Fails when the gaslighting is obvious from the start or when it's used for shock value without exploring the psychological damage.

**Trapped / Contained Thriller**
Characters confined in a space with danger. Works when the confined space creates pressure that forces character revelation, when escape attempts are clever and escalating, and when the containment has psychological as well as physical effects. Fails when the trap is easily escapable and characters just don't try, or when the confined setting becomes monotonous.

## FANTASY / SCI-FI TROPES

### World Tropes

**Chosen One**
A character singled out by prophecy or destiny. Works when the chosen one doesn't want the role, when being "chosen" comes with a genuine cost, and when the prophecy is ambiguous enough to create uncertainty. Fails when the chosen one is special in every way (good at everything, loved by everyone), when the prophecy removes all tension (we know they'll win), or when the "choice" is just a power upgrade.

**Hidden World**
A secret magical/advanced society existing alongside the mundane. Works when the hidden world has its own internal logic, politics, and problems that are not just reflections of the mundane world. The moment of discovery should permanently alter the protagonist's understanding. Fails when the hidden world is easily found (how was it ever hidden?), when the secrecy has no cost, or when the mundane world is irrelevant once the hidden world is revealed.

**Portal Fantasy**
A character transported to another world. Works when both worlds are fully realized, when skills from the origin world matter in the new one in unexpected ways, and when the choice of whether to return creates genuine tension. Fails when the character adapts to the new world instantly, when the origin world is abandoned without consequence, or when the portal is a one-way convenience.

**Dystopia**
A society organized around oppression or a fundamental flaw. Works when the dystopia feels like a plausible extrapolation, when the oppression has specific mechanisms the reader can understand, and when resistance comes with real costs. Fails when the dystopia is cartoonishly evil, when the government is incompetent but somehow maintains total control, or when one teenager can topple the entire system in a week.

**Post-Apocalyptic**
Civilization has fallen or drastically changed. Works when the specifics of survival are grounded and believable, when the apocalypse has shaped culture and psychology in specific ways, and when the story is about rebuilding or adapting rather than just surviving. Fails when the post-apocalyptic setting is just an excuse for a lawless playground, or when characters have mysteriously maintained modern sensibilities without modern infrastructure.

**Space Opera**
Epic sci-fi with grand scale — empires, fleets, interstellar politics. Works when the scale serves emotional storytelling, when the politics feel real and personal, and when the technology is wondrous without overshadowing character. Fails when it's all worldbuilding and no heart, when the science is inconsistent, or when the vastness of space doesn't create genuine logistical and emotional challenges.

**Steampunk**
Victorian-era aesthetics meets advanced technology (usually steam-powered). Works when the tech creates specific social changes that affect the characters, when the Victorian setting is more than costumes, and when the aesthetic serves the story rather than being the story. Fails when steampunk is just an accessory, when Victorian social dynamics (class, gender, colonialism) are ignored, or when the technology is inconsistent.

**Urban Fantasy**
Magic in a modern city setting. Works when the magical and mundane elements create specific friction (how do you hide a dragon in Manhattan?), when the city itself is a character, and when the magic has costs and rules. Fails when the urban setting is interchangeable with any other city, when magic solves problems too easily, or when the fantasy elements don't interact with modern institutions in interesting ways.

### Character Tropes

**Reluctant Hero**
A character forced into heroism against their will. Works when the reluctance is genuine and understandable, when what pulls them in is emotionally compelling, and when their reluctance creates friction with other characters who need them to step up. Fails when the reluctance is abandoned too quickly or when it's a pose rather than a genuine character trait.

**Mentor Figure**
A wise guide who helps the protagonist. Works when the mentor has their own flaws, limitations, and agenda, when the mentorship has a cost, and when the mentor's eventual absence (death, betrayal, or the student surpassing them) is meaningful. Fails when the mentor is a walking exposition device, when they're infallible, or when their death is manipulative rather than earned.

**Dark Lord / Big Bad**
The primary antagonist of epic scope. Works when the dark lord has a comprehensible motivation, when their power is demonstrated rather than told, and when they're a genuine threat that forces the protagonist to change. Fails when the dark lord is evil for evil's sake, when they're incompetent despite supposedly being powerful, or when they monologue instead of acting.

**Morally Gray Protagonist**
A hero whose methods or motives are questionable. Works when the gray morality creates genuine reader uncertainty, when each morally questionable choice has a understandable motivation, and when the story doesn't ultimately excuse everything. Fails when "morally gray" means "does bad things but the narrative still treats them as the hero without consequences," or when the gray is just aesthetic rather than substantive.

**Found Family (SFF)**
Characters building a chosen family unit, often a team or crew. Works with the same principles as romance found family, amplified by the extremity of SFF stakes — these characters forge bonds in life-threatening situations. The group dynamic should have internal friction and hierarchy, not just unconditional warmth. Fails when the group assembles too neatly, when everyone gets along immediately, or when individual members are indistinct.

**AI Consciousness**
Artificial intelligence developing sentience or questioning its nature. Works when the AI's perspective genuinely challenges the reader's assumptions about consciousness, when the story explores specific philosophical questions rather than hand-waving "it became self-aware," and when the AI character has genuine agency. Fails when AI consciousness is treated as either obviously wonderful or obviously terrifying without nuance, or when the AI is just a human in a robot body.

### Romance in SFF

**Fated Mates**
A magical or biological bond that identifies a destined partner. Works when the fated bond creates specific challenges — what if you don't LIKE your fated mate? What if the bond is one-sided? What if fate got it wrong? The external force of the bond must conflict with internal desires and choices. Fails when fated mates is just instalove with a magical justification, when the bond removes all choice and agency, or when both parties are immediately thrilled.

**Soul Bond**
A metaphysical connection between characters — shared emotions, telepathy, linked life force. Works when the bond creates both intimacy and invasion, when sharing emotions or thoughts is as terrifying as it is connecting. Fails when the bond is all upside (knowing each other perfectly with no discomfort) or when it removes the need for actual communication and vulnerability.

**Rivals to Lovers in Magical Setting**
The enemies-to-lovers dynamic amplified by magical competition — rival schools, competing courts, opposing factions. Works when the magical rivalry has real stakes and when the magic creates unique expressions of conflict and attraction. Fails when the rivalry is academic (no one really loses anything) or when the magical setting doesn't change how the rivals-to-lovers dynamic plays out.

**Forbidden Species/Faction Romance**
Love across enemy lines — different species, warring factions, opposing magical traditions. Works when the prohibition is backed by genuine history and consequence, when both sides have valid grievances, and when the relationship forces characters to question inherited beliefs. Fails when the "forbidden" element is easily overcome, when one side is clearly right (removing the moral tension), or when the species/faction differences are cosmetic.

## HORROR TROPES

### Setting Tropes

**Haunted House**
A location with a dark history that manifests. Works when the house has a specific, discoverable history, when the haunting escalates in a pattern the characters can partially decode, and when leaving isn't a viable option (they can't afford to, they inherited it, something holds them). The house should feel like a character. Fails when the characters stay for no reason, when the haunting is random without pattern, or when the house's history is generic.

**Isolated Location**
Cut off from help — remote cabin, island, research station, snowbound lodge. Works when the isolation strips away safety nets systematically, when the setting itself becomes hostile, and when the characters' desperation to reach the outside world creates escalating tension. Fails when the isolation is easily broken (just walk to town) or when it's just a backdrop for unrelated scares.

**Small Town with a Secret**
An entire community hiding something dark. Works when the normalcy of the town is maintained alongside the wrongness, when the secret is woven into the town's identity and economy, and when the protagonist's discovery puts them in genuine danger from people who seem friendly. Fails when the secret is obvious (the town is clearly creepy from page one), when the townspeople are uniformly sinister, or when the secret is too absurd to sustain belief.

**Ancient Evil Awakened**
Something dormant has returned. Works when the awakening is tied to a specific human action (greed, curiosity, hubris), when the ancient evil operates by rules the characters must discover, and when the horror is both cosmic and intimate. Fails when the ancient evil is just a big monster, when it has no personality or agenda, or when the awakening is accidental and unmotivated.

### Character Tropes

**Final Girl**
The last survivor, traditionally female. Works when the final girl's survival is earned through specific traits, decisions, and growth, not just luck. Modern versions interrogate and subvert the traditional "purity" requirements. Fails when the final girl survives because the plot requires it rather than because of her own actions, or when other characters die stupidly just to thin the herd.

**The Skeptic**
A character who refuses to believe in the threat. Works when the skepticism is rational and grounded — the audience should understand why this person doesn't believe. The moment they're forced to accept the truth should be a devastating turning point. Fails when the skeptic maintains disbelief past the point of reason (we've all seen the monster, Karen) or when their skepticism has no consequences.

**Unreliable Narrator (Horror)**
A narrator whose perception may be compromised — by madness, drugs, trauma, or the supernatural. Works when the unreliability creates dread (is this real or imagined? which answer is worse?), and when the story functions on both interpretations. Fails when the "it was all in their head" reveal cheapens the preceding horror, or when the unreliability is used to justify nonsensical plotting.

**The Child Who Sees Things**
A child character who perceives the supernatural. Works when the child's perspective creates dramatic irony (they see what adults dismiss), when the child is a character rather than a device, and when adult dismissal of the child's warnings has real consequences. Fails when the child is used purely for creepiness, when their ability has no cost, or when adults ignore specific, credible warnings for no reason.

### Monster Tropes

**Vampire**
Undead, blood-drinking, often aristocratic. Works when the vampire embodies a specific fear or desire — immortality's cost, the predator among us, seduction as predation, power without accountability. The rules of the vampire should serve the story's themes. Fails when the vampire is just a human with fangs, when the mythology is inconsistent, or when the horror of vampirism is completely sanitized for romance without acknowledgment.

**Werewolf**
Human who transforms into beast. Works when the transformation represents a specific loss of control — the civilized self vs. the animal self. The horror is in what you might do, what you might become. Pack dynamics can add social complexity. Fails when the werewolf is just a shapeshifter convenience, when transformation has no cost or horror, or when pack politics overshadow the personal horror.

**Cosmic Horror**
Entities or truths beyond human comprehension. Works when the horror comes from the IMPLICATIONS rather than the monster itself, when human insignificance is felt viscerally rather than stated, and when encountering the cosmic has a permanent, degrading effect on characters. Fails when the cosmic horror is defeated by punching it, when it's just a big alien, or when the "incomprehensible" entity is fully comprehended by the end.

**Psychological Horror**
The threat comes from within — the mind, perception, identity. Works when the reader cannot be sure what's real, when the horror reflects a specific psychological truth, and when the resolution (or lack thereof) is as disturbing as the journey. Fails when "psychological" means "nothing actually happens," when the explanation is reductive (it was just schizophrenia), or when the ambiguity is frustrating rather than unsettling.

**Body Horror**
The human body as source of revulsion — transformation, corruption, loss of bodily autonomy. Works when the body horror is specific and visceral, when it represents a thematic concern (loss of identity, loss of control, the betrayal of the flesh), and when the horror is in the process, not just the result. Fails when body horror is used purely for shock without thematic resonance, or when the grossness is the entire point.

**Folk Horror**
Horror rooted in rural traditions, pagan rituals, and the natural world. Works when the folk elements feel authentic rather than invented, when the natural world is genuinely alien and threatening, and when the horror emerges from the collision between modern rationality and ancient practice. Fails when the folk horror is just a costume over a standard slasher, when the rural setting is condescending, or when the rituals are explained too thoroughly (mystery is the point).

## LITERARY FICTION TROPES

### Structure Tropes

**Nonlinear Timeline**
The story is told out of chronological order. Works when the structure reveals information in an order that maximizes emotional impact, when the reader can track the timeline without a spreadsheet, and when the non-linearity isn't a gimmick but serves the story's themes (memory, trauma, causality). Fails when the non-linearity confuses rather than enlightens, when it's used to disguise a thin plot, or when chronological order would actually be more powerful.

**Ensemble / Interwoven Narratives**
Multiple POV characters whose stories connect. Works when each perspective is distinct and compelling on its own, when the connections between stories are surprising and meaningful, and when the whole is greater than the sum of its parts. Fails when one storyline is clearly the "real" story and others are filler, when the connections are forced, or when the reader can't keep track of who's who.

**Frame Narrative / Story Within a Story**
A story contained within another story. Works when the inner and outer stories comment on each other, when the frame adds meaning the inner story couldn't achieve alone, and when both levels are engaging. Fails when the frame is just a device to introduce the "real" story, when the relationship between layers is superficial, or when one layer is boring.

**Epistolary / Found Document**
Story told through letters, emails, texts, documents, recordings. Works when the format creates dramatic irony (the reader knows things the characters don't), when the voice of each document is distinct, and when the gaps between documents create tension. Fails when the format is abandoned when inconvenient, when characters write implausibly detailed and literary letters, or when the format doesn't add anything the conventional narration couldn't achieve.

### Theme Tropes

**Coming of Age**
A character's transition from innocence to experience. Works when the loss is specific and felt, when the world is seen through genuinely young eyes that shift, and when the adult understanding that replaces childhood innocence is bittersweet rather than purely triumphant. Fails when the coming of age is generic (everyone goes through puberty), when the child's voice is actually an adult's voice, or when the story romanticizes innocence without respecting the intelligence of youth.

**Identity Crisis**
A character questioning who they fundamentally are. Works when the crisis is triggered by a specific event or discovery, when the questioning is embodied in concrete choices (not just navel-gazing), and when the resolution involves genuine transformation rather than returning to the comfortable starting point. Fails when the crisis is abstract and navel-gazing, when the character talks about identity without it affecting their actions, or when the resolution is facile.

**The Weight of the Past**
Characters haunted by history — personal, familial, cultural. Works when the past is specific and material (not vague regret), when it intrudes on the present in concrete ways, and when the character's relationship to the past evolves over the course of the story. Fails when the past is melodramatic without being specific, when the revelation of the past is the entire story (nothing happens in the present), or when "healing" is too neat.

**Moral Reckoning**
A character forced to confront the consequences of their choices. Works when the original choices were understandable (not obviously evil), when the confrontation is gradual and painful, and when the reckoning changes the character rather than merely punishing them. Fails when the reckoning is preachy, when the character was always clearly in the wrong (no complexity), or when the consequences are disproportionate (suffering for suffering's sake).

**The Outsider**
A character who doesn't belong — to a community, a class, a family, a culture. Works when the outsider status creates both pain and insight, when the world they're outside of is fully drawn, and when the question of belonging versus independence is genuinely complex. Fails when the outsider is simply superior to the insiders, when the outside community is one-dimensional, or when the resolution is a simple "they accepted me."

## WOMEN'S FICTION / BOOK CLUB FICTION

**Life Transition**
A character navigating a major life change — divorce, empty nest, career upheaval, bereavement, retirement, relocation. Works when the transition forces genuine reinvention rather than just adjustment, when the character's identity is truly destabilized, and when the new life isn't simply better but is genuinely different. Fails when the transition is resolved by a new romance (reducing women's fiction to romance) or when the "old life" is entirely bad and the "new life" is entirely good.

**Female Friendship**
The central relationship is between women. Works when the friendship is as complex, layered, and fraught as any romance — with its own jealousies, betrayals, sacrifices, and deep knowledge. The friendship should be tested and proven. Fails when the friends are interchangeable, when conflict between women is catty rather than substantive, or when the friendship is idealized to the point of unreality.

**Multigenerational Story**
A family saga spanning generations. Works when each generation is distinct yet connected by specific themes, objects, secrets, or conflicts that echo and transform across time. The reader should see how family shapes identity across decades. Fails when the generations are too similar, when the connections between eras are superficial, or when one generation's story is clearly the author's favorite and the others suffer.

**Secrets and Lies**
A family or community built on concealed truths. Works when the secrets are specific and their revelation has concrete consequences for living characters, when the keeper of the secret had an understandable reason to hide it, and when the truth is more complex than the lie. Fails when the secrets are melodramatic without being meaningful, when the revelation is the climax without a satisfying aftermath, or when the consequences are disproportionate.

**Reinvention**
A character deliberately rebuilding their life and identity. Works when the reinvention requires courage, when what's left behind has value (it's not just escape from something bad), and when the process is messy and nonlinear. Fails when the reinvention is too easy (move to Tuscany, find a villa, find yourself, the end) or when the "old self" was entirely bad.

**The Inheritance**
A character receives something from the previous generation — property, money, a business, a mystery, a burden. Works when the inheritance comes with strings, when what's inherited reveals something about the giver and forces the receiver to reckon with family history. Fails when the inheritance is purely good fortune, when the strings are easily cut, or when it's just a plot device to move the character to a new location.

## CROSS-GENRE NOTES

### Tropes That Travel Well Between Genres
- **Found family** — Works in romance, fantasy, thriller, literary, and horror. Adapts to any tone.
- **Unreliable narrator** — Moves between thriller, literary, and horror with different effects.
- **Fish out of water** — Effective in romance, fantasy, comedy, and literary fiction.
- **Revenge** — Functions differently in romance (driving conflict), thriller (driving plot), literary (driving theme), and horror (driving the monster).
- **Forced proximity** — Not just for romance. A contained thriller is forced proximity. A haunted house is forced proximity. A stranded crew in sci-fi is forced proximity.
- **Forbidden love** — Works in romance (the romance IS the story), fantasy (the love story complicates the quest), thriller (the relationship creates vulnerability), and horror (love as the thing the monster threatens).

### Tropes That Are Genre-Specific
- **Fated mates** — Requires a supernatural or SFF framework. Doesn't work in contemporary realism.
- **Locked room mystery** — Specifically a mystery/thriller structure. Translating to other genres requires careful adaptation.
- **Chosen one** — Fantasy/sci-fi native. Can be metaphorical in literary fiction but loses its structural power.
- **Final girl** — Horror-specific. Can be subverted or referenced in other genres but carries horror connotations.

### Combining Tropes Across Genres
The most commercially successful books often combine tropes from their primary genre with one or two from another genre. Examples:
- **Romantic suspense** = romance tropes + thriller tropes (enemies to lovers + conspiracy)
- **Cozy mystery** = mystery tropes + community/family tropes + sometimes romance (whodunit + small town dynamics + found family)
- **Dark romance** = romance tropes + horror/thriller tropes (forced proximity + captive/captor + morally gray protagonist)
- **Romantasy** = fantasy tropes + romance tropes (fated mates + chosen one + enemies to lovers)
- **Upmarket fiction** = literary tropes + genre tropes (identity crisis + coming of age + mystery structure)

When combining across genres, identify which genre's reader expectations take priority. That determines your primary shelf and your marketing.
