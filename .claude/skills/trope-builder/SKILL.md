## WHAT THIS SKILL DOES

- Takes an author from "I don't know what tropes to use" to a fully mapped Trope Stack they can outline from
- Analyzes how tropes interact — which amplify each other, which conflict, which create unexpected chemistry
- Provides genre-specific trope libraries with execution notes (see `references/master-trope-library.md`)
- NOT just a list of tropes — a strategic tool for building narrative engines
- Generates comp title analysis so authors can position their trope stack in the market
- Flags red flags: genre mismatches, content warning triggers, over-saturated combinations, and heat-level misalignment

Work through the phases below in order. Do not skip phases. Ask the intake questions first, then build the analysis from the author's answers.

---

## INTAKE (Phase 1)

Before building anything, gather this information from the author. Ask all questions in a single message. Do not proceed until you have answers.

Ask:
- **Genre and subgenre** — Be specific. "Romance" is not enough. Contemporary romance? Dark romance? Paranormal rom-com? Romantic suspense? If the author isn't sure, help them narrow it down based on their concept.
- **Heat level** (for romance) — Use a 1-5 scale:
  - 1 = Sweet/clean (closed door, no explicit content)
  - 2 = Warm (tension and kissing, fade to black)
  - 3 = Medium (on-page intimacy, moderate detail)
  - 4 = Steamy (explicit scenes, multiple encounters)
  - 5 = Scorching (heat is a major feature, very explicit)
- **Tone** — Dark, light, comedic, literary, suspenseful, cozy, angsty, whimsical, gritty. Can be a combination (e.g., "dark but funny" or "cozy with suspense threads").
- **Tropes they already love or want to use** — What's already in their head? Even vague ideas count ("something with a grumpy hero" or "I want a secret").
- **Tropes they're tired of or want to avoid** — What's off the table? What makes them roll their eyes?
- **Target reader** — Who is this book for? Age range, reading habits, what other authors they read, what mood they're chasing.
- **Any specific "what if" premise** — If they're starting from a concept rather than a trope ("what if a hitman had to protect his target's daughter"), capture that here. The premise drives trope selection.

If the author gives partial answers, work with what you have and flag what's missing. Don't stall the process over a missing heat level if they're writing a thriller.

## TROPE ANALYSIS ENGINE (Phase 2)

For every trope the author mentions or that you recommend, provide this full analysis. Do not just name tropes — break them open.

For each trope, deliver:

- **Definition** — What this trope actually IS. Not the TikTok version, not the oversimplified version. The structural reality of what this trope does in a story. Example: "Enemies to lovers" is not "they're mean to each other then kiss." It's a relationship arc where genuine opposition (ideological, professional, personal) creates friction that transforms into understanding, respect, and ultimately attraction. The enemies part must be REAL for the lovers part to land.

- **Reader expectation** — What a reader who picks up a book with this trope WANTS to experience. This is the promise you're making. If you put "enemies to lovers" on your cover, readers want: verbal sparring, the moment of grudging respect, the first crack in the armor, the realization scene, and a satisfying grovel or surrender. Miss these beats and they will be unhappy regardless of how good your prose is.

- **Key beats** — The specific scenes and moments this trope demands. These are the tentpole scenes readers are waiting for. List 5-8 key beats per trope. These become the backbone of the author's outline.

- **Common execution mistakes** — How authors screw this trope up. Be specific and blunt. "The enemies aren't actually enemies, they just mildly dislike each other" or "The forced proximity has an easy exit the characters just choose not to take" or "The fake dating has no stakes if they get caught."

- **Fresh angles** — Ways to execute this trope that feel current and new. Not subversions (that's a different section), but fresh contexts, settings, or character dynamics that breathe new life into familiar structures. Reference recent successful books where possible.

- **Heat implications** (romance only) — How this trope affects pacing of physical intimacy. Some tropes front-load tension and delay payoff (slow burn, enemies to lovers). Some accelerate it (one night stand, forced proximity). Some create complicated dynamics around consent and power that need careful handling (boss/employee, amnesia). Note these.

- **Conflict engine** — What natural, organic conflict does this trope generate? Every good trope is a conflict delivery system. Name the specific types of conflict: internal (character vs. self), external (character vs. situation), interpersonal (character vs. character), and moral (character vs. values). The best tropes generate multiple types simultaneously.

Consult `references/master-trope-library.md` for the comprehensive trope list and execution notes per genre.

## TROPE COMBINATION MATRIX (Phase 3)

This is where the skill earns its value. Individual tropes are easy to find. Knowing how they interact is the craft.

When the author has 2+ tropes in play, analyze the combinations:

### Amplifiers
Tropes that make each other stronger. When Trope A is present, Trope B hits harder.

Examples to illustrate the concept:
- **Forced proximity + enemies to lovers** = pressure cooker. They can't escape each other, so the friction becomes inescapable. The proximity forces confrontation, which forces vulnerability, which forces connection. Gold.
- **Second chance + small town** = nowhere to hide from the past. Every coffee shop visit is a minefield. The community remembers everything and has opinions.
- **Secret identity + slow burn** = every moment of growing closeness is undercut by the reader knowing the lie will detonate. Exquisite tension.
- **Grumpy/sunshine + forced proximity** = the sunshine character's relentlessness wears down the grumpy character's walls because they literally cannot get away.

### Conflicts
Tropes that fight each other — where delivering on one undermines the other. These aren't necessarily bad (tension between tropes can be a feature), but the author needs to know and plan for it.

Examples:
- **Secret identity + open communication** — one of these has to break. If you're marketing "healthy relationship" AND "secret identity," readers will feel betrayed when the secret blows up OR feel cheated if it doesn't.
- **Instalove + slow burn** — these are structurally incompatible. Pick one.
- **Love triangle + found family** — hard to maintain the warmth of found family when someone's going to get hurt.
- **Alpha male + strong female lead** — can work beautifully, but requires precise calibration. If the alpha dominates, the SFL feels fake. If the SFL never yields, the alpha feels toothless. The dance is everything.

### Unexpected pairings
Combinations that create something genuinely new. These are the trope stacks that make readers say "I've never read anything like this."

Look for tropes from different genres or different categories that create surprising chemistry. A horror trope in a romance setting. A heist structure in a love story. A cozy tone with dark tropes. Name specific combinations and what they could produce.

### Overdone combos
Pairings readers are currently tired of. Be honest. Include:
- What made this combo popular in the first place
- Why it's feeling stale now
- What would need to change to make it feel fresh again

### The Tension Map
For the complete trope stack, map out what TYPE of tension each trope creates:
- **Sexual tension** — Will they / won't they? When will they? Physical awareness, interrupted moments, near-misses.
- **Emotional tension** — Can they trust? Can they be vulnerable? Will they get hurt? Past wounds and present risks.
- **External tension** — Forces outside the relationship threatening it. Stakes, deadlines, antagonists, circumstances.
- **Moral tension** — Should they? Is this right? Values in conflict with desires. Duty vs. want.

Chart which trope is doing the heavy lifting for each tension type. Identify gaps. If the stack has no external tension, the book will feel like it happens in a vacuum. If there's no emotional tension, the heat scenes will feel empty.

## TROPE STACK BUILDER (Phase 4)

Generate a complete Trope Stack. This is the deliverable — the thing the author takes into their outline.

### The Stack

1. **Primary Trope** — The hook. This is what goes on the cover, in the blurb, in the Amazon keywords. It's the reason a reader picks up the book. Choose the trope with the broadest appeal for the target audience.

2. **Secondary Trope** — The complication. This is what makes the story interesting beyond the hook. It's the layer that turns a familiar premise into a specific story. Often this is a situational trope that creates the circumstances.

3. **Tension Trope** — The thing keeping them apart or raising stakes. This is the engine of the middle of the book. When the primary trope wants to resolve (they should just get together / the mystery should be obvious / the hero should just accept the call), the tension trope says "not yet." It generates obstacles, complications, and reasons the resolution can't happen yet.

4. **Wildcard Trope** — The unexpected element that makes THIS book different from every other book with similar primary and secondary tropes. This is often a tone element, a genre-blending choice, or a character archetype that readers don't expect in this combination. The wildcard is what makes the book memorable.

### For Each Trope in the Stack, Provide:
- Which scenes it drives (specific scene types, not vague gestures)
- Which act it dominates (Act 1 setup, Act 2 complication, Act 3 resolution)
- How it interacts with each other trope in the stack
- The moment it "activates" (when does the reader first feel this trope at work?)
- The moment it peaks (the scene where this trope delivers its biggest payoff)
- The moment it resolves (how and when does this trope thread close?)

### Trope Timeline

Map the trope stack across the book's three-act structure:

**Act 1 (Setup — first 25%)**
- Which tropes are established here?
- What promises are made to the reader?
- Which trope creates the inciting incident?

**Act 2A (Rising action — 25-50%)**
- Which tropes are driving conflict?
- What's escalating?
- Where do tropes start to interact and complicate each other?

**Midpoint (50%)**
- What shifts? Which trope peaks or transforms here?
- Is there a false victory or false defeat driven by a trope?

**Act 2B (Complications — 50-75%)**
- Which trope creates the "darkest moment"?
- What feels impossible to resolve?
- How do the tropes work against each other here?

**Act 3 (Resolution — final 25%)**
- In what order do the tropes resolve?
- Which trope resolves last (this is usually your primary)?
- How does each resolution feed into the next?

## COMP TITLE ANALYSIS (Phase 5)

For the generated Trope Stack, provide market context:

- **3-5 published books** that used a similar trope combination. Prioritize books published within the last 3 years. Include title, author, and publication year.
- **Marketing signals** — How did those books present their trope stack? Look at:
  - Cover design choices (illustrated vs. photo, color palette, character positioning)
  - Blurb language (which tropes are named explicitly, which are hinted at)
  - Category/keyword placement on retailer sites
  - Social media marketing angles (which trope got the TikTok attention)
- **What worked and what was missed** — For each comp, note what they executed well in their trope stack and where they left opportunity on the table.
- **Differentiation strategy** — Based on the comp analysis, identify the specific angle that makes the author's version distinct. This is not about being "better" — it's about being *different enough* that a reader who loved the comp will want this book too, rather than feeling like they already read it.

## TROPE SUBVERSION WORKSHOP

When an author says they want to do something "different" with a trope, or "subvert" a trope, walk them through this framework:

### The Core Rule
You can subvert the HOW but you must deliver the WHAT.

Readers pick up a book with a trope because they want a specific emotional experience. That emotional payoff is the WHAT. The plot mechanics, character types, and specific circumstances are the HOW.

- **Subverting the HOW** = Fresh. "Enemies to lovers, but the enemies are rival funeral directors" is a new HOW that still delivers the WHAT (friction transforming into connection).
- **Subverting the WHAT** = Risky. "Enemies to lovers, but they actually stay enemies" denies the reader the payoff they came for. This can work in literary fiction where subverting expectations IS the point. In genre fiction, it almost always backfires.

### Subversion Analysis Framework

For any proposed subversion, evaluate:

1. **Which element is being subverted?** — The setting, the character types, the power dynamic, the timeline, the resolution, or the emotional arc?
2. **Does the subversion preserve the emotional payoff?** — Will readers still get the feeling they came for?
3. **Does the subversion create something better, or just different?** — "Different" is not inherently valuable. The subversion needs to generate new tension, new insight, or a new emotional dimension. If it just removes a familiar element without replacing it with something stronger, it's not a subversion — it's a gap.
4. **Has this subversion been done before?** — Many "subversions" are themselves now tropes. The villain love interest. The "chosen one" who rejects the call. The detective who is the killer. Check whether the subversion is actually fresh.

### Examples of Successful Subversions
- The bodyguard romance where the protectee is the dangerous one
- The amnesia romance where the character chooses not to recover their memories
- The "chosen one" fantasy where multiple people were chosen and it's political
- The locked-room mystery where the room itself is the antagonist

### Examples of Subversions That Annoyed Readers
- Love triangles that resolve by choosing neither (denies the romantic payoff)
- Slow burns that never deliver (subverting the payoff, not the method)
- "Strong female leads" who are just mean to everyone (subverting warmth without replacing it)
- Dark romances that end tragically without warning (genre contract violation)

### The Test
Ask the author: "If a reader who loves this trope reads your version, will they feel satisfied or cheated?" If the answer is "cheated," the subversion needs rethinking.

## WARNINGS AND RED FLAGS

Flag these automatically whenever they appear in a trope stack. Do not wait to be asked.

- **Genre mismatch** — Trope combinations that signal a different genre than the author intends. Example: Adding a "race against the clock" thriller trope to a cozy romance shifts reader expectations. The trope stack needs to match the shelf the book will sit on.

- **Content warning triggers** — Tropes that require specific content warnings. Amnesia, dubious consent dynamics, power imbalances, captivity, age gaps, on-page violence, pregnancy/loss, addiction. Name the CW and recommend where/how to disclose it (front matter, blurb, retailer content warnings).

- **Legally/ethically sensitive tropes** — Age gaps (specify both characters must be adults and note reader sensitivity thresholds by subgenre), power dynamics (boss/employee, teacher/student — adults only, and note the consent implications), captive/captor dynamics, and any trope involving dubious or non-consent. Handle with awareness, not avoidance. These tropes have large, passionate readerships — but they require intentional, informed execution.

- **Market saturation** — Trope combinations that are currently over-saturated. Too many books doing this exact thing right now means harder discoverability even if the execution is strong. Note the saturation and suggest a differentiating element.

- **Heat-level mismatch** — Tropes that promise things the author's chosen heat level can't deliver. "One bed" at a 1 heat level needs a very different execution than at a 4. "Fated mates" at a 1 requires different tension sources than the physical pull that drives it at a 5. Flag mismatches and suggest adjustments.

- **Pacing conflicts** — Trope combinations that create pacing problems. Three slow-burn tropes in one stack = a book where nothing happens for 200 pages. Three high-action tropes = reader exhaustion. Balance the stack.

## OUTPUT FORMAT

The final Trope Stack document delivered to the author includes all of the following sections:

1. **Trope Stack Summary** — Primary + Secondary + Tension + Wildcard, each named and described in one sentence.

2. **Trope Interaction Map** — How each trope works with every other trope in the stack. Amplifiers, conflicts, and unexpected chemistry.

3. **Scene Driver List** — Which trope drives which key scenes. Organized chronologically. The author should be able to look at this and start outlining immediately.

4. **Trope Timeline** — Activation, peak, and resolution of each trope mapped across the three-act structure.

5. **Comp Titles** — 3-5 published books with similar stacks, with marketing analysis.

6. **Differentiation Note** — What makes THIS version of this trope stack fresh. The specific angle, twist, or combination that sets it apart.

7. **Cover/Blurb Signals** — Which tropes to lead with in marketing. Which to name explicitly in the blurb, which to hint at, and which to let readers discover. Cover design implications (tone, imagery, typography style).

Format the final output with clear headers, bullet points, and bold text for scannability. The author should be able to reference this document throughout their writing process.
