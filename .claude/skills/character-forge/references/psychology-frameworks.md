# Psychology Frameworks for Fiction Writers

A reference companion to the Character Forge skill. These frameworks are diagnostic tools for deepening characters — not boxes to trap them in. Use them to generate behavior, motivation, and conflict. Discard anything that contradicts what you already know about your character.

---

## ENNEAGRAM FOR FICTION WRITERS

The Enneagram maps core motivation — not behavior, not personality, but the WHY underneath everything a character does. Two characters can behave identically and be completely different Enneagram types because their reasons are different.

For fiction, the Enneagram is most useful for:
- Defining what a character wants at the deepest level
- Predicting how they'll behave under stress (their worst self) vs. in growth (their best self)
- Building character arcs that feel psychologically real
- Creating conflict between characters with competing motivational structures

### Type 1: The Reformer

**Core Fear:** Being corrupt, evil, defective, or wrong
**Core Desire:** To be good, ethical, balanced, and have integrity
**Core Weakness:** Resentment — a simmering anger they believe they're not allowed to express

**Healthy 1:** Principled, fair, wise, idealistic but realistic. Accepts imperfection in themselves and others. Fights for justice without self-righteousness.
**Unhealthy 1:** Rigid, judgmental, self-righteous, punishing. The inner critic runs the show. Holds everyone — especially themselves — to impossible standards. Seethes with repressed anger that leaks out as criticism.

**Stress Direction (1 --> 4):** Under stress, Ones become moody, withdrawn, and self-pitying. They feel misunderstood and unappreciated. The controlled exterior cracks and the emotional interior floods.
**Growth Direction (1 --> 7):** In growth, Ones lighten up. They become spontaneous, joyful, and able to let imperfection exist without trying to fix it. They discover that life doesn't have to be a relentless improvement project.

**Story Arc Implication:** The One's arc is about releasing the inner critic and accepting that being good enough IS good enough. The dark moment is when their rigidity destroys something they love. The transformation is choosing mercy — for themselves first, then others.

**Typical Wound Patterns:**
- Punished for small mistakes as a child
- Parentified early (had to be the responsible one)
- Told they weren't good enough no matter what they did
- Witnessed injustice they were powerless to stop

**In Love:**
- Shows love through improvement and acts of service (rearranging your bookshelf, fixing your resume)
- Terrified of being seen as flawed by their partner
- Represses desire and spontaneity, then resents the partner for the repression
- Falls for people who are "messy" — the chaos they won't permit in themselves
- Resists love by nitpicking, finding flaws, and convincing themselves the partner isn't good enough

**Best Romantic Tension Pairings:** 1-7 (rigidity meets chaos), 1-4 (control meets emotion), 1-9 (passion meets passivity)

**Fictional Examples:** Hermione Granger, Atticus Finch, Steve Rogers (Captain America), Monica Geller

---

### Type 2: The Helper

**Core Fear:** Being unwanted, unloved, or dispensable
**Core Desire:** To be loved, needed, and appreciated
**Core Weakness:** Pride — they believe they know what others need better than those people do, and they believe they themselves don't have needs

**Healthy 2:** Genuinely generous, empathetic, nurturing without agenda. Can give without keeping score. Acknowledges their own needs.
**Unhealthy 2:** Manipulative through "kindness," martyred, possessive. Gives in order to obligate. Keeps a ledger of favors. Falls apart when not needed. Uses emotional manipulation disguised as care.

**Stress Direction (2 --> 8):** Under stress, Twos become aggressive, controlling, and demanding. The repressed anger at not being appreciated finally erupts. They become domineering — "After everything I've done for you."
**Growth Direction (2 --> 4):** In growth, Twos turn inward and discover their own emotional world. They become self-aware, creative, and able to sit with their own feelings instead of managing everyone else's.

**Story Arc Implication:** The Two's arc is about learning that they are lovable for who they are, not what they do for others. The dark moment is when their helping is exposed as controlling, or when the person they've sacrificed everything for doesn't reciprocate. The transformation is allowing themselves to receive.

**Typical Wound Patterns:**
- Love was conditional on usefulness ("You're such a good helper")
- Emotional parentification (made responsible for a parent's feelings)
- Neglected unless performing care
- Learned that having needs makes you a burden

**In Love:**
- Overwhelming in early stages — too much, too fast, too giving
- Reads the partner's needs almost psychically (and resents it when the partner doesn't reciprocate)
- Terrified of being alone, but frames it as "I just love deeply"
- Falls for people who need saving — confuses being needed with being loved
- Resists love by over-functioning (if I do everything, I'm indispensable and safe)

**Best Romantic Tension Pairings:** 2-5 (smothering meets withdrawal), 2-8 (softness meets hardness), 2-3 (giving meets performing)

**Fictional Examples:** Samwise Gamgee, Molly Weasley, Peeta Mellark, Beth March

---

### Type 3: The Achiever

**Core Fear:** Being worthless, unsuccessful, or exposed as incompetent
**Core Desire:** To be valuable, successful, and admired
**Core Weakness:** Deceit — not necessarily lying to others, but losing themselves in the performance of who they think they should be

**Healthy 3:** Authentic, inspiring, effective. Pursues goals that align with their real values. Motivates others without competing with them.
**Unhealthy 3:** Hollow, image-obsessed, deceptive. Becomes whoever they need to be to succeed. Can't distinguish between the mask and the real self. Will sacrifice relationships, ethics, and health for the appearance of winning.

**Stress Direction (3 --> 9):** Under stress, Threes disengage. They become apathetic, numbed out, and passive — the opposite of their usual driven selves. They zone out rather than face the possibility of failure.
**Growth Direction (3 --> 6):** In growth, Threes become loyal, collaborative, and willing to be vulnerable. They discover that people love them for who they are, not their achievements.

**Story Arc Implication:** The Three's arc is about stripping away the performance and finding out who's underneath. The dark moment is public failure or exposure — the mask comes off and they don't know who they are without it. The transformation is choosing authenticity over image.

**Typical Wound Patterns:**
- Praised only for achievements, not for being
- A parent who lived vicariously through them
- Early experience of being "not enough" that they've been outrunning ever since
- Learned that love must be earned through performance

**In Love:**
- Presents the "best version" of themselves (curated, not real)
- Terrified of being seen without the polish
- Can be emotionally unavailable because feelings are inefficient
- Falls for people who see through the act — it's terrifying and magnetic
- Resists love by staying busy, performing, and avoiding intimacy that requires vulnerability

**Best Romantic Tension Pairings:** 3-4 (surface meets depth), 3-9 (ambition meets contentment), 3-6 (image meets authenticity)

**Fictional Examples:** Jay Gatsby, Tony Stark (early), Rachel Green (early), Tom Ripley

---

### Type 4: The Individualist

**Core Fear:** Being ordinary, having no identity, being insignificant
**Core Desire:** To be unique, authentic, and fully themselves
**Core Weakness:** Envy — a chronic sense that something essential is missing from their life that others seem to have

**Healthy 4:** Deeply creative, emotionally honest, capable of transforming pain into beauty. Sees what others miss. Holds space for the full range of human experience.
**Unhealthy 4:** Self-absorbed, melodramatic, envious, and paralyzed by their own emotions. Creates suffering to feel special. Pushes people away and then mourns their absence. Romanticizes their own pain.

**Stress Direction (4 --> 2):** Under stress, Fours become clingy, people-pleasing, and desperate for connection. They abandon their independence and become needy in ways that shame them.
**Growth Direction (4 --> 1):** In growth, Fours become disciplined, principled, and action-oriented. They channel their emotional depth into something productive instead of just experiencing it.

**Story Arc Implication:** The Four's arc is about discovering that they don't need to be special to be worthy. The dark moment is realizing that their "uniqueness" is actually isolation. The transformation is choosing to belong without losing themselves.

**Typical Wound Patterns:**
- Felt fundamentally different from family (the "wrong" child)
- Abandoned or misunderstood during key developmental moments
- Early loss that they couldn't process at the time
- Told they were "too much" — too emotional, too sensitive, too intense

**In Love:**
- Craves a soulmate-level connection (and will reject anything less)
- Idealizes partners who are unavailable, then loses interest when they become available (the "push-pull")
- Terrified that being truly known will confirm they're defective
- Falls for people who represent what they feel they're missing
- Resists love by creating drama, testing, and withdrawing into melancholy

**Best Romantic Tension Pairings:** 4-8 (vulnerability meets invulnerability), 4-1 (chaos meets order), 4-3 (depth meets surface)

**Fictional Examples:** Heathcliff, Jay Gatsby, Marianne Dashwood, Loki, Mr. Rochester

---

### Type 5: The Investigator

**Core Fear:** Being useless, helpless, incapable, or overwhelmed
**Core Desire:** To be competent, knowledgeable, and self-sufficient
**Core Weakness:** Avarice — not material greed, but a hoarding of energy, time, space, and self. They conserve everything because they fear the world will drain them.

**Healthy 5:** Brilliant, perceptive, innovative. Genuinely wise. Shares knowledge generously. Engaged with the world rather than observing from a safe distance.
**Unhealthy 5:** Isolated, paranoid, detached. Lives entirely in their head. Hoards information and energy. Becomes the eccentric recluse who has replaced human connection with intellectual pursuits.

**Stress Direction (5 --> 7):** Under stress, Fives become scattered, impulsive, and manic. They chase distractions and stimulation instead of going deeper — the opposite of their usual focused nature.
**Growth Direction (5 --> 8):** In growth, Fives become decisive, embodied, and powerful. They step out of observation mode and into action. They take up space instead of minimizing themselves.

**Story Arc Implication:** The Five's arc is about engagement — choosing to participate in life instead of studying it from the sidelines. The dark moment is when withdrawal fails them and they're forced into the arena. The transformation is discovering that connection doesn't deplete them.

**Typical Wound Patterns:**
- Overwhelmed or invaded as a child (no privacy, no boundaries)
- Neglected in a way that forced premature self-reliance
- Learned that the world is too much and the only safety is inside their own head
- Emotions were dangerous or unwelcome in their family

**In Love:**
- Needs enormous amounts of space (which reads as rejection to many partners)
- Shows love through knowledge-sharing and problem-solving, not emotional expression
- Terrified of being engulfed or losing autonomy
- Falls for people who are patient, self-sufficient, and don't demand constant emotional access
- Resists love by intellectualizing feelings, creating distance, and rationing time

**Best Romantic Tension Pairings:** 5-2 (withdrawal meets pursuit), 5-8 (cerebral meets visceral), 5-4 (detachment meets emotional intensity)

**Fictional Examples:** Sherlock Holmes, Dr. Gregory House, Elizabeth Bennet (arguably), Spock

---

### Type 6: The Loyalist

**Core Fear:** Being without support, guidance, or security
**Core Desire:** To be safe, secure, and supported
**Core Weakness:** Anxiety — a persistent scanning for what could go wrong, who might betray them, and whether the ground beneath them is stable

**Healthy 6:** Courageous (true courage, not fearlessness), loyal, committed. Excellent in a crisis because they've been preparing for one their whole life. Trusts themselves and others.
**Unhealthy 6:** Paranoid, reactive, self-doubting, and either rigidly obedient to authority or combatively rebellious against it. Sees threats everywhere. Tests relationships to destruction.

**Stress Direction (6 --> 3):** Under stress, Sixes become image-conscious and competitive. They try to prove they're capable by performing competence rather than actually trusting themselves.
**Growth Direction (6 --> 9):** In growth, Sixes become calm, trusting, and grounded. They discover inner peace and stop scanning for danger. They learn to trust their own judgment.

**Two subtypes matter for fiction:**
- **Phobic 6:** Runs FROM fear. Cautious, hesitant, seeks reassurance. The worrier.
- **Counterphobic 6:** Runs AT fear. Confrontational, daring, aggressive. Looks like an 8 but is driven by anxiety, not anger.

**Story Arc Implication:** The Six's arc is about learning to trust — themselves first, then others, then the world. The dark moment is a betrayal (real or perceived) that confirms every fear. The transformation is choosing faith over certainty.

**Typical Wound Patterns:**
- Unreliable or unpredictable caregivers
- Sudden loss of safety (home, family, stability)
- Betrayed by someone they trusted completely
- Grew up in an environment where danger was real and vigilance was survival

**In Love:**
- Tests the partner's loyalty — sometimes consciously, sometimes not
- Alternates between devotion and suspicion
- Needs reassurance but is suspicious of reassurance ("They're just saying that")
- Falls for people who feel solid, stable, and consistent
- Resists love by interrogating motives, creating worst-case scenarios, and preemptively pulling away

**Best Romantic Tension Pairings:** 6-8 (anxiety meets certainty), 6-4 (security-seeking meets instability), 6-3 (authenticity meets performance)

**Fictional Examples:** Ron Weasley, Hamlet, Samwise Gamgee (also reads as 2), Marlin from Finding Nemo

---

### Type 7: The Enthusiast

**Core Fear:** Being deprived, trapped in pain, or limited
**Core Desire:** To be happy, satisfied, and free
**Core Weakness:** Gluttony — not just for food, but for experience. An insatiable hunger for stimulation that's actually a flight from pain.

**Healthy 7:** Genuinely joyful, grateful, present. Can sit with discomfort without running. Uses their energy and optimism to inspire and create.
**Unhealthy 7:** Manic, scattered, escapist. Addicted to novelty. Can't commit to anything (person, project, feeling) because commitment means closing doors. Uses positivity as a weapon against anyone who tries to make them face hard truths.

**Stress Direction (7 --> 1):** Under stress, Sevens become critical, rigid, and perfectionistic. The fun stops and they become harsh — with themselves and others. The trapped feeling turns into controlling behavior.
**Growth Direction (7 --> 5):** In growth, Sevens become focused, deep, and contemplative. They discover that depth is more satisfying than breadth. They learn to stay.

**Story Arc Implication:** The Seven's arc is about learning that running from pain means running from everything — including joy. The dark moment is when there's nowhere left to run and the pain catches up. The transformation is choosing to stay present with the hard thing.

**Typical Wound Patterns:**
- Early loss or deprivation that they decided would never happen again
- Pain that was dismissed ("You're fine, stop crying, cheer up")
- Learned that the only safe emotion is happiness
- Trapped in a situation they couldn't escape (sick family member, restrictive environment)

**In Love:**
- Electrifying in the beginning — adventure, spontaneity, charm
- Panics when it gets real, deep, or painful
- Reframes problems as "not that bad" rather than engaging with them
- Falls for people who are exciting and slightly unpredictable
- Resists love by keeping things light, avoiding conflict, and maintaining escape routes

**Best Romantic Tension Pairings:** 7-1 (freedom meets structure), 7-5 (breadth meets depth), 7-4 (lightness meets heaviness)

**Fictional Examples:** Peter Pan, The Doctor (Doctor Who), Captain Jack Sparrow, Fred and George Weasley

---

### Type 8: The Challenger

**Core Fear:** Being controlled, harmed, or violated
**Core Desire:** To protect themselves, be in control of their own life, and determine their own course
**Core Weakness:** Lust — not sexual (necessarily), but an intensity and excess in everything. They live big, fight hard, love fiercely, and rage enormously.

**Healthy 8:** Protective, magnanimous, heroic. Uses strength in service of others. Vulnerable with trusted people. A force of nature in the best sense.
**Unhealthy 8:** Domineering, destructive, ruthless. Sees vulnerability as weakness. Controls through intimidation. Destroys what they can't control. "I'll hurt you before you can hurt me."

**Stress Direction (8 --> 5):** Under stress, Eights withdraw. They become secretive, suspicious, and isolated. The person who dominates every room suddenly disappears into one.
**Growth Direction (8 --> 2):** In growth, Eights become tender, nurturing, and emotionally open. They discover that vulnerability is not weakness — it's the ultimate act of strength.

**Story Arc Implication:** The Eight's arc is about vulnerability. The dark moment is when their strength destroys something precious — usually a relationship. The transformation is allowing someone to see them without armor.

**Typical Wound Patterns:**
- Victimized, betrayed, or overpowered early in life
- Decided they would NEVER be weak or vulnerable again
- Had to be strong too young (fought for survival — physically, emotionally, or economically)
- Witnessed someone they loved being victimized and were powerless to stop it

**In Love:**
- Intensity that can feel like being consumed
- Protects ferociously — which can become possessive
- Terrified of betrayal (vulnerability was offered and punished)
- Falls for people with quiet strength — people who can't be bulldozed
- Resists love by dominating, testing, and maintaining control of the relationship's terms

**Best Romantic Tension Pairings:** 8-2 (armor meets softness), 8-5 (intensity meets detachment), 8-4 (invulnerability meets raw emotion)

**Fictional Examples:** Rhett Butler, Khal Drogo, Rosa Diaz, Lady Macbeth, Wolverine

---

### Type 9: The Peacemaker

**Core Fear:** Loss, fragmentation, separation, conflict
**Core Desire:** To have inner peace, harmony, and wholeness
**Core Weakness:** Sloth — not laziness, but a falling asleep to their own desires, opinions, and anger. They merge with others' agendas to avoid conflict.

**Healthy 9:** Grounded, accepting, genuinely peaceful (not just conflict-avoidant). A calm, powerful presence. Sees all sides without losing their own.
**Unhealthy 9:** Passive, disengaged, stubborn through inaction. Has merged so completely with others' desires that they've lost themselves. The anger they refuse to acknowledge comes out as passive-aggression, stubbornness, or complete withdrawal.

**Stress Direction (9 --> 6):** Under stress, Nines become anxious, worried, and suspicious. The calm exterior cracks and the buried anxiety floods.
**Growth Direction (9 --> 3):** In growth, Nines become energized, focused, and self-assertive. They discover their own desires and pursue them with startling effectiveness.

**Story Arc Implication:** The Nine's arc is about waking up — finding their own voice, their own anger, their own desires. The dark moment is realizing they've been sleepwalking through their own life and everyone around them has been making decisions for them. The transformation is choosing to show up and fight for what they want.

**Typical Wound Patterns:**
- Overlooked or invisible in the family
- Conflict at home taught them that having opinions was dangerous
- Absorbed someone else's identity because theirs didn't seem to matter
- Learned that keeping the peace was their only valuable role

**In Love:**
- Adapts to the partner's preferences so seamlessly they lose themselves
- Avoids conflict until resentment builds to a breaking point, then either explodes or walks away
- Terrified of abandonment but won't say so — just quietly accommodates everything
- Falls for strong-willed people who make decisions for them (then resents it)
- Resists love by numbing out, going along to get along, and never stating what they actually want

**Best Romantic Tension Pairings:** 9-8 (passivity meets aggression), 9-3 (inaction meets ambition), 9-1 (acceptance meets criticism)

**Fictional Examples:** Bilbo Baggins (pre-adventure), Fantine (Les Mis), Pam Beesly (early), Aang (early)

## ATTACHMENT STYLE GUIDE FOR ROMANCE

Attachment theory is the single most useful psychological framework for writing romance. It explains why characters pursue, withdraw, test, and sabotage love — and it does so with behavioral specificity that translates directly to scene-level craft.

### The Four Attachment Styles

#### Secure Attachment

**Core Belief:** "I am worthy of love. Others can be trusted. Relationships are safe."

**In Relationships:**
- Comfortable with intimacy AND independence
- Communicates needs directly
- Doesn't play games, test loyalty, or manufacture drama
- Can handle conflict without it becoming an existential threat
- Regulates their own emotions while being responsive to their partner's

**How They Flirt:** Directly. Warmly. Without games. A secure person flirts by showing genuine interest and being present. It can actually read as boring on the page because there's no push-pull — which is why most romance protagonists are NOT secure at the start.

**How They Fight:** Fair. They address the issue, not the person. They stay present during conflict. They repair quickly. They don't stonewall or escalate.

**How They Love:** Consistently. Steadily. Without drama. They show up. They make space. They don't weaponize affection.

**How They Leave:** Clearly and kindly, when they've decided the relationship isn't working. They don't ghost, they don't manipulate, they don't drag it out.

**Romance Use:** Secure characters usually serve as the "after" state — what the protagonist is growing toward. A fully secure character as a protagonist can work, but the conflict must come from external forces or from being paired with an insecure partner who challenges their security. Alternatively, a character who APPEARS secure but is actually performing security (earned-secure covering old wounds) has rich story potential.

---

#### Anxious-Preoccupied Attachment

**Core Belief:** "I'm not enough. If I let go, they'll leave. I have to earn love constantly."

**In Relationships:**
- Hyper-attuned to partner's moods and behaviors
- Reads into everything (a slow text response becomes "they don't love me anymore")
- Needs constant reassurance but can't be reassured for long
- Gives too much too fast in an attempt to become indispensable
- Abandonment terror runs the show

**How They Flirt:** Intensely. They come on strong — too available, too eager, too much eye contact. They study the object of interest and become exactly what that person wants. It's magnetic and slightly overwhelming.

**How They Fight:** Escalation. They push for immediate resolution because they can't tolerate the uncertainty. They'll start a fight just to get a response — because negative attention is better than no attention. They cry, plead, pursue, or rage. Walking away mid-fight is their worst nightmare.

**How They Love:** With their whole chest. All in, no safety net. They track their partner's emotional state like a seismograph. Their mood is a mirror of their partner's. When the relationship is good, they're euphoric. When it wavers, they're devastated.

**How They Leave:** They don't. Not easily. They'll endure almost anything rather than face abandonment. When they do finally leave, it's after years of trying to make it work, and they're gutted. They'll reach out again. And again.

**Behavioral Tells for Writing:**
- Checks their phone obsessively
- Overanalyzes conversations after the fact
- Apologizes preemptively
- Plans elaborate gestures (buying love)
- Becomes physically ill when the relationship is uncertain
- Internal monologue is a constant running commentary on the partner's perceived investment level

**Triggered By:** Distance, silence, perceived coldness, the partner spending time with others, inconsistency, mixed signals, anyone who reminds them of the parent/person who was intermittently available.

---

#### Dismissive-Avoidant Attachment

**Core Belief:** "I don't need anyone. Needing people is dangerous. Independence is safety."

**In Relationships:**
- Maintains emotional distance even in committed relationships
- Minimizes feelings (their own and their partner's)
- Values autonomy above all else
- Uncomfortable with too much closeness — it feels like suffocation
- Genuinely believes they'd be fine alone (and yet keeps entering relationships)

**How They Flirt:** Sparingly. They're charming in small doses — a look, a comment, a moment of unexpected warmth that makes the recipient feel special precisely because it's rare. They pull back after showing interest, creating a maddening push-pull that reads as mystery.

**How They Fight:** They don't — they shut down. Stonewall, walk away, go silent. If pressed, they become contemptuous or dismissive: "You're overreacting." "This isn't a big deal." They can be devastatingly cold when they feel cornered.

**How They Love:** In actions, not words. They'll fix your car but not say "I love you." They'll show up in a crisis but be absent in daily tenderness. Their love lives in the gaps between what they say and what they do.

**How They Leave:** Cleanly and with apparent ease. They compartmentalize. They seem fine — almost too fine — and this drives their partner insane. The grief comes later, privately, if at all.

**Behavioral Tells for Writing:**
- Creates physical space (sits across the room, not next to someone)
- Changes the subject when conversations get emotional
- Has a rigid personal routine that they protect fiercely
- Appears self-contained and unbothered (the internal world is more complex)
- Describes emotions in physical terms rather than emotional ones ("My chest felt tight" rather than "I was scared")
- Retreats to work, exercise, or solitary activities when overwhelmed

**Triggered By:** Demands for emotional intimacy, clinginess, being told they're "cold" or "unavailable," feeling trapped, someone needing too much from them, vulnerability being expected or required.

---

#### Fearful-Avoidant (Disorganized) Attachment

**Core Belief:** "I want love desperately but I'm terrified of it. People who get close enough to love me will hurt me."

This is the most complex attachment style and often produces the most compelling romance characters. The fearful-avoidant lives in a constant push-pull — craving the very thing they're running from.

**In Relationships:**
- Chaotic approach-avoidance pattern (come close, push away, panic, come back)
- Simultaneously terrified of abandonment AND engulfment
- Can swing from anxious (when they fear loss) to avoidant (when they fear closeness) within the same conversation
- Often has a history of trauma that makes love and danger feel intertwined
- The most likely style to self-sabotage a good relationship

**How They Flirt:** Unpredictably. Moments of intense, almost startling connection followed by sudden withdrawal. They run hot and cold — not as a game, but because both impulses are genuine. One moment they're looking at you like you're the only person alive. The next moment they're across the room pretending you don't exist.

**How They Fight:** Explosively and confusingly. They escalate, then shut down, then escalate again. They say "I don't care" when they care desperately. They threaten to leave when they're terrified of being left. They push hardest at the exact moment they most want to be held.

**How They Love:** Like someone defusing a bomb. Every tender moment is shadowed by the anticipation of pain. When they let themselves feel it — those rare unguarded moments — the intensity is staggering precisely because of how hard they've fought against it.

**How They Leave:** In a way that ensures the door isn't fully closed. They leave dramatically, chaotically, or ambiguously. They want to be chased. They want the other person to prove it's safe. And if the other person does chase, they run further. It's agony for everyone involved.

**Behavioral Tells for Writing:**
- Hot-cold pattern that confuses everyone including themselves
- Sabotages good moments (starts a fight after a perfect date)
- Hypervigilant to threat but also to tenderness (both register as danger)
- Uses humor, deflection, or aggression to avoid vulnerability
- Physical intimacy may be easier than emotional intimacy (sex is less threatening than saying "I need you")
- Internal monologue is contradictory ("Go. Stay. I want you. Get away from me. Don't leave.")

**Triggered By:** Everything. Too much closeness triggers avoidance. Too much distance triggers anxiety. Being seen triggers fear. Not being seen triggers loneliness. This style is in a constant state of activation.

---

### Attachment Pairing Dynamics

#### Anxious + Avoidant: The Classic Romance Engine

This is the most common pairing in romance fiction and in real life, and for good reason — it creates an almost addictive cycle of pursuit and withdrawal that generates enormous tension.

**The Pattern:**
1. Avoidant creates distance (normal for them)
2. Anxious interprets distance as rejection (their wound activates)
3. Anxious pursues harder (calls, texts, shows up, demands reassurance)
4. Avoidant feels suffocated and pulls further away
5. Anxious panics and escalates
6. Avoidant shuts down or leaves
7. Anxious is devastated, confirms "I'm not enough"
8. Avoidant, now alone, feels the loss and reaches back out
9. Cycle repeats

**For the romance arc:** The anxious partner must learn to self-soothe and give space without spiraling. The avoidant partner must learn to tolerate closeness and communicate needs instead of disappearing. Meeting in the middle is the HEA.

**Why it works in fiction:** It creates organic, psychologically motivated conflict that doesn't require misunderstandings or contrived obstacles. The conflict IS the characters.

#### Anxious + Anxious

**The Pattern:** Intense, fast, co-dependent. Both partners mirror each other's intensity. It feels like fate — "No one has ever understood me like this." The problem: no one regulates. When both partners are activated simultaneously, there's no anchor. Fights are explosive. Makeups are passionate. The relationship is a fever.

**Fiction Use:** Good for passionate, tempestuous romances or for showing why this pairing ultimately needs growth. Can be used for the "wrong relationship" before the character finds someone healthier.

#### Avoidant + Avoidant

**The Pattern:** Parallel lives that occasionally intersect. Both partners maintain distance. The relationship looks calm from the outside but is emotionally barren. Neither pushes for more, so neither grows.

**Fiction Use:** Rare as a central pairing. More useful as the "before" state — the safe, numb relationship the character is in before the story starts. "We were perfectly comfortable and completely alone together."

#### Fearful-Avoidant + Anyone

**The Pattern:** Chaos. The fearful-avoidant switches between anxious and avoidant responses, so their partner can never find a consistent strategy. What worked yesterday backfires today.

**Fiction Use:** The most dramatic, highest-conflict pairings. Fearful-avoidant characters create inherently unstable relationships that demand growth from both parties. This is where the "broken" characters live — the ones readers ache for because the desire for love is so visible and the terror of it is so real.

### How Attachment Shifts Across a Romance Arc

**The Journey to Earned Security:**

Real attachment theory says people CAN develop "earned secure" attachment through:
1. A corrective emotional experience (someone staying when the character expected them to leave)
2. Repeated evidence that contradicts the core belief
3. A moment of risk that is met with safety instead of punishment

In a romance arc:
- **Early chapters:** The character's attachment style is fully active. Anxious characters are pursuing. Avoidant characters are retreating. Fearful-avoidants are oscillating.
- **Midpoint:** A corrective experience. Something happens that doesn't fit the pattern. The avoidant partner stays during a conflict. The anxious partner gives space without falling apart. Trust is earned in a small, specific moment.
- **Dark moment:** The attachment system is fully activated by a perceived betrayal or loss. The character reverts to their most extreme attachment behavior. All progress seems erased.
- **Resolution:** The character chooses differently. Not because the fear is gone — it's not — but because the relationship is worth the risk. This is earned security: not the absence of insecurity, but the courage to love despite it.

### Red Flags vs. Character Growth Markers

**Red Flags (character is stuck, not growing):**
- The controlling behavior is presented as romantic
- Jealousy is treated as proof of love
- One partner consistently sacrifices their needs and this is framed as devotion
- Conflict resolution always involves one person capitulating
- The "dark moment" behavior would be abuse in real life and is never addressed

**Growth Markers (character is developing earned security):**
- They notice their pattern and name it, even if they can't yet change it
- They apologize specifically, not generically
- They tolerate discomfort without fleeing or controlling
- They ask for what they need instead of testing whether the partner will guess
- They let the partner see an unedited version of themselves
- They choose the relationship over the protection the wound offers

## WOUND CATALOG

Wounds are the foundation of character psychology. A well-built wound doesn't just explain backstory — it generates present-day behavior, shapes the character's worldview, and drives every choice they make in the story.

For each wound category below: the specific wound, how it manifests in adult behavior, the false belief it creates, what the character needs to heal, and how a story can challenge it.

---

### Abandonment Wounds

The core experience: Someone who should have stayed, left. The child/person concluded they were not worth staying for.

#### "My parent left."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Clings to relationships past their expiration. Monitors partner's commitment obsessively. Either can't be alone at all or has sworn off needing anyone.
**False Belief:** "People always leave. If I need someone, they'll disappear."
**What They Need:** To experience someone staying — not because they had to, but because they chose to. Especially staying through the ugly parts.
**Story Challenge:** Put them in a situation where they must trust someone to stay. Then have that trust tested — and rewarded.

#### "My lover betrayed me / chose someone else."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Compares themselves to others constantly. Hyper-aware of competition. Either avoids romantic attachment entirely or enters relationships preemptively guarded. May become the one who leaves first, always.
**False Belief:** "I'm not enough. There's always someone better."
**What They Need:** To be chosen — consistently, specifically, not as a default or consolation prize.
**Story Challenge:** Love interest has an obvious "better option." Chooses the wounded character anyway, for specific reasons that address the wound directly.

#### "My friend disappeared when I needed them."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Tests friendships and relationships. Keeps people at arm's length. Has many acquaintances but no true intimates. Doesn't ask for help because asking gives people the chance to fail you.
**False Belief:** "You can only count on yourself. Needing people makes you vulnerable."
**What They Need:** To need someone, ask for help, and have that need met without conditions.
**Story Challenge:** A crisis that can't be handled alone. The character must ask. The person they ask must deliver — imperfectly but genuinely.

#### "I was given away / placed in foster care / raised by someone other than my parents."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Persistent feeling of not belonging anywhere. May overperform to "earn" their place. May sabotage good situations because they don't trust permanence. Struggles with the question "Where are you from?"
**False Belief:** "I'm disposable. Real families don't give people away."
**What They Need:** To be claimed — to have someone say "you're mine" and mean it permanently.
**Story Challenge:** Found family. Being chosen not by obligation or blood, but by deliberate love.

---

### Betrayal Wounds

The core experience: Trust was given and weaponized. The person concluded that trust itself is dangerous.

#### "Someone I trusted revealed my secret."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Pathological privacy. Shares nothing personal. Has an elaborate public persona that protects the real self. May become a liar themselves — controlling information flow so no one ever has ammunition.
**False Belief:** "If people know the real me, they'll use it against me."
**What They Need:** To be fully known and accepted — to have their real self witnessed without consequence.
**Story Challenge:** A forced reveal. The secret comes out (or must be shared) and the anticipated destruction doesn't happen. The people who matter stay.

#### "My mentor / authority figure used me."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Deep distrust of authority, institutions, and people with power. May become either reflexively defiant or reflexively compliant (both are responses to the same wound). Struggles to accept guidance without suspecting ulterior motives.
**False Belief:** "People in power always serve themselves. Loyalty is a tool for exploitation."
**What They Need:** To experience authority that serves rather than uses. To learn that not all hierarchies are corrupt.
**Story Challenge:** A situation requiring trust in someone with power over them. A mentor figure who earns trust through consistent action, not words.

#### "My partner was unfaithful."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Hypervigilance in relationships. Checks phones, reads into innocent interactions, interprets late nights as evidence. May swing between desperate clinging (if anxious) and preemptive detachment (if avoidant). Trust becomes a currency they hoard.
**False Belief:** "Love is a lie people tell while they're doing what they want behind your back."
**What They Need:** Radical, boring consistency. Not grand gestures — daily, unglamorous proof that the person is who they say they are.
**Story Challenge:** A love interest whose trustworthiness is tested in ways the character can verify. Transparency offered, not demanded.

#### "My family loyalty was used against me."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Torn between family obligation and self-preservation. May be the family member who keeps secrets, manages dysfunction, or takes the blame. Guilt is their default emotional state.
**False Belief:** "Love requires sacrifice. If I put myself first, I'm betraying everyone who depends on me."
**What They Need:** Permission to prioritize themselves without guilt. To understand that self-sacrifice is not love — it's a trauma response.
**Story Challenge:** A situation where their loyalty to family directly conflicts with their own well-being or a new relationship. They must choose themselves and survive the guilt.

---

### Shame Wounds

The core experience: The self was exposed and found wanting. The person concluded that they are fundamentally flawed.

#### "I was publicly humiliated."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Avoids visibility. Refuses promotions, recognition, or situations where they'd be center stage. OR overcompensates by becoming hyper-visible and controlling the narrative before anyone else can. Perfectionism as preemptive defense.
**False Belief:** "If people see me — really see me — they'll see what I'm hiding. And it's ugly."
**What They Need:** To be seen at their worst and not abandoned. For the humiliation to be witnessed, acknowledged, and met with compassion instead of judgment.
**Story Challenge:** A public moment where the mask slips. The old shame surfaces. And this time, someone stands beside them instead of pointing.

#### "I failed spectacularly at something important."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Either refuses to try (if I don't attempt, I can't fail) or becomes a relentless overachiever (if I'm perfect enough, the failure is erased). May sabotage success when it's close — success feels fraudulent because the failure feels like the real truth.
**False Belief:** "I am my worst failure. Everything I've done since is just a costume over the real me."
**What They Need:** To fail again — and survive it. To learn that failure is an event, not an identity.
**Story Challenge:** A high-stakes situation where failure is possible and the character must engage anyway. The outcome matters less than the engagement.

#### "My identity was rejected by my community / family."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Code-switching, masking, performing an acceptable version of themselves. May have left the community and carries exile guilt. May have stayed and carries suppression resentment. The real self exists in private, if it exists at all.
**False Belief:** "Who I really am is unacceptable. Love is conditional on being the right kind of person."
**What They Need:** Unconditional acceptance of the authentic self. A space where the mask can come off.
**Story Challenge:** A person or community that sees them fully and says "yes." The character must risk rejection to receive acceptance.

---

### Loss Wounds

The core experience: Something essential was taken. The person concluded that love is temporary and happiness is borrowed.

#### "Someone I loved died."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** May avoid attachment (if I don't love, I can't lose). May cling to attachment (hold everything tight because it could disappear). Might live in a suspended state — unable to fully grieve or fully move on. Guilt about being alive, being happy, or replacing the lost person.
**False Belief:** "Loving someone means losing them. Happiness is just the setup for devastation."
**What They Need:** To love again knowing it will end someday — and choosing it anyway. To understand that grief is not a betrayal of the lost person, and joy is not a betrayal of grief.
**Story Challenge:** A new love that doesn't ask them to forget the old one. A moment where grief and joy coexist, and the character lets both be true.

#### "My marriage / long-term relationship ended."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Questions their own judgment ("I was wrong about the most important choice I ever made"). May have restructured their entire identity around the relationship and now doesn't know who they are. Dating feels like auditioning for a role they already failed at.
**False Belief:** "I don't know how to be loved. I thought I did, and I was wrong."
**What They Need:** To discover who they are outside of a relationship before entering another one. Then to enter one as a whole person, not a refugee.
**Story Challenge:** A love interest who wants them as they are NOW, not as a project to fix or a void to fill. The character must show up as themselves, not as who they think they should be.

#### "I lost an opportunity that would have changed everything."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Haunted by "what if." May become reckless (grabbing at every chance, terrified of missing another) or paralyzed (if the last leap failed, why jump again?). Resentment toward people who got the opportunity they lost.
**False Belief:** "My one chance is gone. Life is a series of closing doors."
**What They Need:** To understand that there's more than one door. To stop living in the alternate timeline.
**Story Challenge:** A new opportunity that echoes the old one. The character must choose it while knowing it might also be taken away.

---

### Control Wounds

The core experience: Power was taken from them. The person concluded that safety requires absolute control.

#### "I was physically or emotionally abused."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Hypervigilance. Reads rooms instantly — who's angry, who's dangerous, where's the exit. May flinch at sudden movements. May become extremely controlling (if I control everything, nothing bad can happen) or extremely passive (if I make myself small, I won't be a target). Trust is nearly impossible.
**False Belief:** "The world is dangerous. People who love you hurt you. Safety means never letting your guard down."
**What They Need:** Patient, consistent safety. Not someone who promises to never hurt them — someone who proves it over time. Choice. Agency. The ability to say no and have it respected.
**Story Challenge:** A relationship where power is shared, not taken. A partner who respects boundaries without being told twice. A moment where the character is vulnerable and the vulnerability is honored.

#### "I was manipulated by someone I loved."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Second-guesses their own perceptions ("Am I overreacting? Am I being crazy?"). Difficulty trusting their own judgment. May become hyper-independent to avoid ever being in someone's power again. May become suspicious of kindness — "What do they want?"
**False Belief:** "My instincts are broken. I can't tell the difference between love and manipulation."
**What They Need:** To rebuild trust in their own perception. Someone who validates their reality instead of editing it.
**Story Challenge:** A situation where their instincts tell them something is wrong and they're RIGHT. Proof that they can trust themselves.

#### "I was trapped in a situation I couldn't escape."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Claustrophobic — literally or metaphorically. Keeps escape routes open in all areas of life (won't commit to a lease, a job, a relationship). May hoard money (financial escape route). Panics when options are reduced.
**False Belief:** "Commitment is a trap. The moment you can't leave is the moment you're not safe."
**What They Need:** To experience commitment as freedom, not imprisonment. To stay somewhere by choice and discover that the door was open the whole time.
**Story Challenge:** A situation that looks like a trap but is actually a home. A relationship where staying is a daily choice, freely made.

---

### Identity Wounds

The core experience: The self was undefined, unwelcome, or false. The person concluded that who they are is a problem to be solved.

#### "I never belonged anywhere."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Chameleon behavior — becomes whoever the current group needs them to be. Or: fierce outsider identity — if they can't belong, they'll make not-belonging their brand. May move frequently, keep relationships surface-level, or maintain an ironic distance from every community.
**False Belief:** "I'm fundamentally different from other people. There's no place where I fit."
**What They Need:** To be seen as they are and welcomed — not tolerated, not accommodated, but wanted specifically.
**Story Challenge:** A community or person who wants them — quirks, edges, differences, and all. The character must risk being authentic to discover they can belong.

#### "I grew up in the wrong family."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Persistent imposter syndrome regardless of achievement. Feels like a visitor in their own life. May overidentify with a chosen family/group. May harbor secret resentment toward biological family alongside guilt about that resentment.
**False Belief:** "I'm not really one of them. I'm pretending, and eventually they'll notice."
**What They Need:** To define their own identity separate from where they came from. To build a self that isn't reactive to their origin.
**Story Challenge:** A moment where they must choose between the family they were given and the life they want. And permission to grieve what they're leaving without guilt.

#### "I built my life on a lie / secret."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Exhausted by the maintenance of the false front. Everything is filtered through "does this threaten the secret?" Genuine intimacy is impossible because intimacy requires truth. May become an excellent liar — or may be desperately lonely because the real self is locked away.
**False Belief:** "If the truth comes out, I lose everything. The real me is not someone anyone would want."
**What They Need:** To be loved for who they actually are. The secret must come out and the world must not end.
**Story Challenge:** The reveal. Forced or chosen, the truth surfaces. The character discovers that who they are is enough — and that the people who leave weren't their people anyway.

#### "I don't know who I am without [role/person/career]."
**Behavioral Manifestation:** Existential crisis when the defining element is removed. May cling to the lost identity or flail without direction. Doesn't know what they want, enjoy, or value because those things were always defined by the role.
**False Belief:** "I am what I do / who I'm with. Without that, I'm nothing."
**What They Need:** To discover a self that exists independent of any role. To answer "who am I?" with something that doesn't reference another person, a job title, or an accomplishment.
**Story Challenge:** Strip the identity away. Force them to exist as just themselves. Then let them discover that "just themselves" is someone worth being.

## USING THESE FRAMEWORKS TOGETHER

The most psychologically rich characters sit at the intersection of multiple frameworks:

**Start with the wound.** The wound determines everything else.

**The wound shapes attachment style.** Abandonment wounds tend to create anxious or fearful-avoidant attachment. Control wounds tend to create dismissive-avoidant attachment. Not always — but the connection should be traceable.

**The wound maps to an Enneagram type.** A shame wound often produces a Three (performing to cover the shame) or a Four (turning the shame into identity). A control wound often produces an Eight (seizing power) or a Six (hypervigilance). Multiple paths are valid — the key is internal logic.

**Attachment style drives romance dynamics.** Once you know each character's attachment style, the romance conflict writes itself. The anxious character will pursue. The avoidant will retreat. The fearful-avoidant will do both at alternating intervals. These patterns generate scene-level conflict without requiring misunderstandings or external obstacles.

**Enneagram stress/growth directions map to the arc.** The character's behavior at the dark moment should match their Enneagram stress direction. Their behavior at the resolution should match their growth direction. This creates an arc that feels psychologically inevitable rather than structurally imposed.

**The wound heals through the relationship.** In romance, the love interest should provide a corrective experience for the protagonist's wound — not by being perfect, but by being exactly the right kind of imperfect. The avoidant character's wound heals when someone stays. The shame character's wound heals when someone sees them fully and doesn't leave. The control character's wound heals when someone earns their trust through consistent, patient behavior.

When all frameworks align — wound to attachment to Enneagram to arc — the character feels inevitable. Like a real person you might know. That's the goal.
