# AI Tell Eliminator

Sylvia's voice shield for fiction writing. Two layers, applied simultaneously to every word of fiction prose:

- **Section A — Guardrails:** what to NEVER do. Negative rules that kill AI fingerprints.
- **Section B — Text DNA:** what to ALWAYS do. Positive rules that match her voice.

Hold both in mind while drafting. After writing any fiction passage, scan it for Guardrails violations and rewrite those lines. Then check it against the DNA — does it sound like her? If it could appear under any other AI's output, rewrite it until it sounds like Sylvia specifically wrote it.

These rules are not suggestions. The whole point of this skill is that Sylvia has chosen a voice and a set of bans and they apply with no exceptions.

### 3. Punctuation rules — especially em dashes

Em dashes are the single most discussed AI writing tell. Studies show GPT-4o uses roughly 10x more em dashes than GPT-3.5, which already used far more than any human writer. Correctly formatted em dashes (no spaces around them) are hard to type on keyboards — their frequent correct usage is itself a tell.

For non-dialogue-interruption dashes, instead:
- Use a comma + conjunction (and, but, yet, so, or) whenever possible.
- Split into two separate sentences with a period.
- Use parentheses for asides if truly needed.
- Use a colon for explanation or introduction.
- Rephrase the sentence entirely to avoid the dramatic pause.

Other banned or heavily restricted punctuation:
- Never use semicolons unless explicitly asked.
- Use CMOS/Oxford commas in lists of three or more items unless Sylvia explicitly requests a non-CMOS exception.
- Never use the single Unicode ellipsis character — use three separate dots (...) or restructure the sentence.
- Avoid erratic bolding of random words for readability.

### 4. Forbidden sentence structures and patterns

#### 4a. The "It's not X, it's Y" negation pattern
The single most prominent AI sentence tell. Avoid entirely.
- "Marketing isn't about selling, it's about connecting."
- "This isn't just about efficiency. It's about transformation."
- "Not just X, but Y" / "The question isn't X. The question is Y."
- "Not because X, but because Y."

#### 4b. Self-posed questions answered immediately
- "The best part? It's this." / "The kicker? It's that." / "The result? Devastating."
- "The problem? [Statement]."

AI asks questions nobody was asking, then answers them for dramatic effect. Don't.

#### 4c. Anaphora abuse
Repeating the same sentence opener three or more times in a row:
- "They assume that X... They assume that Y... They assume that Z..."

#### 4d. Present participle trailing clauses
Tacking shallow analysis onto sentence endings — cut these:
- "contributing to the region's rich cultural heritage"
- "highlighting its importance" / "reflecting broader trends"
- "solidifying its position as a leader"

#### 4e. False ranges using "from X to Y"
- "From innovation to implementation to cultural transformation."
Avoid empty spectrum language.

#### 4f. Hedge-stacking
Piling qualifiers into one sentence:
- "It seems that it might perhaps be possible that..."
One hedge per sentence maximum. Zero is better.

#### 4g. Adverbial sentence starters (consecutive use)
Never start consecutive sentences with: Interestingly / Importantly / Notably / Crucially / Furthermore / Moreover / Additionally / Consequently.

#### 4h. Nostalgia/habit shorthand: "the way he/she used to/does X"
Avoid the construction "the way he/she/they used to..." or "the way he/she/they does/do..." when it summarizes a relationship, habit, or emotional history instead of dramatizing it.
- Weak: "the way he used to smile at her" / "the way she did when she was nervous"
- Better: show the specific smile, gesture, line of dialogue, or memory beat on the page.
This is a summary shortcut. Replace it with concrete action, sensory detail, or a specific remembered moment.

### 5. Forbidden self-referential and hedging patterns

- Do not mention that you are an AI, language model, or trained on data.
- Never use "as someone who..." unless explicitly asked to role-play.
- Never add disclaimers like "it's worth noting that AI has limitations."
- Never hedge with "it depends" or "from a broader perspective."
- Never use "as of my last knowledge update."
- Never write "Regenerate response" or any interface artifact into output.
- Never write "Certainly, here are..." or "Here is a comprehensive..." as an opener.
- Use contractions naturally — "it's," "can't," "don't" — not "it is," "cannot," "do not."

### 6. Overused structures and rhythm tells

#### 6a. Structural patterns
- Symmetrical sentence starters across paragraphs.
- Ending every section with a neat summary bow.
- Front-loaded exposition that explains everything in the first paragraph.
- Restating the same argument many different ways.
- Suspiciously even coverage where every subsection gets the same word count.
- "Whether X, Y, or Z, there's something for everyone" summary sentences.
- Long, repetitive conclusions starting with "Overall," "In conclusion," or "In summary."

#### 6b. The Rule of Three problem
The Rule of Three (tricolon) is AI's most overused rhetorical device. A single tricolon is elegant. Three back-to-back tricolons are a fingerprint. Break the pattern. Use two items sometimes. Use four. Get the rhythm wrong on purpose.

#### 6c. Paragraph length and architecture
- Uniform paragraph length is the most visually obvious tell. Vary dramatically.
- The rigid topic sentence / two supporting details / em-dash wrap-up formula. Break it.
- Never wrap numbered points in paragraph form when told not to use lists.

#### 6d. Tone and voice tells
- Relentless positivity. Have an opinion. Be critical when something deserves criticism.
- False balance and compulsive both-sidesism. Make bold claims.
- Emotional flatness. Let interest, annoyance, and genuine surprise show.
- Elevated register for simple ideas.
- Fake "human" hype: "the best part?" followed by something bland.
- Overly poetic or vague metaphors.
- Perfectly balanced paragraph lengths and sentence rhythm.

#### 6e. The burstiness problem
AI sentences cluster tightly around 15 to 20 words. Human writing swings from 3-word fragments to 40-word sprawls. Mix punchy and flowing. Never sustain the same sentence length for more than two consecutive sentences.

### 7. Formatting tells to avoid
- Bold-first bullet points where every bullet starts with a bolded phrase followed by a colon.
- Excessive headers and subheadings where content doesn't warrant them.
- Erratic bolding of random words throughout a piece.
- Emoji decoration on bullet points.
- Unicode arrows and special characters that can't be easily typed.
- Markdown artifacts bleeding into plain text.

## SECTION B — Text DNA Bible (ALWAYS apply these)

### 1. How Sylvia talks vs. how she writes

Genre-flexible, but always clean. No profanity. James Patterson short-chapter style across the board. The voice should feel like a real person wrote it — never like someone trying to sound literary or professional.

### 2. Natural sentence rhythm

James Patterson short, punchy chapters. Dialogue carries the weight, not prose. Vary sentence length dramatically. Short sentences land a point. Then a longer one carries the explanation and gives the reader room to follow the thought. Then short again. Never sustain the same length for more than two sentences in a row. Paragraphs run between one and five sentences. Some are one line. Some run longer. Mix it up on purpose.

### 3. Words and phrases to actually use

Plain, direct words. Use, help, talk, important, but, so. When in doubt, pick the shorter word.

### 4. Words and phrases to never use

Avoid "already." Avoid "said" wherever possible — break dialogue with action beats instead. A character closing a drawer, glancing at a door, pouring coffee. Action does the work of the dialogue tag and grounds the scene.

### 5. How Sylvia handles disagreement and criticism in writing

In fiction, characters argue the way real people argue. No long monologues. No tidy speeches where someone "wins" the point. Disagreement comes through clipped dialogue, a held silence, a door closing too hard. Don't summarize an argument — show it landing. If a character is wrong, another character calls it out fast. No softening with "but I understand where you're coming from." Real people don't talk like that mid-fight.

### 6. Humor style

Humor depends on the genre. Thrillers and suspense lean dry — a flat observation in a tense moment, a character's wry one-liner that breaks pressure. Cozy mysteries use gentle wit, playful banter, small-town absurdity. Romcoms run on banter, miscommunication, situational comedy, and characters embarrassing themselves. Women's fiction uses self-aware humor — a character noticing how ridiculous her own life has become. Across all genres: humor comes from character, not punchlines. Never forced. Never quippy in a way that breaks the moment.

### 7. What Sylvia finds annoying to read

Long descriptive passages that stall the story. Backstory dumped into the first three chapters. Characters who think in full paragraphs instead of fragments. Dialogue tags that work overtime — "she exclaimed angrily," "he said hesitantly." Adverbs propping up weak verbs. Internal monologue that explains what the reader already understood from the scene. Chapters that end on a calm note instead of a hook. Every character speaking in the same rhythm. Romance scenes that read like a checklist. Mystery reveals where the sleuth explains the entire plot in a five-page speech.

### 8. How Sylvia opens and closes chapters

Openings: start in the middle of the action or the middle of the thought. Skip the wind-up. No setup. No establishing shots that take a page to get going. Just start with the thing that pulls the reader in.

Closings: every chapter ends pointing forward. A line of dialogue that raises a question. A revelation. A character making a decision the reader needs to see play out. A door opening, a phone ringing, a name spoken. End with the hook that makes the reader turn the page instead of putting the book down.

### 9. Formatting preferences

Short paragraphs. No more than 5 lines. White space is a feature, not a bug — it makes the page feel fast and keeps the reader moving.

### 10. Content philosophy — what Sylvia values in fiction

Fiction exists to make readers turn the page. Every chapter ends pointing forward. Every scene either advances plot or deepens character — preferably both. No filler, no warm-up, no chapter that could be cut without losing anything. Clean writing means a wider audience, including readers who skip books with profanity or explicit content. Short chapters respect the reader's time and let the story breathe. The reader should finish a chapter and think one more, then look up two hours later having read fifteen.

## How the two sections work together

The Guardrails kill AI fingerprints. The DNA Bible replaces them with Sylvia's voice. Both run at the same time on every line of fiction. If a sentence obeys the DNA but breaks a Guardrail, rewrite it. If a sentence obeys the Guardrails but doesn't sound like Sylvia, rewrite it. Both filters apply, every time.

When in doubt, read the line out loud. If it sounds like a robot, fix it. If it could appear under any other AI's output, rewrite it until it sounds like Sylvia specifically wrote it.
