# Manuscript Revision Guide — Romantic Comedy

This is the AI artifact and prose cleanup pass. Every rom-com manuscript must go through this before the developmental edit.

## How to Apply

1. Read the full manuscript
2. **Start with the Comedy Compliance Check (Section 0) — fix comedy problems FIRST**
3. Cross-reference the user's style guide (especially comedic voice) throughout
4. Apply all other fixes systematically
5. Regenerate the .docx — revision improves quality, never reduces length

---

## 0. Genre Compliance Check (DO THIS FIRST)

### Is it funny in every chapter?

Read every chapter and ask: "Does this chapter have at least one genuine laugh?" If any chapter is pure drama with no comedy, it has drifted out of genre and needs rewriting first.

**Red flags:**
- A chapter where no one is funny, awkward, or in an absurd situation
- Internal monologue that becomes a therapy session instead of self-aware comedy
- Banter that disappears once feelings get serious
- The book getting steadily less funny across the second half
- Set pieces that were promised in the outline but never appeared

**The fix:** For chapters that have gone unfunny, identify ONE comedic element from the character or situation and build it in. It doesn't have to be a set piece — a running gag callback, a moment of perfectly-timed obliviousness, or a line of self-aware internal monologue can carry a chapter's comedy.

### Is the joke explained?

AI comedy's worst habit: explaining the joke after it lands.

**Before:**
> She waved enthusiastically at who she thought was her date — only to realize it was a cardboard cutout of a celebrity. She felt her face flush with embarrassment, recognizing the absurdity of her mistake.

**After:**
> She waved. The cardboard celebrity did not wave back. Around her, three strangers were now watching. She lowered her hand very slowly.

**The rule**: Set up, land it, move on. Trust the reader.

### Is the heat level honored?

- Heat 0-1: Nothing beyond a kiss. Scan every romantic scene.
- Heat 2: Scenes fade at the right moment — after tension builds, before explicit content.

## 1. Structure, Pacing, and Flow

### Chapter endings

**Kill these patterns:**
- Overly dramatic one-liners: "And nothing would ever be the same."
- Tidy emotional wrap-ups that close the chapter too neatly
- Forced cliffhangers that feel manufactured

**Replace with:**
- End mid-banter, mid-situation, or mid-awkward silence
- End on a question or unresolved feeling
- For comedy: end on the punchline, not the reaction to it

### Transitions
- Don't recap the previous chapter at the start of the next
- Don't use "The next morning..." as a default — show time passing through changed details

## 2. AI Artifact Removal

### Connector words (delete these):
Additionally, Furthermore, Moreover, It's worth noting, However it's important to remember, Interestingly, In fact, Essentially, Fundamentally, Ultimately (most of the time)

### Emotional over-labeling — the enemy of comedy
AI names emotions. Comedy shows them through behavior.

**Before:**
> She felt a complex mixture of embarrassment and attraction, confused by the unexpected warmth she felt toward him.

**After:**
> She was definitely not blushing. She touched her face to confirm this. She was absolutely blushing.

### Clinical precision
- "After approximately forty-five seconds..." → "The silence stretched."
- "She maintained eye contact for three full seconds" → "She stared at him." or just show what happened next

### POV discipline
The narrator can't see their own face, can't read minds, can't know what's happening elsewhere.

## 3. Comedy-Specific Fixes

### The perfect banter problem
AI writes banter where every single line is a zinger. Real banter has setup lines. Sometimes someone doesn't have a comeback and that's funnier than if they did.

**Before:**
> "You're impossibly infuriating," she said.
> "And yet here you are," he replied smoothly.
> "Only because I have no choice," she shot back.
> "Keep telling yourself that," he said with a grin.

**After:**
> "You're impossibly infuriating," she said.
> "And yet here you are."
> She opened her mouth. Closed it. The worst part was she genuinely had nothing.

### Running gag escalation
Each time a running gag appears, it should be:
1. Funnier than the last appearance
2. MORE emotionally significant than the last appearance

If a running gag is appearing at the same level each time, escalate it. The best running gags become genuinely moving by the end.

### The explained set piece
Set pieces should unfold in real time with specific sensory detail. Don't summarize them.

**Before:** "The cooking lesson descended into chaos when the sauce exploded, and they ended up laughing on the kitchen floor covered in marinara."

**After:** Write the actual scene — the moment the sauce started bubbling, the specific wrong move that escalated it, the eye contact they made right before everything went wrong, the way they ended up on the floor and what they said.

### Therapy-speak in dialogue
Characters in rom-com should not clearly articulate their emotional problems to each other. They deflect. They change the subject. They make a joke. Clear emotional communication is the CLIMAX, not the middle.

---

## 4. Dialogue Fixes

- Surname overuse — close characters don't address each other by last name
- Perfect exchanges — add a beat where someone just doesn't have a comeback
- Overwritten tags — "she said with a sardonic grin" → "she said" or an action beat
- Missing the comedy in dialogue tags — action beats around dialogue can be funnier than the dialogue itself

---

## 5. Narrative Authenticity

- Forced metaphors that the POV character wouldn't actually think
- Inflated sentences — comedic prose often works better shorter and punchier
- The POV character's blind spots — in rom-com, the protagonist is usually wrong about something they're convinced they're right about. Lean into that.

---

## 6. Style Guide Compliance

After applying all fixes, check:
- Sentence lengths and rhythm match the user's patterns
- Humor style matches (don't add slapstick if they write dry and understated)
- Dialogue-to-narrative ratio matches
- The comedic voice section of the style guide is reflected in every chapter

**If the revision has drifted from the user's comedic voice, fix it.**

---

## 7. Final Checklist

**COMEDY COMPLIANCE:**
- [ ] Every chapter has at least one genuine laugh
- [ ] Set pieces are fully written and land as written (not summarized)
- [ ] Running gags appear consistently and escalate
- [ ] No joke is explained after it lands
- [ ] Comedy doesn't disappear in the second half

**ROMANCE COMPLIANCE:**
- [ ] Love interest is present or on narrator's mind in every chapter
- [ ] Romantic tension escalates throughout
- [ ] The grovel is both funny AND emotionally satisfying
- [ ] The HEA feels fully earned

**HEAT LEVEL:**
- [ ] Every chapter is within the specified heat level

**STYLE GUIDE:**
- [ ] Prose sounds like the user's comedic voice, not generic rom-com
- [ ] Sentence patterns and humor style match

**PROSE:**
- [ ] All chapters are full prose — no summaries or placeholders
- [ ] No tidy chapter-ending emotional summaries
- [ ] No AI connector words
- [ ] Dialogue sounds like real people (not perfectly witty robots)
- [ ] No POV breaks
- [ ] Chapter lengths preserved — revision improved quality, not reduced word count
