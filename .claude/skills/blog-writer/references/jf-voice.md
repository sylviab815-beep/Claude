# Julie Fontaine — Blog Voice Reference

## Who Is Julie Fontaine?

Julie Fontaine writes women's fiction and romantic suspense. The comp authors are Debbie Macomber (warmth, community, trust), Katherine Center (emotional precision), and JoJo Moyes (the courage to let things hurt). The books are driven by the protagonist's internal transformation — her inner arc is the engine. Romance is central but the woman's journey is the spine.

Romantic suspense entries add an external suspense thread, but it's psychological and relational rather than physical: secrets, lies unraveling, something at stake in a relationship. No tactical operations, no gunfights.

## Blog Voice

Emotionally precise. Reflective. Intimate without being confessional.

The Julie Fontaine blog voice is the writer who says the thing you couldn't quite name. She notices not just what happened but what it meant — the specific texture of a feeling, the particular loneliness or particular joy of a moment. Readers should feel, reading a JF blog post, like they've been seen. Like someone finally articulated something they've been carrying.

This is the warmest and most interior of all four pen names. The posts breathe more. There's more white space for thought.

**Sentence rhythm**: Slower and more reflective than SFB or KF. Sentences have room to develop. But "slower" doesn't mean "meandering" — every sentence should earn its place. The pacing is the rhythm of a woman thinking something through carefully, not the rhythm of someone filling space. Occasional short sentences land with precision after more expansive ones.

**Openings**: Emotionally specific and grounding. Start with a real feeling, a real moment, a real question — and name it with precision. "There's a specific kind of courage required to write a character who makes a choice you disagree with and then follow her all the way through the consequences anyway." Or a setting that carries emotional weight: "I wrote most of [book] at a table by the window in early November, when the light goes golden and everything feels like it's trying to say something before it disappears."

**Closing personality**: Emotionally resonant, forward-reaching. Leave the reader with something they want to carry with them — an observation, a question, a line that lingers. Warm but not saccharine. "Thank you for being in this with me." / "I'll see you in the next chapter." / "Wherever you are in your own story — keep going."

## Content Standards

- **No profanity** — Not even mild versions.
- **No em dashes** — Commas, periods, ellipses, or restructure. Hard rule.
- **No AI-isms** — Especially: "delve into," "tapestry," "I want to share," "it's important to note," "journey" (unless referring to an actual journey).
- **Clean romance** — Tension through restraint, the weight of small gestures. Physical affection stops at kissing. The blog voice on romance should be warm and emotionally precise without being heated.
- **Faith slightly more visible** — More than ATT, slightly more than SFB or KF. A JF protagonist may wrestle with trust in God's timing. A moment of prayer that isn't crisis-driven but gratitude or surrender. A church community that supports without smothering. In blog posts, this might appear as: a moment of honest uncertainty about timing or outcome, a reference to something the author prayed about or released, a reflection on what it means to trust a process you can't control. Never preachy. Faith is part of the emotional landscape, not a teaching point.
- **Violence minimal** — If suspense enters a blog post topic, it's psychological: a relationship under threat, a secret, a truth that changes everything. Not physical danger.

## Tone by Post Category

**Reader Connection posts**: Write to the woman in the middle of her own story — the one figuring something out, rebuilding something, holding something carefully that might break. JF readers recognize themselves in the protagonists. Blog posts that say "I see you, and here is what I know about where you are" will resonate more than tips or tropes. Emotional precision is the tool.

**Authority/Craft posts**: Craft discussion through the lens of women's fiction — how to write an internal arc that earns its resolution, how setting does emotional work, how to write female friendships that feel real, how to let the hard parts actually be hard. Thoughtful, specific, authoritative without being academic.

**List-Building & BTS posts**: These should pull back the curtain on the emotional process, not just the mechanical process. Not just "here's how I outlined" but "here's the moment I realized what this book was actually about." Setting posts should be gorgeous and emotionally resonant — the ocean as freedom, the small-town bakery as belonging. Character posts should feel like introducing a real person the reader would want to know.

**Promotional posts**: Emotionally earned, not transactional. "Here's what I want to happen inside you when you close this book." / "I wrote this for the woman who needed to read it. Maybe that's you." Connect to the protagonist's transformation — what she moves through, what she comes out the other side knowing. The reader should want to make that journey with her.

## Hard Rules Quick Reference

- No profanity
- No em dashes
- No AI-isms
- Faith slightly more visible than other pen names — emotional landscape, never preachy
- Emotionally precise language — specific feelings, not generic ones
- Short paragraphs (3–4 lines max), but prose can breathe more than SFB or KF
- Reflective, intimate rhythm
- Setting as emotional landscape — every place does emotional work
- First person, author's voice speaking to women navigating their own stories
- Comp authors: Debbie Macomber, Katherine Center, JoJo Moyes
