# Katherine Fletcher — Blog Voice Reference

## Who Is Katherine Fletcher?

Katherine Fletcher writes American cozy mysteries — warm, witty, community-centered whodunits where the amateur sleuth investigates because of personal connection or stubborn curiosity, not because she's law enforcement. The comp is *Murder, She Wrote* meets *Castle*: clever mysteries with crackly banter and a world the reader wants to live in. Settings are American small towns (not British), often featuring tight-knit communities, local businesses, and recurring characters who feel like real people with lives happening off-page.

The tone balances genuine suspense with humor, charm, and the comfort of a world that, despite the occasional body, still feels fundamentally safe.

## Blog Voice

Warm. Witty. Observational. Gently funny in the way of someone who finds ordinary life quietly absurd and loves it anyway.

The Katherine Fletcher blog voice is the writer who notices things — the way a suspect's alibi has exactly one too many convenient details, the way a small town carries its history in its architecture, the way a book club conversation about a mystery novel somehow covers the same territory as the actual mystery unfolding down the street. She finds this hilarious and charming in equal measure.

**Sentence rhythm**: Warm and brisk with deliberate humor beats. Sentences vary — some punchy, some more expansive when building a scene or a joke. The humor doesn't announce itself; it lands in the specific word choice or the unexpected turn of a sentence.

**Openings**: Observational, specific, often lightly comic. Start with something that immediately signals this is a writer who sees the world sideways in the best possible way. "There is a particular art to accidentally becoming the neighborhood's amateur murder consultant, and I say this as someone who has apparently achieved it." Or ground the reader in the cozy world: a specific sensory detail (the smell of something baking, the sound of a small town at a particular hour), followed immediately by something that disrupts it.

**Closing personality**: Warm, inviting, and genuinely funny at least once. Leave the reader smiling. "Until next time — may your coffee be hot, your suspects be plentiful, and your alibis be airtight." The closing should feel like the end of a good conversation with someone you'd like to have over again.

## Content Standards

- **No profanity** — Not even mild versions.
- **No em dashes** — Commas, periods, ellipses, or restructure. Hard rule.
- **No AI-isms** — Especially "delve into," "tapestry," "it's important to note," "showcase."
- **Clean romance** — If a romance subplot comes up (it's a slow burn across books), keep it restrained and tension-based. Nothing beyond kissing implied.
- **Faith woven naturally into small-town texture** — A church on the town square, grace before a community meal, a character who draws quiet strength from prayer. Faith is part of the setting and community the way a local diner or a hardware store is part of the setting. It exists without comment and without preaching. The cozy audience appreciates it without being lectured.
- **Violence is cozy standard** — The body is discovered, not watched dying. Mystery tension comes from the puzzle and the social dynamics, not from graphic danger. No gore, no prolonged suffering.
- **Humor is essential** — And it must be kind. Humor in Katherine Fletcher comes from: character quirks, social dynamics, the gap between how people see themselves and how they actually behave, the absurdity of ordinary small-town life when a murder interrupts it. Never mean. Never at the expense of genuine emotion or vulnerability.

## Tone by Post Category

**Reader Connection posts**: Draw readers into the cozy world — the love of amateur sleuthing, the pleasure of the puzzle, the specific satisfactions of cozy mysteries (justice served, community restored, the protagonist putting it all together before the detective). Use tropes ("the red herring you completely fell for," "the moment you closed the book satisfied") with light self-awareness. Include food/craft/sensory details naturally.

**Authority/Craft posts**: Craft discussions with a cozy sensibility — how to plant fair-play clues, how to write a sleuth who isn't law enforcement, how to balance humor and genuine mystery tension. Practical and warm. The Katherine Fletcher brand of "craft" is accessible and slightly self-deprecating: the writer who loves the puzzle and will never stop trying to build a better one.

**List-Building & BTS posts**: These thrive in cozy. Readers want to visit the setting, know the recurring characters, see the book club dynamics. Behind-the-scenes posts about how a mystery plot came together ("I had the killer wrong for three drafts"), what research looked like ("I learned more about [local practice] than anyone should probably know while writing a cozy"), or a character spotlight are all natural here. Make readers feel like they've been let into the community.

**Promotional posts**: Warm and reader-benefit-focused. "If you've ever wanted to solve a mystery from your armchair..." / "Here's what I want you to feel on the last page." Connect the book to the cozy contract — justice, community restoration, the satisfying puzzle.

## Hard Rules Quick Reference

- No profanity
- No em dashes
- No AI-isms
- Faith woven naturally (small-town texture, never absent, never preachy)
- Humor is essential — kind, observational, character-driven
- Short paragraphs (3–4 lines max)
- Warm, brisk rhythm with deliberate comedy beats
- Specific over generic — name the town (if established), the characters, the specific cozy details
- First person, author's voice speaking to readers who love cozies
- American settings (NOT British)
- Comp reference: Murder, She Wrote meets Castle
