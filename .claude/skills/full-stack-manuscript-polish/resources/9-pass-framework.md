# Full-Stack Manuscript Polish — 9-Pass Editorial Framework

A reusable sequence for taking a fiction draft from rough to publishable. Run passes in order. Each pass depends on the previous one.

> Calibrated for Sylvia Baumgartner's pen names: S.F. Baumgartner (thrillers/suspense), Angie Tang Tompkins (romance/rom-com), Katherine Fletcher (cozy mysteries), Julie Fontaine (women's fiction/romantic suspense). Voice DNA: James Patterson short-chapter style, dialogue over prose, clean writing (no profanity), page-turn hooks, no Oxford commas, no em dashes.


Pass 1 — Structure Check
Purpose: Lock the architecture before touching prose. If the bones are wrong, line edits are wasted.
Scan for:

Chapter order makes sense in time and emotion. No chapter doing the work of two; no chapter that should have been split.
Chapter break points land on momentum, not in the middle of a beat.
Front-matter sequence is right (dedication / content warnings / epigraph / opening).
Prologue earns its keep or gets cut.
Opening chapters orient the reader without info-dump. The first three chapters answer: who / where / what's wrong / what's at stake.
Mid-book sag — chapters where nothing structurally advances. Flag for cut, merge, or expansion with new beat.
Climax sits in the right place. The book's biggest moment is at 85-95%, not at 75% or 100%.
Denouement is sized right — long enough to land the emotional beats, not so long it dilutes the climax.

Cross-book canon (for series):

Names, ages, timelines consistent with established/upcoming books.
Magic system / world rules consistent.
First kisses / first sex / first I-love-you placed where they belong in the arc, not duplicated or contradicted across books.
LI roles and constraints stable.

Output: A chapter-by-chapter ledger — title, word count, function, what it advances. Plus a punch list of structural fixes.

Pass 2 — Expansion Check
Purpose: Hit genre word count by adding what's missing, not by padding what's there.
Scan for:

Chapters under genre minimum (PNR/RH typically 2k+ for major chapters; transition chapters can be 800-1500w).
Emotional beats that happen off-page but should be on-page. Each major LI should have at least one body-anchored intimacy beat per book (in zero-kiss/slow-burn books, this is a restraint beat — held inch, not-touching).
Climax expansion — the climax chapter should be the longest or near-longest in the book. Thin climaxes are the most common diagnostic of a rushed book.
Setting/place texture — does the world feel inhabited? Each book should have at least one parish/town/community-life beat that isn't about the plot.
Pathos beats — does each LI have a moment where their cost is felt, not just stated?

Don't expand by:

Padding existing scenes with description.
Adding internal monologue that recaps what the reader already knows.
Extending dialogue past its natural close.

Do expand by:

Adding new in-body sensory beats.
Writing scenes that show what's currently being told.
Drafting restraint beats / almost-kiss / almost-touch moments.
Drafting LI cost beats (what does this character lose / what can they not do).

Output: Numbered list of expansion targets with word count estimate per target. Total target should bring manuscript to genre count.

Pass 3 — Chapter Openings (In-Scene, Not Narration)
Purpose: Every chapter drops the reader into a moment. No setup, no recap, no narration of what's about to happen.
Scan every chapter opener for:

✗ Routine opening: "The next morning I woke up and..." / "That afternoon I was..." / "Three days later..."
✗ Status-narration opening: "For the next week, things were quiet." / "In the days that followed..."
✗ Recap opening: "After what had happened the night before..."
✗ Internal-monologue opening: "I had been thinking about it for hours."
✗ Establishing-shot opening: "The kitchen was warm. The street was quiet. The light was..." (when not earned by being a real moment)

Replace with:

✓ Sensory present-tense moment: "The light woke me." / "The salt line was the first thing I saw."
✓ Action mid-stride: "I drew the blade across my palm." / "He was already at the waterline when I came down."
✓ Dialogue in: "'You're early,' he said."
✓ Image that does double duty as setting and emotional weight: "Frost. On every surface. Five weeks early."

Rule: If you can read the first three lines and not know what's happening or who's there, the opening is doing narration, not scene.

Pass 4 — Chapter Closers / Hooks
Purpose: Every chapter ends pulling the reader forward. No wrap-up summaries.
Scan every chapter closer for:

✗ Wrap-up: "And so the day ended with the boundary holding and the four at their posts."
✗ Recap closer: "It had been the longest day of my life."
✗ Reflection closer: "I lay in bed thinking about everything that had happened."
✗ Foreshadow-via-narration: "Little did I know what would happen the next morning."

Replace with:

✓ Image that holds: "The clock above the stove read eleven-forty-six. I went out."
✓ Line that turns: "What he had not done was already in me. A held inch. Already louder than what we had said."
✓ Action that opens the next chapter: "I went to find her."
✓ Dialogue line with weight: "I know you would. That's the problem."
✓ Quiet beat that carries forward: "For now, it was enough."

Rule: A good closer hands the reader into the next chapter without telling them what's coming.

Pass 5 — Dialogue Diversification
Purpose: Each character should be recognizable from one line of dialogue without a tag.
Scan for staccato echoes:

Everyone answering "Yes." or "No." — common AI/draft pattern.
Everyone using the same affirmation ("Right." / "Okay." / "Sure.").
Everyone hedging the same way ("I think..." / "Maybe..." / "I guess...").
Echo-back patterns where character B repeats character A's word verbatim.
Same sentence rhythm across characters in the same scene.
Adverb-heavy dialogue tags — "she said angrily," "he replied hesitantly." Cut the adverb; put it in the character's words or an action beat instead.

Build a register chart per character using resources/dialogue-register-template.md. Fill it fresh for each manuscript — characters differ by book.

Sylvia's dialogue principles:
Characters argue the way real people argue. No long monologues. No tidy speeches where someone "wins" the point. Disagreement comes through clipped dialogue, a held silence, a door closing too hard. Don't summarize an argument — show it landing. If a character is wrong, another character calls it out fast.

Humor by genre: thrillers and suspense lean dry (a flat observation in a tense moment). Cozy mysteries use gentle wit and small-town banter. Romcoms run on banter, miscommunication and situational comedy. Women's fiction uses self-aware humor. Across all genres: humor comes from character, not punchlines.

Rule: If you delete the dialogue tags from a scene with all characters present, can the reader still tell who's speaking? If no, the registers haven't differentiated yet.

Pass 6 — Language Pass (Voice + Banned Vocabulary + AI-Tells)
Purpose: Match Sylvia's Text DNA Bible and kill the AI-tells that creep in during drafting. This pass runs in coordination with the ai-tell-eliminator skill — use that skill for prose rewrites. This pass provides the diagnostic scan. Run each sub-pass as its own scan. Don't try to catch everything in one read.

6a. Banned vocabulary scan
Run grep / find on every banned term. Zero is the goal. Each hit must be rewritten.

HIGH-ALARM VERBS (never use in fiction prose):
delve / leverage / utilize / harness / unlock / unleash / empower / facilitate / foster / bolster / optimize / streamline / navigate / spearhead / underscore / illuminate / elucidate / embark / unravel / elevate / reimagine / revolutionize / transcend / resonate / showcase / grapple / intertwine / entwine / weave / garner / craft / embrace / enrich / glean / unveil / uncover / champion

HIGH-ALARM ADJECTIVES:
multifaceted / nuanced / layered / intricate / seamless / robust / comprehensive / holistic / meticulous / groundbreaking / transformative / innovative / vibrant / dynamic / compelling / invaluable / paramount / indelible / poignant / timeless / relentless / unwavering / whimsical / captivating / bustling / burgeoning / flourishing / unprecedented / profound

HIGH-ALARM NOUNS:
tapestry / rich tapestry / realm / testament / beacon / myriad / liminal / kaleidoscope / landscape / ecosystem / paradigm / nexus / catalyst / symphony / sentinel / interplay / intricacies / synergy / roadmap / toolkit / facet / journey / endeavor / cornerstone / bedrock / pinnacle / crucible / enigma / linchpin / milestone / plethora / treasure trove

HIGH-ALARM ADVERBS:
furthermore / moreover / additionally / notably / crucially / importantly / consequently / seamlessly / meticulously / profoundly / indelibly / tirelessly / relentlessly / essentially / fundamentally / holistically / undoubtedly / subsequently

DIALOGUE-SPECIFIC BANS:
"said" — avoid wherever possible. Use action beats instead. A character closing a drawer, glancing at a door, pouring coffee. Action does the work of the dialogue tag and grounds the scene.
"already" — cut on sight.
Adverb dialogue tags — "she said angrily," "he replied hesitantly." Delete the adverb; put it in the words or action.

Add to this list whenever a new tic surfaces per manuscript. Keep it living.

6b. AI-tell pattern scan

"It's not X, it's Y" negation structure — the single most prominent AI sentence tell. "This isn't just about efficiency. It's about transformation." Cut entirely.
Self-posed questions answered immediately — "The best part? It's this." / "The kicker? It's that." AI asks questions nobody was asking, then answers them. Don't.
Anaphora abuse — same sentence opener three or more times in a row: "They believe that X... They believe that Y... They believe that Z..." Break the pattern.
Present participle trailing clauses — shallow analysis tacked onto sentence endings: "contributing to the region's rich cultural heritage" / "highlighting its importance." Cut.
Hedge-stacking — "It seems that it might perhaps be possible that..." One hedge maximum. Zero is better.
Throat-clearing openers — "In today's world..." / "Now more than ever..." / "When it comes to..." Cut the opener entirely, start with the actual point.
Em dash overuse — Sylvia bans em dashes. Use a comma + conjunction, split into two sentences, use parentheses for asides, or rewrite. Flag every em dash in the manuscript.
Past-perfect retrospection clusters — I had been waiting. I had been told. I had not believed. Three or more in close proximity: restructure to simple past.
Internal monologue that explains what the scene already showed — cut any paragraph where the narrator explains to the reader what just happened. Trust the scene.

6c. List formatting (no Oxford comma)
Sylvia does not use Oxford commas. Sentence-stack and and-stack patterns are also banned for enumerative lists.

❌ Oxford comma: I sat, stood, and walked.
❌ Sentence-stack: I sat. I stood. I walked.
❌ And-stack: I sat and stood and walked.
✓ Comma list, no serial comma: I sat, stood and walked.

Applies to enumerative lists — items in the same category being inventoried (actions, objects, qualities). Examples:

I sat. I stood. I sat again. → I sat, stood and sat again.
Jeans. Two shirts. The sweater she'd left. → Jeans, two shirts and the sweater she'd left.
He was tired and slow and quiet. → He was tired, slow and quiet.

Does NOT apply to sequential narrative beats with distinct content. I made coffee I didn't drink. I checked the door. I looked at my phone. — these are three different actions with different objects and context; they are narrative beats, not a list.
Test: if the items are categorically the same (all actions in a row, all qualities, all objects), it's a list — apply the comma rule, no serial comma. If each sentence carries distinct context and object, it's narrative — leave as separate sentences.

6d. Repeated words / lexical echoes
Scan for the same noun, verb or adjective appearing multiple times in close proximity.

Verb echoes: He turned. She turned. They turned. — clustering the same verb within a scene.
Filler verbs: was / had / went clustered across multiple consecutive sentences.
Adjective echoes: the same descriptive word applied to different things within a paragraph.
Character-name echoes: using a character's name three or more times in a short passage where a pronoun would serve.

Flag any content word repeated 3+ times within 300 words. Decide: keep (earned anaphora) or vary.

6e. Repeated sentence patterns
Same structural shape used back-to-back.

Subject-verb-object stack: He turned. She nodded. He left. She stayed. — uniform short SVO across multiple sentences. Vary with one longer carrying-sentence or invert clause order.
Em-dash mid-clause — banned entirely for Sylvia. Every em dash is a flag (see 6b).
Trailing-clause stack: She arrived, her coat still wet. He left, his coffee untouched. Multiple sentences using the same ", [noun phrase]" tail pattern.
Anaphora unintentional: He said. He turned. He left. (or I said. I turned. I left.) when not earned.

Test: read three consecutive sentences aloud. Do they have the same shape? If yes, vary one.

6f. Sentence-starter variation
Rule: No back-to-back sentences starting with the same word. Even two in a row is a flag.
Common offenders:

I... I... I... — first-person tic; the most common drafting failure.
The... The... The... — descriptive tic.
He... He... He... / She... She... She...
It was... It was... It was...
There was... There was...

Fix patterns:

Insert a connective: I made coffee I didn't drink. Then I checked the door.
Restructure to lead with the object: I looked at the phone on the table. → The phone sat on the table. I looked at it.
Compress into a comma-list when items are listable (see 6c): I sat. I stood. I sat again. → I sat, stood and sat again.
Lead with dependent clause: I waited. → Waiting at the window, I...
Lead with participle: I felt his shoulder. → Feeling his shoulder against mine, I...
Lead with dialogue: "You're late," she said.

Test: scan every chapter for any two consecutive sentences with the same first word. Every hit gets a fix.

6g. Voice consistency
Sylvia's voice: James Patterson short-chapter style. Dialogue carries the weight, not prose. Short paragraphs — no more than 5 lines. Varied sentence length is non-negotiable: short punch. Longer explanation sentence. Short again. No more than two sentences of the same length in a row.

POV holds throughout (no head-hopping in close-third or first).
Tense holds (no slipping between past and historical present).
Clean writing — no profanity across all pen names.
Chapter openings: start in the middle of the action or thought. No wind-up, no establishing shots.
Chapter closings: every chapter ends pointing forward — a question, a revelation, a decision, a hook.

6h. Burstiness check (sentence length variation)
AI sentences cluster tightly around 15-20 words. Human writing swings from 3-word fragments to 40-word sprawls. Check any passage where three or more consecutive sentences are similar in length. Break the rhythm deliberately. Mix punchy and flowing. One-sentence paragraphs are a tool — use them at beat-markers.

6i. Fragment density
Fragments are allowed when they serve rhythm, not as filler. Three or more fragments in a row should land at a beat-marker (climax, decision, revelation). Fragment-clusters at random points feel patterned. Audit any run of 3+ fragments and confirm each earns its place.

Pass 7 — Continuity Read-Through
Purpose: Catch what only a continuous read can catch.
Scan for:

Names spelled consistently throughout.
Ages, dates, timeline math (if she arrived in October, the first frost in December is two months — does the narrative match?).
Object continuity — if a character picks something up in Ch 5, it should be accounted for when it matters in Ch 12.
Wound continuity — if a character is injured in Ch 8, is that injury tracked correctly through subsequent chapters?
Setting continuity — was the kitchen described as facing east in Ch 1? Then it can't face west in Ch 30.
Character knowledge — does character X know fact Y at this point in the timeline?

Read in one sitting if possible. Mark every flag. Fix in a single pass at the end.

Pass 8 — Final Front and Back Matter
Purpose: Build the complete book.
Front matter:

Title page
Copyright page (year, author, publisher imprint, ISBN if owned)
Dedication
Content warnings (specific, not vague — e.g., "graphic sexual content, on-page violence, references to addiction")
Epigraph (if used)
Pronunciation guide / glossary (if needed for series)

Back matter:

Author's Note (relationship to themes, content sensitivity context, any dedication of the work to real events or people)
Pen name engagement section (optional, varies by pen name — e.g., book recommendations, playlist, themed list; confirm with Sylvia whether this pen name uses one)
Newsletter / reader community CTA (Kit signup link, reader magnet offer, or community mention — confirm which CTA is active for this pen name at time of publication)
Also By section (other books, ordered by series; include all active titles under this pen name)
Acknowledgments
About the Author (pen name bio — confirm current version is up to date)
Next book teaser (cover image, excerpt, or pre-order link if available)


Pass 9 — Final Assembly
Purpose: Ship-ready manuscript.

Build DOCX from working source. Preserve italics, scene breaks, drop caps if used.
Verify chapter numbering is continuous and titles match the table of contents.
Verify scene break formatting is consistent (* * * vs # vs blank).
Run final word count for genre target.
Save versioned final (e.g., BookTitle_PenName_FINAL_v1.docx).
Generate change log if working with editor / beta readers.
