# Outline Adherence & Anti-Spoiler System

This document defines the rules that prevent the AI from jumping ahead, revealing the killer too early, or writing scenes that only make sense if you already know the ending. These rules are NON-NEGOTIABLE and apply to every single chapter, every single paragraph, every single sentence.

## Why This Matters

The AI "knows" who the killer is from the moment the outline is created. This creates a fundamental problem: the AI writes backward from the answer instead of forward from the mystery. Left unchecked, this produces manuscripts where:

- The killer gets more suspicious descriptions than other suspects
- The sleuth "notices" things about the killer that she has no reason to notice yet
- Red herrings feel half-hearted because the AI knows they're wrong
- The reveal feels flat because the reader saw it coming from Chapter 3
- Descriptions of the killer use loaded language ("something about his smile didn't reach his eyes") that telegraph guilt
- The killer's scenes have a different narrative energy — more tension, more careful word choices — than other suspects' scenes

A well-crafted cozy mystery makes the reader genuinely uncertain who did it until the reveal. That requires the AI to write each chapter as if it doesn't know the answer.

---

## The Chapter-Locked Knowledge State

Before writing each chapter, construct a **Knowledge State** — a list of exactly what the sleuth knows and doesn't know at this point in the story.

### How to Build the Knowledge State

For each chapter, answer these questions before writing a single word:

1. **What facts has the sleuth confirmed?** (not suspected — confirmed through direct observation, testimony, or evidence)
2. **What suspects has she identified and what does she know about each one's motive, means, and opportunity?**
3. **What clues has she found?** (list them — only clues that have appeared on-page in previous chapters)
4. **What red herrings has she encountered?** (and does she know they're red herrings yet?)
5. **What doesn't she know yet?** (this is the most important list — these things CANNOT influence the prose)
6. **What is she wrong about?** (any false conclusions she's drawn that haven't been corrected yet)

### The Iron Rule

**The prose in Chapter N must be written as if Chapters N+1 through the end DO NOT EXIST.**

The sleuth cannot notice, wonder about, feel uneasy about, or pay special attention to anything that only matters because of what happens later. She notices things because they're unusual, interesting, or relevant to what she knows NOW — not because they'll matter in Chapter 12.

---

## Equal Suspect Treatment Protocol

The killer must be written with EXACTLY the same narrative approach as every other suspect. This means:

### Page Time
Track approximate word count devoted to each suspect per chapter. No suspect should consistently get more or less attention than others unless the outline specifically calls for it (e.g., "Chapter 7: Focus on Suspect B as primary person of interest").

### Description Language
When the sleuth meets or interacts with each suspect, the descriptions must use the same REGISTER of language. If the sleuth notices the florist's "warm smile and flour-dusted apron," she must notice similarly neutral-to-positive details about the killer. If the sleuth notices the accountant's "tight grip on his briefcase," she must notice similarly ambiguous details about the innocent suspects too.

**BAD (telegraphs guilt):**
- Innocent suspect: "Margaret handed me a cup of tea with a warm smile."
- Killer: "David's smile didn't quite reach his eyes as he offered me coffee."

**GOOD (equal treatment):**
- Innocent suspect: "Margaret handed me a cup of tea, though I noticed her hands shook slightly."
- Killer: "David offered me coffee with the easy confidence of someone used to hosting."

### Emotional Reactions
The sleuth's gut feelings must NOT consistently point at the killer. If the sleuth feels "something off" about the killer in Chapter 4, she must feel "something off" about at least one innocent suspect in a nearby chapter. Distribute unease equally.

### Alibi Presentation
Every suspect's alibi should feel equally solid AND equally breakable. If the killer's alibi has a visible crack, at least two innocent suspects' alibis must also have visible cracks. The reader should not be able to eliminate suspects by alibi strength alone until the outline says so.

## The Backward Writing Test

After writing each chapter, apply this test:

**"If I didn't know who the killer was, would I have written this chapter differently?"**

If the answer is yes — if ANY phrase, description, observation, or narrative choice was influenced by knowledge of the ending — rewrite those sections.

### Common Backward Writing Failures

| What the AI does | Why it's backward writing | What to do instead |
|---|---|---|
| Describes the killer with subtly negative language | The AI knows they're guilty and unconsciously signals it | Use neutral or positive language — the killer is hiding in plain sight |
| Has the sleuth linger on a detail about the killer | The AI knows this detail matters later | The sleuth should notice it the same way she notices a dozen other things — in passing, without emphasis |
| Writes the killer's dialogue with hidden double meanings | The AI is being clever because it knows the truth | Write dialogue that is genuinely normal — no dramatic irony until the outline calls for it |
| Gives the killer a scene where they "almost slip up" too early | The AI wants to create tension but it's premature | Save near-slips for when the outline says suspicion should start shifting |
| Has other characters act slightly different around the killer | The AI projects collective unconscious guilt | Other characters treat the killer normally — they don't know either |
| Describes a location the killer uses later with ominous language | The AI is pre-loading atmosphere for a future scene | Describe all locations with the same level of detail and the same tonal register |

## Clue Planting Discipline

### The Outline Is the Schedule

The outline specifies when each clue appears. This schedule is absolute:

- **No early drops.** If Clue #4 is scheduled for Chapter 9, it does not appear as a "subtle background detail" in Chapter 5. The reader should not have access to information before the outline says they should.
- **No late drops.** If Clue #2 is scheduled for Chapter 4, it must appear in Chapter 4 — not pushed to Chapter 6 because the AI got absorbed in a character scene.
- **No extra clues.** Do not invent additional clues beyond what the outline specifies. Extra clues dilute the puzzle and risk making the solution too obvious.
- **No missing clues.** Every clue in the outline must appear on-page. If a clue is needed for the reveal, it MUST exist in the text before the reveal chapter.

### How to Plant Clues Without Spotlighting Them

The best clues are hidden in plain sight — embedded in normal scenes where the reader's attention is elsewhere.

**Techniques:**
- **The sandwich method**: Place the clue between two more interesting or dramatic things. The reader's attention goes to the drama; the clue sits quietly in the middle.
- **The character moment**: Embed the clue in a scene that's emotionally about something else. The sleuth is having a heart-to-heart with her best friend and happens to notice that the clock on the mantle is wrong.
- **The humor cover**: Drop the clue during a funny moment. The reader is laughing and doesn't register the significance.
- **The sensory embed**: Bury the clue in sensory description. "The kitchen smelled like burnt sugar and something sharper — bleach, maybe" where the bleach is the clue but the burnt sugar is what the reader remembers.
- **The familiar distraction**: The talking animal familiar draws attention away from the clue by doing something funny or demanding at the same moment.

### Clue Verification Checklist

After writing any chapter that contains a clue:
1. Is the clue present in the text? (not just implied — actually stated or shown)
2. Is it buried enough that a first-time reader won't flag it as Important?
3. On a re-read, would a reader say "oh, THAT'S what that was about"?
4. Does the sleuth react to the clue with the right level of attention for her current knowledge state?
5. Is the clue accurate? (it must point to the actual killer — no misleading clues that break fair-play rules)

## Red Herring Integrity

Red herrings are not throwaway misdirection — they are complete alternative theories that the reader should genuinely believe could be the answer. The AI must write each red herring with FULL CONVICTION.

### Writing Red Herrings as If They're Real

When writing a red herring scene, the AI must pretend — fully, completely — that THIS suspect is the real killer. Write their suspicious behavior with the same care and specificity you'd give the real killer's reveal. The motive should be compelling. The evidence should be damning. The alibi crack should feel significant.

**The test:** If you pulled out just the red herring scenes and gave them to a reader, that reader should believe this person did it.

### Red Herring Resolution

When a red herring is debunked, it must be debunked for a SPECIFIC, SATISFYING reason — not just "turns out they had an alibi after all." The false suspect's suspicious behavior must have a real, interesting explanation that makes the reader feel clever for having been fooled and satisfied by the answer.

## The Final Audit

After the manuscript is complete and before the editorial pipeline, run a dedicated Outline Adherence Audit:

### Audit Steps

1. **Knowledge State Verification**: For each chapter, verify that no information from future chapters leaked into the prose. Flag any instance where the sleuth knows something she shouldn't know yet.

2. **Suspect Treatment Scan**: Compare the descriptive language used for the killer vs each innocent suspect. Flag any pattern where the killer consistently gets more loaded, suspicious, or ominous description.

3. **Clue Timing Check**: Verify every clue appears in the chapter the outline specifies — no earlier, no later, no extras, no missing.

4. **Red Herring Quality Check**: Verify each red herring is written with full conviction and has a satisfying debunk. Flag any red herring that feels half-hearted.

5. **Backward Writing Scan**: Read the manuscript looking specifically for phrases, descriptions, or scenes that only make sense if you know the ending. Flag and rewrite.

6. **Reveal Integrity Check**: Verify the reveal chapter uses ONLY information already on-page. No new clues, no new evidence, no convenient confessions. The sleuth connects existing dots.

Any flagged issues must be rewritten before proceeding to the editorial pipeline.
