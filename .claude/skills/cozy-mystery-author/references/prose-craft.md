# Prose Craft Rules for Cozy Mystery

These rules apply during WRITING, not just revision. Every chapter must meet these standards from the first draft.

---

## Rule 1: Emotions Are Physical, Not Named

Never write "I feel anxious" or "fear courses through me." Show what the emotion does to the body.

**BAD:** "I felt a surge of fear when I saw the broken window."
**GOOD:** "The broken window stopped me mid-step. My hand found the doorframe and gripped it hard enough to feel the paint crack under my nails."

**BAD:** "I was attracted to him but tried to hide it."
**GOOD:** "He reached past me for the coffee pot and I forgot what I was saying. I turned toward the sink like there was something fascinating in it."

In a cozy, emotional showing is gentler than in dark fiction but still specific: sweaty palms wrapped around a coffee mug, a stomach that won't unclench even with the best scone in town, the familiar pressing closer because it senses something's wrong.

**Self-check:** If you can delete an emotion sentence and the reader still knows the feeling from surrounding action and dialogue, that sentence was telling. Delete it.

---

## Rule 2: World-Building Through Daily Life, Not Explanation

Readers learn the town by living in it alongside the sleuth. Don't explain — demonstrate.

**BAD:** "The bakery was known for its enchanted pastries that triggered memories in whoever ate them."
**GOOD:** "I bit into the blueberry muffin and suddenly I was six years old, sitting on my grandmother's kitchen counter, my legs swinging, flour on my nose."

**BAD:** "Familiars chose their person through a mystical bonding ritual that happened at the age of —"
**GOOD:** "The hedgehog was on my porch again. Third morning in a row. He looked up at me with an expression that clearly communicated: 'I live here now. Adjust your expectations.'"

**Self-check:** If a character explains something both characters already know, it's info-dumping. If one character genuinely doesn't know, the explanation comes through experience or demonstration, not a patient lecture.

---

## Rule 3: Theme Lives in Action, Not Dialogue

Characters never say the theme. No "maybe starting over isn't about forgetting where you've been" or "this town has a way of giving you exactly what you need." Theme emerges from what characters DO.

**BAD:** "I think I'm finally learning to trust again," I told her.
**GOOD:** I handed her the spare key without thinking about it. Halfway home I realized what I'd done and didn't go back to ask for it.

**Self-check:** Search dialogue for abstract nouns (belonging, trust, second chances, home, starting over). If characters discuss these concepts directly, rewrite so they're living the concept through concrete action.

---

## Rule 4: Mystery Clues Through Observation, Not Announcement

The sleuth notices things — a scent that's wrong, a detail that doesn't match, a reaction that's too quick. She does NOT announce "that's suspicious!" She files it away, and the reader files it too.

**BAD:** "I noticed his shoes were muddy, which was strange since he said he'd been home all morning. That seemed suspicious."
**GOOD:** "His shoes tracked mud across the bakery floor. Tina frowned and reached for the mop. He'd said he was home all morning."

The observation sits there. The sleuth doesn't editorialize. The reader can draw their own conclusion — or miss it entirely until the reveal, which is exactly what you want.

---

## Rule 5: Scenes Move Through Action, Not Reflection

Open in the middle of something happening. No waking-up-and-thinking openings (unless the familiar is doing something ridiculous that pulls her out of bed). No paragraphs of recap.

**BAD:** "I woke up thinking about what Margaret had said yesterday. The more I thought about it, the more I realized she might be right. I decided to go talk to David about it."
**GOOD:** "David's shop door was locked at 10 AM on a Tuesday, which was wrong. I pressed my face against the glass and cupped my hands around my eyes."

**Self-check:** If the first paragraph of any chapter is the character thinking about the previous chapter, cut it and start where the action begins.

---

## Rule 6: Dialogue Is Behavior, Not Information

Characters talk to get something — answers, comfort, a reaction, a laugh. They interrupt, deflect, change the subject. They never patiently explain plot points to each other.

**BAD:**
"So the victim was found at 6 AM by the mailman," she said.
"Yes, and the police estimate time of death between midnight and 3 AM," I replied.
"That means anyone who was at the fundraiser until 11 PM could have done it," she concluded.

**GOOD:**
"The mailman found her." She said it like she was testing how the words tasted.
"At six in the morning."
"He must have —"
"He screamed. Linda heard him three houses down."
She set her coffee cup down very carefully. "I was going to ask if he called 911 first."

---

## Rule 7: Cozy Comfort Is Woven In, Not Bolted On

Food, drinks, weather, cozy spaces, and sensory warmth are genre-contract essentials in cozy mystery. But they must be integrated into the scene, not dropped in as decoration.

**BAD:** "I sipped my chamomile tea. It was warm and comforting. The rain pattered against the window."
**GOOD:** "The tea had gone cold while I was reading the coroner's notes. I wrapped my hands around the mug anyway. Outside, rain turned the garden into a watercolor version of itself."

The comfort elements serve double duty — they ground the reader in the cozy world AND reflect the sleuth's emotional state or advance the scene.

---

## Rule 8: The Familiar Is a Character, Not Comic Relief

If the story has a talking animal familiar, it must be a full character with consistent personality, its own agenda, and genuine animal behavior.

- The familiar has opinions that don't always align with the sleuth's priorities
- The familiar communicates important mystery information in unhelpful, animal-filtered ways
- The familiar still ACTS like its species (a hedgehog curls up when startled, a ferret steals shiny things, a cat ignores you when you need them most)
- The familiar's humor comes from the clash between animal perspective and human concerns — not from being a joke delivery system

---

## Dialogue Voice Differentiation

Every named character should be identifiable by their speech patterns alone:

- **Sentence length**: Some characters speak in short bursts. Others meander.
- **Vocabulary level**: The professor uses different words than the baker.
- **Verbal tics**: One character always starts with "Listen." Another says "the thing is" before every opinion.
- **What they avoid saying**: Some characters never curse. Some never say "I love you." Some never admit they're wrong. What a character WON'T say reveals as much as what they will.
- **How they handle conflict**: Deflectors, confronters, joke-makers, subject-changers, silent-treatment givers.

Test: Cover the dialogue tags. Can you tell who's speaking? If not, the voices aren't distinct enough.
