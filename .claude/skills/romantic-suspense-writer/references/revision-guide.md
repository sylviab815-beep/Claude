# Revision Guide — Romantic Suspense

The complete editorial checklist for the manuscript revision pipeline. Read the whole guide before starting any revision pass.

## Section 0: Dual-Track Genre Compliance (DO THIS FIRST)

Before touching anything else, verify the manuscript is delivering romantic suspense — not romance with a dangerous backdrop, and not a thriller with a romantic subplot.

### Is the dual-track mandate being met?

Read through every chapter and apply the dual-track test: **Does this chapter advance both the romance arc and the suspense arc?**

**Red flags for romance track failure:**
- Chapters where the love interest doesn't appear and isn't meaningfully on the protagonist's mind
- External threat taking over the story for 3+ chapters with romance completely absent
- The climax resolving the danger without resolving the relationship
- The book summarizable as "protagonist solves a crime" rather than "two people fall in love while in danger"

**Red flags for suspense track failure:**
- Chapters where the external threat is entirely absent — pure romance without danger undercurrent
- The antagonist disappearing from the story for long stretches
- The danger feeling static — the same level of threat, not escalating
- "Safe house chapters" where nothing threatens anyone

**The fix:** For any chapter that's failing one track, identify what single addition would bring the other track in. If it's a pure action chapter, add a moment that changes the relationship dynamic — even briefly, even subtly. If it's a pure romance chapter, let the threat cast a shadow — a sound, a reminder, a piece of evidence that surfaces at the worst moment.

### Does the black moment break BOTH tracks?

The single most common structural failure in AI-written romantic suspense is a black moment that addresses only the romance (the couple separates for romantic reasons) while the external threat is handled separately.

**Correct**: The revelation that drives the couple apart is connected to the threat. The worst romantic moment and the maximum danger moment are the same moment.

**Incorrect**: The couple has a fight about a misunderstanding in Chapter 28, then separately in Chapter 30 there's a dangerous confrontation with the antagonist.

If the black moment and the external climax are structurally separate, the book needs a developmental revision to braid them.

### Does the HEA come after the external resolution?

The HEA cannot be the climax. The external threat resolves first; then the romantic resolution follows. If the last chapter is the confrontation with the antagonist and the HEA is a paragraph at the end, that's insufficient — the HEA needs space.

## Section 1: Suspense Track Revision

### Information Control Audit

For each chapter, verify the information release matches the outline:
- No clue appears before its planted chapter
- No revelation appears before its reveal chapter
- No description loads the antagonist with suspicious qualities before the reveal
- Active misdirection is maintained — the reader believes the red herring because the evidence supports it

### Tension Escalation Check

Chart tension 1-10 per chapter. Verify:
- The baseline is rising (even accounting for relief-valve dips)
- No extended flat sections (same tension level for 4+ consecutive chapters)
- The mid-book section (chapters 12-20 in a 30-chapter novel) has its own escalation
- Relief valve chapters plant a seed before they let the reader breathe

### Protagonist Competence Check

Verify the protagonist:
- Draws at least two wrong conclusions before the right one
- Pursues false leads for multiple chapters (not resolved in a paragraph)
- Has a blind spot that costs them something in Act 2
- Earns the breakthrough through their specific background/expertise/character — not lucky coincidence

### Twist Reverse-Engineering Check

For each twist: identify the 3+ planted clues. If you can't find them on a re-read, they need to be added.

## Section 2: Romance Track Revision

### Romance Arc Beat Verification

Every beat must happen on-page. Verify:
- [ ] Alliance — they commit to working together despite reasons not to trust
- [ ] First Real Connection — a moment of genuine vulnerability, usually at maximum external pressure
- [ ] Deepening Stakes — the love interest becomes personally connected to the danger
- [ ] The Almost — an interrupted near-miss that's more charged than completion would have been
- [ ] Trust Break — evidence from the suspense arc that appears to implicate or undermine the love interest
- [ ] Black Moment — both tracks break simultaneously
- [ ] The Turn — protagonist acts on love/trust, which connects to resolving the external threat
- [ ] HEA — fully delivered, with time and space, after the danger is resolved

### Sweet/Clean Compliance — Julie Fontaine Standard (Heat Level 0.5, Christian)

**Hard rules:**
- No explicit sexual content of any kind
- No graphic or extended physical description of kissing or touching
- No fade-to-black that implies intimacy beyond kissing
- No morning-after scenes, no implied shared bedroom
- No "she wanted him" language that describes physical desire directly
- Paragraphs no longer than 5-6 lines

**What is permitted:**
- Kissing — but described briefly, not lingered on
- Physical awareness: proximity, heartbeat, warmth, the specific weight of a hand
- Full emotional intensity: the internal decision, the cost, the vulnerability, the fear of what this means
- Attraction expressed through charged awareness, subtext, and what is not said

**The Julie Fontaine kiss standard:**
When writing a kiss, keep the physical description brief. The scene's heat lives in the emotional response — what the kiss costs the character, what wall it breaks, what terrifying truth it confirms. Write the before (the building toward) at length. Write the after (the aftermath, the recognition) at length. Write the kiss itself in one or two sentences, maximum.

**The intensity rule**: Christian-standard clean does not mean emotionally flat. The romantic tension should be at full emotional intensity. What is reduced is explicitness and physical description. The reader should feel the weight of the moment through the character's internal experience, not through physical description.

**If any passage crosses into explicit or over-described territory:** Cut the physical description. Replace with the character's emotional experience — what this moment means, what it changes, what it costs. The reader's imagination handles the rest.

## Section 3: AI Artifact Removal

### AI Connector Words (delete wherever found)

| Word/Phrase | Action |
|---|---|
| Additionally | Delete |
| Furthermore | Delete |
| Moreover | Delete |
| It's worth noting that | Delete — just state the thing |
| However, it's important to remember | Cut to the actual point |
| Interestingly | Delete |
| Essentially / Fundamentally | Delete |
| Ultimately | Delete 90% of occurrences |
| Notably | Delete |

### Emotional Over-Labeling

AI names emotions instead of showing them through body and behavior.

**BAD**: "I felt a complex mixture of fear and attraction, tinged with something that might have been hope."

**GOOD**: "My chest was doing that thing it had started doing every time he looked at me too long, which was becoming a serious problem given that someone was trying to kill us."

The rule: the body, the behavior, the specific thought. Not the label. The reader should feel the emotion because the prose creates the sensation, not because they were told what to feel.

### Romantic Suspense-Specific AI Patterns

**The Danger-Reset Problem**: AI tends to introduce a threat, raise tension for 2 chapters, then neutralize the threat and start over. Fix: threats should be contained or escalated, never fully neutralized until the climax. Each resolved threat should leave the protagonist MORE exposed, not reset to baseline.

**The Uncoupled Tracks Problem**: AI defaults to alternating pure-romance chapters with pure-suspense chapters. Fix: every chapter braids both. See dual-track audit.

**The Too-Quick Breakthrough**: The protagonist figures out the key clue in a single "aha" scene. Fix: they should pursue at least two wrong theories first, with each one costing them time and trust.

**The Near-Miss That Doesn't Build**: AI writes almost-kisses that are emotionally flat — the buildup is described rather than felt, and the interruption doesn't land hard. Fix: write the near-miss at full intensity, let it build to the edge, then make the interruption feel like something has been taken from the reader (and the characters).

**The Sanitized Clean Tension**: AI defaults to "clean" meaning "emotionally neutral" — the romantic tension is absent because the AI is avoiding explicit content. Fix: the heat is still there, just expressed through different channels. See prose-craft rules.

## Section 4: General Prose Revision

### Chapter Endings

**Kill these patterns:**
- One-line dramatic closers that summarize what just happened
- Philosophical wrap-ups ("Maybe that's what trust really was...")
- Tidy emotional summaries where the chapter's feeling is labeled and packaged
- Manufactured cliffhangers that feel disconnected from the chapter's content

**Replace with:**
- End mid-action, mid-conversation, or mid-thought
- End on a question the character (and reader) hasn't answered
- End on a hook that pulls at least one track — ideally both
- End on an unresolved feeling, not a labeled one

### Dialogue

- Characters don't address each other by name constantly — remove most name use in direct address
- Every conversation should be doing double work: advancing the plot AND the relationship
- Banter doesn't ping-pong perfectly — one character can not have a comeback and that's fine
- When a character is lying or hiding something, their evasions should be specific (changing the subject, answering a different question, asking a question back)

### POV Discipline

The narrator can only know what they can perceive. Flag and fix:
- Describing their own facial expression
- Knowing what the other character is thinking with certainty ("He was definitely thinking about...")
- Omniscient observations about events happening elsewhere
- Describing themselves from the outside

## Section 5: Style Guide Compliance

After all other fixes, re-read the user's style guide and verify:
- Sentence lengths match their patterns
- Dialogue-to-narrative ratio matches
- Descriptive density matches
- Humor style matches (or is absent if they don't write humor)
- Vocabulary level matches
- Their distinctive voice markers are preserved

The final manuscript should sound like the user wrote it on a very good day — not like a different, "better" writer polished it.

## Final Checklist

**DUAL-TRACK COMPLIANCE:**
- [ ] Every chapter advances both romance and suspense tracks
- [ ] Black moment breaks both tracks simultaneously
- [ ] HEA delivered after external resolution with adequate space

**ROMANCE TRACK:**
- [ ] All romance arc beats present on-page
- [ ] Love interest present/on-mind in every chapter
- [ ] HEA fully satisfying

**SUSPENSE TRACK:**
- [ ] Information budget matches outline — no early reveals
- [ ] Tension ratchets upward
- [ ] Protagonist earns breakthroughs through character
- [ ] Twists reverse-engineerable (3+ clues per twist)
- [ ] Threat felt in every chapter

**SWEET/CLEAN (Christian standard, heat 0.5):**
- [ ] Zero explicit content
- [ ] Kiss scenes: brief physical description, full emotional depth
- [ ] Full romantic tension through emotional channels (not physical description)
- [ ] Near-miss moments written at full emotional intensity
- [ ] No "said" as primary dialogue attribution — action beats and body language instead

**PROSE:**
- [ ] All chapters fully written prose — no summaries or placeholders
- [ ] AI connector words removed
- [ ] Emotional over-labeling replaced with body/behavior/thought
- [ ] Chapter endings are hooks, not summaries
- [ ] Dialogue sounds like real people (messy, subtext-laden, not ping-pong)
- [ ] POV clean — no breaks

**VOICE:**
- [ ] Prose matches user's style guide throughout
- [ ] All names and places avoid the avoidance list
