# Prose Craft Rules for Romantic Suspense

These rules govern the writing itself — how to create the specific experience of romantic suspense on a sentence and scene level. Apply during writing, not just revision.

---

## Rule 1: Let Danger Do the Work That Explicit Content Would Do in Other Subgenres

In sweet/clean romantic suspense, the physical and emotional charge that other subgenres deliver through explicit intimacy is delivered through **danger**. This is not a limitation — it's the genre's superpower.

When two people are in genuine danger together, their bodies respond. Adrenaline, hyperawareness, the narrowing of attention to only what matters. This is the same physiological state as attraction. The genre exploits this overlap deliberately.

**The Julie Fontaine standard (heat level 0.5, Christian)**: Write the emotional awareness at full intensity — the internal surrender, the decision, the cost of allowing the moment. Physical description stays minimal, especially during kisses. The heartbeat, the proximity, the specific weight of a hand held too long — these are fine. What is not fine: lingering physical description of the kiss itself, hands-and-lips mechanics, or "deepening" language. The heat lives in the *before* (the building of what cannot be resisted) and the *after* (the terrifying recognition of what just happened), not in the act itself.

**BAD (flat, sanitized):**
> He grabbed her hand and pulled her to safety. She was glad he was there.

**GOOD (charged without explicit content):**
> His hand closed around hers before she'd finished the thought — warm, certain, like he'd already decided she wasn't going anywhere without him. She didn't let herself think about how much she wanted that to mean something else.

The second example has real heat. It's still clean. The difference is specificity and the character's interiority.

---

## Rule 2: Near-Misses Are More Powerful Than Arrivals

The interrupted moment — the almost-kiss stopped by gunfire, the hands held one breath too long before someone steps away, the declaration that doesn't quite get made before the situation changes — is the fundamental unit of romantic tension in this genre.

The interrupted moment works because:
- The reader's imagination finishes it (more powerful than writing it out)
- The interruption by danger is never arbitrary — it's proof that the external threat is always present
- It leaves the unresolved tension to carry forward, which drives the reader to the next chapter

After every almost, the reader should be slightly frustrated and desperate for the next chapter. That's correct. That's the engine.

**Write the almost in full.** Don't cut away before the moment crests — let it build to the edge, then let the interruption land hard.

---

## Rule 3: Danger Strips Defenses

People under threat lose their social performance. They say things they'd never say in a safe situation. They act on instinct. They stop lying about what they feel.

Use the external threat to do the emotional heavy lifting that would otherwise require 30 chapters of slow development. A character who would never admit they're scared says so when there's a gun involved. A character who would never ask for help takes it when the alternative is dying.

This is why romantic suspense can have faster relationship development than other romance subgenres — the situation forces honesty. The skill is making that honesty feel earned by the specifics of the danger, not by contrivance.

---

## Rule 4: Physical Awareness is Specific, Not General

Avoid generic physical attraction descriptions. The character shouldn't notice that someone is "handsome" or "beautiful" — they should notice specific things, and those specifics should reveal something about the character noticing them.

**Generic (AI default):**
> He was tall, dark-haired, and undeniably attractive.

**Specific (character-driven):**
> She noticed his hands first — how still they were. Most people couldn't hold that still in a situation like this. Either he'd done this before or he didn't care if he made it out.

The specific detail does three jobs: it describes him, it reveals what she notices (competence, stillness, threat assessment), and it advances the tension.

---

## Rule 5: Dialogue in Romantic Suspense Is Coded

In a dangerous situation, characters don't talk directly about their feelings — and they often can't talk directly about the threat either. This creates dialogue that operates on multiple levels simultaneously.

Everything is subtext. A conversation about the plan is also a conversation about trust. An argument about strategy is also an argument about who's in charge of this relationship. A moment of dry humor in a bad situation is a statement of attraction.

**BAD (everything stated directly):**
> "I can't stop thinking about you," he said. "Even with everything happening, I keep thinking about what you mean to me."
> "Me too," she admitted. "But we have to focus right now."

**GOOD (subtext does the work):**
> "You keep saving my life," she said.
> He was quiet for a moment. "Someone has to."
> "Is that what this is?"
> He didn't answer. Which was, she was starting to understand, its own kind of answer.

---

## Rule 6: The Setting Is Never Neutral

In romantic suspense, every location either threatens or shelters — and both states are temporary. The abandoned building that was shelter becomes a trap. The small town that seemed peaceful is where someone is watching.

When describing setting, layer:
- The surface: what the place looks like
- The threat layer: what danger exists here or what danger this place reminds the protagonist of
- The intimacy layer: how this specific place forces or enables closeness

A scene set in a safe house has different emotional texture than a scene in an exposed parking garage, but both should create some version of charged tension. No setting is just a backdrop.

---

## Rule 7: The Chapter Hook in Romantic Suspense Pulls Both Threads

End every chapter on a hook that pulls at least one track — ideally both.

**Suspense hook only (adequate, not ideal):**
> The text came at midnight: *I know where you are.*

**Romance hook only (adequate, not ideal):**
> She was almost asleep when she heard him say her name — just her name, nothing else. It was the way he said it that she'd be thinking about all night.

**Both tracks (best):**
> The door opened before she could knock.
> He'd been watching for her, she realized. In the dark, in a town where someone was trying to kill them both, he'd been watching for her specifically.
> She told herself that wasn't the part that made her heart do what it did.

---

## Rule 8: The Internal Monologue Is the Battleground

In romantic suspense, the protagonist's internal monologue should consistently show two competing impulses:
- The instinct toward the love interest (attraction, trust, the desire to let them in)
- The counter-instinct from the threat (self-preservation, suspicion, "I can't afford to feel this right now")

These two forces in direct conflict in the internal monologue create the specific anxiety that is romantic suspense. The reader feels both pulls because the protagonist feels both pulls.

The resolution of this conflict — the moment the protagonist chooses the love interest despite all the reasons not to — is the emotional climax of the romance arc.

---

## Rule 9: Pacing — Action and Intimacy Alternate, Never Separate

Romantic suspense chapters have a characteristic rhythm: pressure → release → pressure (higher). The "release" is usually an intimate or emotionally vulnerable moment. But the intimate moment is never fully safe — the threat is always in the background.

Short chapters work well in this genre because they allow the reader to toggle between track states quickly. The ideal is chapters that end mid-scene with either the threat escalating OR a romantic beat landing — never both fully resolved.

---

## Rule 10: The Antagonist's Shadow Must Fall on Love Scenes

Even in the most tender moment, the external threat should be perceptible. Not as an interruption — as an atmosphere. The reader should never fully relax into the romance because they know what's coming for these characters.

This doesn't mean referencing the threat explicitly in every tender scene. It means: write the scene so that the reader, even while feeling the warmth, also feels the precariousness. These people are allowing themselves to feel this in a situation where feeling this is dangerous. That awareness is always present.

The best way to achieve this: the protagonist is aware of what's at stake. They're not just falling in love. They're falling in love knowing it creates a vulnerability they can't afford.
