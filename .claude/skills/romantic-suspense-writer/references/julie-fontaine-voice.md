# Julie Fontaine Voice & Style Standards

This reference file defines the hard rules and voice characteristics that apply to all Julie Fontaine manuscripts. These rules are absolute and non-negotiable — they define the pen name.

---

## Hard Rules (No Exceptions)

**No profanity.**
No swearing, no substitute swearing (no "freaking" used as a stand-in for a profanity), no taking the Lord's name in vain. Characters express frustration, anger, and intensity through action, sharp dialogue, and subtext. Strong emotion does not require strong language.

**Clean romance — Christian standard (heat level 0.5).**
Physical affection does not go beyond kissing. No implied bedroom scenes. No fade-to-black that suggests intimacy beyond kissing. No morning-after scenes that imply a sexual encounter occurred. Romance is expressed through emotional vulnerability, sacrifice, trust, and commitment. Love scenes are built on conversation, small gestures, and the weight of what is not said.

**When writing kiss scenes specifically:** Lean into the emotional response, not the physical description of the kiss itself. The reader does not need to be walked through the mechanics of lips and hands — they need to feel what it costs the character to allow this moment. The heat in a kiss scene is in the *internal surrender*, the *decision to stop fighting it*, the recognition that everything has changed — not the physical act. Keep descriptions of the kiss brief. Expand the before and after: the moment of decision, the shattering of a wall, the terrifying vulnerability of having chosen someone.

**BAD (over-described kiss):**
> His lips found hers, warm and certain, and she melted into him, her hands gripping his jacket as the kiss deepened.

**GOOD (emotional response at center):**
> She let him. That was all. She stopped arguing with herself and let him, and it turned out that was the only thing she'd needed to decide — the rest happened as if it had always been inevitable. She would think about what it meant later. She would probably think about it for the rest of her life.

**Christian/Catholic elements are woven naturally — never preachy, never absent.**
Faith shows up in the texture of characters' lives: a character might pray under pressure, notice a church steeple on a skyline, reference a saint or a Scripture without quoting chapter and verse, cross themselves before danger, or draw strength from a quiet moment of faith. Faith is part of who they are, not what the book is about. Never forced as a plot device. Never absent from the world. For Julie Fontaine specifically, faith may be slightly more present than in other pen names — a protagonist wrestling with God's timing, a moment of prayer about gratitude or surrender, a church community that supports without smothering.

**No em dashes.**
Use commas, periods, ellipses, or sentence restructuring instead. This is absolute — no exceptions.

**Short chapters, Patterson style.**
Each chapter targets 1,800 to 2,500 words. Hard floor minimum is 1,500 words — do not end a chapter before reaching this count. The 1,800-word target is the aim; 1,500 is the absolute bottom. A chapter is a single scene or a single emotional beat. When the beat is done, the chapter is done — but if the word count is under 1,500, the beat is not done. Find the next layer: deepen the interiority, extend the aftermath, add a sensory beat, let a conversation breathe longer. End every chapter on a hook, a question, a reversal, or a gut punch.

**No head-hopping.**
Every chapter has a clear POV character established in the first paragraph. Stay locked in that character's perceptions, thoughts, and sensory experience for the entire chapter. The reader never knows something the POV character does not know.

**Paragraphs no longer than five to six lines** unless absolutely necessary. White space is a pacing tool.

**Dialogue over prose.** Dialogue is the engine. Characters reveal themselves through what they say, how they say it, and what they refuse to say. Break up internal narration with dialogue, action, or sensory beats.

**Action beats over dialogue tags — not "said."** When lengthy dialogue needs to be broken up, use physical action or body language to identify the speaker, not "said." Reserve "said" for short exchanges or moments where absolute clarity requires it. Short back-and-forth dialogue does not need any attribution at all — the rhythm of the exchange carries it.

**BAD (over-reliance on "said"):**
> "I need you to trust me," he said. She looked away. "I'm not sure I can," she said. "You haven't given me a reason to," she said.

**GOOD (action beats do the work):**
> "I need you to trust me." He didn't move, didn't reach for her. Just waited.
> She looked away. Outside, a car passed and was gone.
> "You haven't given me a reason to."

Never exotic tags (exclaimed, intoned, hissed, breathed). Never adverbs on dialogue tags.

**Violence: minimal and psychological.**
When suspense is present, danger is psychological and relational rather than physical. A threatening phone call, a lie unraveling, a secret that could destroy a relationship. If physical danger occurs, it is brief, not graphic, and the focus is on the emotional aftermath, not the event itself.

## Voice Characteristics

**The Emotional Engine.**
Julie Fontaine's A-plot is always the protagonist's inner arc: identity, self-worth, healing, forgiveness, reinvention, letting go, or learning to trust again. The romance serves this arc. The suspense pressurizes it. Every external event pushes the protagonist toward or away from the internal truth she needs to face.

**Tone: Three Authors in One Voice.**
- *Debbie Macomber's warmth* — Worlds feel lived-in and safe even when hard things happen. Community matters. Friendship matters. Small kindnesses carry weight. The reader trusts the story will take her somewhere hopeful.
- *Katherine Center's emotional precision* — Every feeling is earned and specific. "She was sad" becomes "She felt the particular loneliness of standing in a room full of people who all seemed to know something she didn't." Never settle for a generic emotion when a precise one exists.
- *JoJo Moyes's courage to hurt* — Don't flinch from the hard parts. Characters lose things, grieve, make mistakes with real consequences. The resolution feels earned because the reader walked through the valley, not around it.

**Pacing: Slower and More Reflective.**
The prose breathes. A chapter can live inside a single conversation, a walk on the beach, a memory triggered by a song. But "slower" does not mean "static" — every chapter moves the protagonist's arc forward. The pacing is the rhythm of a woman thinking her way through something difficult.

**Interiority: More Room Than Other Pen Names.**
The protagonist's thoughts, memories, and emotional processing get more space on the page. But the same rules apply: show over tell, sensory over abstract, brief and sharp over lengthy meditation. Internal moments should feel like eavesdropping on someone's real thoughts, not reading a therapy journal.

**Cinematic and Sensory.**
Ground every scene in concrete sensory detail: what the POV character sees, hears, smells, touches, tastes. Establish the physical environment in the first few lines of every chapter. Use specific, concrete nouns over abstract ones. Sensory grounding continues throughout the scene, not just the opening.

**Setting as Emotional Landscape.**
The setting mirrors and amplifies the protagonist's inner state. The ocean is freedom, or grief, or the vastness of starting over. A small-town bakery is belonging, or the weight of expectation, or the comfort of doing one thing well. Ground every scene in the physical world, but let the setting do emotional work.

**The 50/50 Rule for Romantic Suspense.**
Maintain a roughly equal balance between the developing romance and the external suspense plot. Neither dominates for more than two consecutive chapters. They feel intertwined: progress in the relationship creates vulnerability in the mission, and pressure from the mission forces emotional breakthroughs in the relationship.

**Banter is Currency.**
Witty, sharp, character-specific banter is the lifeblood of the romantic dynamic. It is how guarded characters test each other, push each other, and eventually let each other in. Banter should evolve across the book: what starts as defensive sparring gradually softens into the private language of two people who know each other.

**Friendship and Ensemble.**
Women's fiction lives in relationships beyond the romance. Female friendships, mother-daughter dynamics, sister bonds, chosen family. These relationships should be as textured and evolving as the romance. A best friend who tells the protagonist the truth she doesn't want to hear is as important as the love interest.

**The Women's Fiction Contract.**
The ending is hopeful and earned, but not necessarily a fairy tale. The protagonist has changed. She sees herself differently. She has faced the hard thing and come through it. The world is not perfect, but she is stronger, and the reader feels that strength. Bittersweet is allowed. Hollow is not.

## Character Voice Rules

**Each POV character must sound distinct.** The reader identifies whose chapter it is within the first few lines based on voice alone, before the POV is named.

Voice is shaped by:
- **What they notice** — filter every observation through the character's expertise and personality
- **How they think** — sentence rhythm, vocabulary, and internal metaphor should match the character
- **What they avoid** — what a character refuses to think about or talk about reveals as much as what they say

**Show, don't tell, especially emotion.** Do not write "She was afraid." Write what afraid looks like in her body, her breath, her grip on the steering wheel. Emotion lives in the physical. Internal thoughts should be brief and sharp, not lengthy meditations.

**Tight, active prose.** Favor active voice. Favor strong, specific verbs over adverbs. Cut filler words: "just," "really," "very," "quite," "seemed to," "began to," "started to." If a character does something, they do it.

**Sentence variety.** Mix short punchy sentences with longer, rhythmic ones. Use fragments for impact. Use a single-sentence paragraph to land a moment. Patterson's style: hit, hit, breathe, hit.

---

## Technical Continuity Rules

- Always reference the codex and outline for each chapter's specific beats, POV character, tone, goal, complication, and character decision. Do not deviate from outline structural beats without being asked.
- Maintain continuity: track character locations, injuries, objects in hand, time of day, and emotional state from the previous chapter.
- If a character is undercover or using an alias, use the cover name in dialogue and the real name in internal narration (unless the POV character would not know the real name).
- Write the full chapter — no summaries, no partial drafts, no placeholders.
