## Step 1: Setup Questions

Use AskUserQuestion for each:

- Novella (~15-20 chapters, ~20,000-35,000 words)

**Heat Level**:
- 0 — Inspirational/Christian (nothing beyond a kiss, faith elements may be present)
- 1 — Sweet/Clean (closed door, warm and modest)
- 2 — Fade to black (romantic tension, scene cuts away)

**Series Position**:

## Step 3: Generate 5 Story Ideas

Generate 5 unique, compelling rom-com premises. Each must:

- Feature a **central comedic premise** — the situation that generates natural comedy throughout the whole book (not just a funny meet-cute)
- Include **at least 3 romance tropes** drawn from market performance or K-Lytics data
- Have a **built-in misunderstanding** or **comedic conflict** that escalates over the middle of the book
- Feature casting that reflects the diversity guidance from Step 1
- Include a **unique, marketable title** — avoid all overused title words from the avoidance list
- Include a 2-3 sentence premise
- List all romance tropes used
- Note the **core comedic engine** (what makes this situation inherently funny?)

**Strong rom-com comedic engines to draw from:**
- Mistaken identity or false impression that snowballs
- Enemies forced to cooperate (work, family, shared space)
- Fake relationship that gets real feelings involved
- Competitive situation where attraction is inconvenient
- The protagonist is spectacularly bad at something the love interest is great at
- Two people with completely opposing life philosophies stuck together
- A secret that would ruin everything if revealed
- Someone trying to get over their ex runs straight into their next person
- "Later in life" — a fresh start, a second chance, or a woman reinventing herself
- Found family dynamics where romance complicates (or completes) belonging

**Diversity in casting**: When generating names and backgrounds, draw from the full range of cultural identities. Don't default to generic names. Reference the AI Names avoidance list for names to skip.

**CRITICAL**: No names, places, or title words from the avoidance list.

**Present all 5 ideas and ask: "Which story idea speaks to you most?"** They may ask for modifications or a fresh set.

## Step 4: Hook and Pitch

Once the user selects a story idea, generate:

- **Hook**: A single sentence capturing the comedic AND romantic premise. It should be funny and make the reader want to know more.
- **Pitch**: 150-250 words weaving in all the tropes, establishing both leads, the comedic central situation, the emotional stakes, and why the HEA will feel earned. The pitch should sound like the back cover of a book a reader would laugh at in the store and immediately buy.

Present and wait for feedback. Revise if asked.

## Step 5: H1 Character Bio

Generate a detailed bio for H1 using the Novelcrafter format from SKILL.md.

**Critical for rom-com**: The **Comedic Role** field is essential. What makes H1 funny? It should be rooted in their character flaw or wound — the thing that makes them endearing AND gets them into trouble. The best rom-com protagonists are funny because they're too stubborn, too honest, too anxious, too competitive, too proud — not because they tell jokes.

**Internal Conflict** should connect directly to why the romance is complicated for them — and why that complication is inherently funny.

**CRITICAL naming rules**: Name must not appear on the avoidance list. Draw from diverse cultural backgrounds, historical names, or unusual combinations.

**Present the bio and ask for feedback.** Let them know they can add specific details.

---

## Step 6: H2 Character Bio

Generate a detailed bio for H2 using the same format.

H2 should create **maximum comedic friction** with H1 while also being obviously right for them. The chemistry should be visible from reading both bios — readers should both want them to get together AND fully understand why they're driving each other crazy.

**Same naming and diversity rules apply.**

**Present the bio and wait for feedback.**

## Step 7: Comedy Beat Map

This is unique to rom-com and replaces the standard trope scene mapping. For the chosen story, map out:

### The Central Comedic Engine
The situation or dynamic that generates comedy across the WHOLE book (not just one scene). Every chapter should be able to draw on this engine.

### Set Pieces (3-5 major comedy scenes)
These are the big, planned-out funny scenes that readers will tell their friends about. Each set piece should:
- Arise naturally from character and situation
- Escalate the romantic tension at the same time
- Be specific and visual (you should be able to see it)

Example: The disastrous dinner party, the accidental duet at karaoke, the wedding speech that goes wrong, the cooking lesson that turns into a food fight.

### Running Gags (2-3)
Small recurring comic elements that build across the book. Each appearance should be a little funnier and a little more meaningful than the last.

### The Misunderstanding Arc
Rom-com typically features one MAJOR misunderstanding (or a series of smaller ones escalating to a big one) that drives the dark moment. Map it out:
- How does it start?
- How does it build (what keeps them from clearing it up)?
- When does it fully explode?
- How is it resolved — and why does the resolution require more than just explaining?

### The Grovel
The grand gesture in rom-com is usually public, a little ridiculous, and emotionally naked. What form does it take in this story? What does H1 (or H2) have to risk or sacrifice to earn their way back?

### Trope Scene Checklist
For EACH confirmed trope:
- **Must-have scenes**: What does this trope require?
- **Key moments**: Emotional turning points specific to this trope
- **Comic potential**: How does this trope generate natural comedy for THIS story?

**Present the full comedy beat map and ask: "Does this feel like the right shape for your story?"**

## Step 8: Romance Arc

Plot out H1 and H2's complete romance arc, calibrated for rom-com pacing:

- **The Meet**: How they meet. First impressions. The comedic setup that puts them in each other's orbit.
- **The Forced Proximity / Reason to Stay**: Why they can't just walk away from each other.
- **The Banter Phase**: The chemistry building through disagreement, competition, or mutual frustration.
- **The First Crack**: When one of them sees something in the other that makes avoidance harder.
- **The Accidental Vulnerability**: The moment that shifts things from funny to genuinely tender.
- **The Midpoint Escalation**: Stakes raise — for the comedy AND the romance.
- **The Almost**: The moment they come closest before everything falls apart.
- **The Black Moment / Misunderstanding Explosion**: When the central misunderstanding or conflict fully detonates. Everything seems lost. Readers should feel it.
- **The Grovel and Grand Gesture**: How they find their way back.
- **The HEA/HFN**: What their future looks like. Should feel earned AND a little funny — this is a rom-com.

**Present the romance arc and ask for user input.**

---

## Step 9: Supporting Character Bios

Generate bios for all key supporting characters using the format in SKILL.md.

**Same naming and diversity rules apply.**

In rom-com, supporting characters often serve specific comedic functions:
- **The chaos agent** — the one whose well-meaning interference makes everything worse
- **The straight man** — the sensible friend who watches the protagonist's disasters with bewildered affection
- **The meddler** — usually a parent, sibling, or best friend who is enthusiastically unhelpful
- **The foil** — someone who represents what the protagonist could become if they don't grow

Don't write flat archetypes — give each supporting character specific personality, backstory, and purpose.

**Present all bios and ask for feedback.**

---

## Step 10: Settings List

Create a list of settings, each including:
- **Name/Location**
- **Description** (2-3 sentences, sensory and specific)
- **Scenes likely set here**
- **Comedic potential** (what about this setting creates natural comedy opportunities?)
- **Romantic resonance** (what about this setting could also be tender or meaningful?)

**CRITICAL**: No place names from the avoidance list. No "Maplewood", "Riverside", "Main Street Diner". Research real naming patterns. Settings should feel specific and real — not generic small-town.

**Present the settings list and wait for feedback.**

---

## Step 11: Detailed Chapter Outline

Before generating, ask:
- How many chapters (remind them of the format range from Step 1)?
- Any specific scenes or moments they definitely want in the book?

Generate the full chapter outline. **Every chapter must include ALL fields from the Chapter Outline Format in SKILL.md.**

Build around:
- The romance arc beats from Step 8
- The comedy beat map from Step 7 (set pieces, running gags, misunderstanding arc)
- The trope scene checklist
- The established characters and settings

**Comedy is non-negotiable in EVERY chapter.** Every chapter should have at least one genuine laugh — either a situation, a line, a running gag callback, or a character doing something perfectly in-character that's also perfectly funny. If a chapter is pure drama with no humor, it has drifted out of genre.

**Heat level compliance**: No content beyond the heat level specified in Step 1. For sweet/clean, the most physical contact is a lingering look, hand-holding, almost-kiss, or a first kiss. No fade-to-black scenes unless the user selected Heat Level 2.

**Do NOT include the epilogue in this step.**

**IMPORTANT**: If the outline is too long for one response, stop at a natural break point and offer a "continue" command.

**Once the full outline is presented, ask for feedback and revisions.**

---

## Step 12: Epilogue Ideas

Generate **at least 3** different epilogue ideas. Each should:
- Jump forward in time to show H1 and H2's life together
- Be warm, funny, and a little ridiculous — this is a rom-com epilogue
- Reference a running gag or set piece from the book for resonance
- Vary in approach: vary the time jump, the occasion, the POV

**Present the ideas and let the user pick, combine, or request something different.**

---

## Step 13: Final Delivery

Compile everything into a single .docx file:
- Selected story idea with hook and pitch
- H1 and H2 character bios
- Supporting character bios
- Comedy beat map
- Romance arc
- Settings list
- Full chapter outline
- Selected epilogue

Save as a .docx the user can download and use in Novelcrafter or their writing tool.
