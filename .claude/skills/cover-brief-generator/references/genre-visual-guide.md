# Genre Visual Guide — Book Cover Conventions by Genre

Current as of 2025. Trends move fast — always verify against live Amazon searches before finalizing a brief.

---

## Romance

### Contemporary Romance (Mainstream / Steamy)
**Palette:** Warm and saturated — corals, dusty pinks, warm blues, burnt orange, terracotta. High contrast is trending.
**Typography:** Bold sans-serif titles dominate. Centered. Large, confident type. Author name often as large as or larger than title.
**Imagery:** Couples — torso crops or over-shoulder shots. Faces sometimes visible, sometimes not. Outdoors and natural light trending over studio shots.
**Mood:** Playful, warm, inviting. Not dark. Not literary.
**Genre signal must-haves:** A couple (implied or explicit), warm color, contemporary clothing visible if figures shown.
**Red flags:** Dark backgrounds, script-only fonts, illustrated covers (reads as historical or indie).

### Sweet / Clean Romance
**Palette:** Softer, lighter versions of contemporary palettes — blush, sage, sky blue, warm cream, soft lavender.
**Typography:** Mix of script and serif. More decorative than steamy counterparts. Titles often centered with a script element.
**Imagery:** Often illustrated OR couples fully clothed from a distance or in embrace without exposed skin. Small-town settings, floral elements common.
**Mood:** Warm, cozy, wholesome. Safe.
**Genre signal must-haves:** Softer palette, non-revealing imagery. Illustrated or distance/silhouette figures.
**Red flags:** Dark/moody tones, exposed skin, urban grittiness — these signal the wrong heat level immediately.

### Inspirational / Christian Romance
**Palette:** Warm, pastoral, natural — cream, sage, wheat gold, soft blue. Earth tones. Never cold or dark.
**Typography:** Elegant serif or soft script. Traditional feel. Never edgy.
**Imagery:** Countryside, small towns, couples from a distance or in wholesome poses. Nature-forward. Churches, farmhouses, or cozy interiors as settings.
**Mood:** Hopeful, faith-touched, warm. The aesthetic should feel like it belongs in a faith bookstore and on Amazon.
**Genre signal must-haves:** Warm natural palette, pastoral or small-town setting, understated romance.
**Red flags:** City skylines, dark moody tones, cinematic thriller aesthetics.

### Romantic Suspense
**Palette:** Darker, more contrast-forward than straight romance. Navy, slate, charcoal with warm accent (gold, amber, rose gold). Teal is very current.
**Typography:** Mix — bold serif for suspense-forward titles, script elements for the romance component. The typography balance signals the heat ratio.
**Imagery:** Woman in danger / woman alone in atmospheric setting trending over couple shots. City at night. Silhouettes. Dramatic skies.
**Mood:** Tension + warmth coexisting. Darker than straight romance, warmer than pure thriller.
**Genre signal must-haves:** Atmospheric tension in the image, darker palette than pure romance, the feel of something at stake.
**Red flags:** Pure thriller palette (cold, grey, blood) — loses the romance signal. Pure romance palette (blush, floral) — loses the suspense signal.

**Clean/Sweet Romantic Suspense specifically (Julie Fontaine territory):**
Skews warmer than standard romantic suspense. Teal + gold, navy + warm cream, deep burgundy + soft light. Silhouettes or partial figures rather than full reveal. More literary-leaning than mass-market thriller.

### Historical Romance
**Palette:** Rich jewel tones — deep burgundy, forest green, midnight navy, antique gold. Or period-appropriate pastels for Regency.
**Typography:** Period-specific. Regency: script-forward. Victorian: ornate serif. Highland: bold, rustic. Author name smaller than title.
**Imagery:** Period clothing is mandatory. Couples in embrace showing costume detail. Regency: ballrooms, estates, gardens. Highland: Scotland, kilts.
**Mood:** Sweeping, romantic, period-specific.
**Genre signal must-haves:** Period clothing visible. Palette must feel historical — no modern-looking stock photos.

### Rom-Com / Romantic Comedy
**Palette:** Bright, fun, pop-art adjacent. Hot pink, chartreuse, cobalt, citrus yellow. High saturation. Not subtle.
**Typography:** Quirky, playful fonts. Often tilted, oversized, or with graphic elements integrated. Fun > elegant.
**Imagery:** Illustrated covers very common (especially trad pub). Photographic covers use bright backgrounds and fun poses. Objects/props representing the plot hook. Feet/legs (not faces) common.
**Mood:** Breezy, fun, self-aware. Should make you smile before you read the title.
**Genre signal must-haves:** Bright palette, playful typography, a sense of lightness and comedy.

## Mystery / Thriller

### Cozy Mystery
**Palette:** Warm, inviting, genre-specific to the theme. Bakery cozies: cream, brown, warm red. Cat cozies: cozy neutrals + cat colors. Small-town: warm naturals.
**Typography:** Warm serif or soft sans-serif. Often includes a pun-related font treatment. Approachable, not threatening.
**Imagery:** Almost universally illustrated. The illustration signals "cozy" before the reader reads a word. Objects related to the sleuth's hobby (cats, baked goods, yarn, gardens). Small-town settings. Cute animal companions.
**Mood:** Welcoming, a little whimsical, decidedly non-threatening. Death is present but not dwelling.
**Genre signal must-haves:** Illustrated cover (photography reads as thriller, not cozy). Warm palette. Thematic object or animal. Pun-friendly typography.
**Red flags:** Photography, dark moody palettes, no thematic elements visible — this reads as amateur thriller.

**Paranormal Cozy specifically:**
Adds purple, deep teal, starry backgrounds, magical elements (crystals, cauldrons, magical animals). Still warm and illustrated, just with supernatural visual cues.

### Psychological Thriller
**Palette:** Cold, desaturated, high-contrast. Slate grey, cold blue, white, black. Occasional red accent used sparingly for menace.
**Typography:** Clean, sharp, modern sans-serif or stark serif. Nothing decorative. Title placement: center or upper third.
**Imagery:** Single woman from behind, or face partially hidden. Houses, roads, empty spaces. Distorted or fragmented imagery. Water. Mirrors. The ordinary made threatening.
**Mood:** Unease, paranoia, dread. Everything looks slightly wrong.
**Genre signal must-haves:** Cold palette, a sense of menace in an ordinary setting, single figure alone.
**Red flags:** Warm colors, couples, illustrated style — all signal wrong genre.

### Domestic Thriller
Similar to psychological thriller but with domestic settings foregrounded. Suburban houses, kitchen windows, backyard fences — the home as the site of danger. Woman as both protagonist and potential victim. Still cold palette, clean typography.

### Crime / Detective Fiction
Broader palette than thriller — can include darker romance palettes. City streets, crime scene elements (tape, shadows, rain-slicked streets). More cinematic than thriller. Badge or weapon imagery common in police procedurals.

## Women's Fiction

**Palette:** Sophisticated, warm but not saccharine. Dusty rose, sage green, warm grey, butter yellow, terracotta. More nuanced than romance palettes.
**Typography:** Elegant serif or soft modern sans-serif. Title is usually the focus. Subtitle common (clarifies the emotional territory).
**Imagery:** Single woman, often from behind or at a distance. Women in meaningful settings (beaches, vineyards, gardens, doorways). Looking outward. A sense of journey or transformation.
**Mood:** Emotional depth, hope, a little melancholy, ultimately uplifting. Should feel like a book worth reading slowly.
**Genre signal must-haves:** Single woman (not couple), meaningful setting or landscape, sophisticated palette, the feeling of an inner journey.
**Red flags:** Couple = reads as romance. Too dark = reads as thriller. Too bright/pop = reads as chick lit (a distinct subgenre).

## General Cover Design Rules That Cross All Genres

**Thumbnail test:** The cover must be readable and genre-identifiable at 100px wide — the size it appears in Amazon search results. If the genre isn't clear at thumbnail size, the cover is failing its primary job.

**Typography hierarchy:** Title > Author Name (usually). Exception: established authors where name is the brand.

**Illustrated vs. photographic:** These signals are nearly as strong as palette:
- Illustrated = cozy mystery, rom-com, sweet romance, middle grade
- Photographic = thriller, mainstream romance, women's fiction, suspense

**Series consistency:** If this is part of a series, the cover must share a visual DNA with the others — same typography treatment, same palette family, similar composition. Series branding > individual book optimization.

**The 3-second rule:** A reader browsing Amazon has about 3 seconds to decide if a cover is worth a click. The cover must communicate: (1) genre, (2) tone, (3) promise — in that order and that fast.
