# Welcome Sequence Email Craft

## The Golden Rule
These emails are from the AUTHOR, not from "a newsletter." They should read like a friend who happens to write books, not a marketing department.

## Voice Matching
Before writing any email, the author's voice must be established:
- If manuscripts were uploaded: match the narration style, humor, emotional register
- If described: follow the description precisely
- The emails should feel like the same person who wrote the book is writing to the reader personally

## Subject Lines
Subject lines for author emails follow different rules than marketing emails:

**DO:**
- Use the author's voice/personality
- Be conversational ("So about that ending..." / "I have a confession" / "Quick question for you")
- Create curiosity without clickbait
- Reference the magnet, characters, or shared experience
- Keep under 50 characters when possible

**DON'T:**
- Use ALL CAPS or excessive punctuation
- Sound corporate ("Your Weekly Update from [Pen Name]")
- Use spam trigger words (FREE, LIMITED TIME, ACT NOW)
- Be vague ("Newsletter #1")

## Email Structure Rules

### Length
- Delivery email: 150-250 words (short, excited, get them reading)
- Email 1: 300-500 words (personal, storytelling)
- Email 2: 250-400 words (value-focused, not wordy)
- Email 3: 300-500 words (catalog tour, needs space for multiple books)

### Formatting
- Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences)
- No headers or bullet points (this is a personal email, not a blog post)
- One clear CTA per email (don't split attention)
- Links should be contextual ("Check out [Book Title] here" not "CLICK HERE")
- Sign off with first name, not "The [Pen Name] Team"
- PS lines are gold — high read-rate. Use for the secondary message.

### Tone by Genre
**Romance:** Warm, conspiratorial, excited about love stories. "I ugly-cried writing chapter 12."
**Dark Romance:** Confident, slightly dangerous, unapologetic. "Fair warning: this one has no redemption arc."
**Cozy Mystery:** Friendly, chatty, like talking over tea. "I may have based the murder weapon on my actual kitchen."
**Thriller:** Direct, slightly intense, compelling. "I wrote the twist ending first. Everything else was reverse-engineered."
**UF:** Snarky, personality-forward, casual. "My protagonist would absolutely judge me for how long this email took to write."
**Horror:** Atmospheric, slightly unsettling, honest. "I wrote chapter 9 at 2 AM and had to turn all the lights on when I finished."
**Cozy Fantasy:** Warm, whimsical, inviting. "If I could live in any world I've created, it would be this one."

## The Purchase Bridge
Every welcome sequence must bridge to purchase without being pushy. The technique:

**Email 1 (Connection):** Mention the paid book ONCE, casually, in context of the story being told. Not a pitch — a reference.
**Email 2 (Value):** The paid book appears as part of the value content. If recommending books, include your own alongside others. If sharing behind-the-scenes, reference a specific scene.
**Email 3 (Catalog):** Direct but warm. "Here's everything I've written, and here's where I'd start if I were you." This is the one email that's explicitly about the catalog.

## Reply Invitations
Emails that get replies improve deliverability. Include a genuine question:
- "Hit reply and tell me — did you see the twist coming?"
- "I'm curious — which character did you connect with most?"
- "Reply with your favorite [genre] book and I'll add it to my TBR"

## Unsubscribe Grace
Never guilt-trip about unsubscribing. The footer should be clean and respectful. Optional: add personality to the unsubscribe text ("No hard feelings if this isn't your thing — unsubscribe here").
