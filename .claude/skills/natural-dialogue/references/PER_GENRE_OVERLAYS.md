# Per-Genre Dialogue Overlays

Genre-specific dialogue notes that layer on top of the 14 categories. Read only the section relevant to the user's genre.

---

## Reverse Harem (RH) / Why Choose / Romantasy

**Genre-required dialogue beats:**
- **Claim language** — *mine, yours, say it, ours, you're not theirs.* Each romantic interest should have a distinct claim register — common archetypes: clipped-alpha possession, bratty-charmer demand, worship-tier consent-asking, terse-protector.
- **Verbal possession in spice scenes** — *"Yours." "Say it." "Mine."* These are signature, not flat. Audit for character-appropriateness, not for category-3 acknowledgment-fluff.
- **Public claim moments** — short, weighted, often interruptive. *"She's not yours to threaten."*
- **Per-MMC praise/bratty/worship register** — each MMC's spice register should have its own dialogue shape.

**Genre-specific dialogue traps:**
- **All MMCs sound the same** — speaker-voice collapse (Category 10) is the single biggest RH bug. Three (or four) men with distinct trope archetypes need three (or four) distinct dialogue registers. The "broody alpha" voice creeping into all of them is the most common drift.
- **Heroine apologizes for her wanting** — DNF trigger; flag any line where she does.
- **Heroine ranks the MMCs in voice** — "*He was the safest one.*" / "*Caspian made me feel things [Kieran] never could.*" Breaks Why-Choose promise; flag.
- **Fated-mate as consent override** — *"You're mine. You don't get to refuse."* Without consent reframe = DNF trigger. Flag.
- **Group-scene dialogue** — when group/shared spice scenes appear, dialogue must distribute across all MMCs proportionally. Flag if one MMC dominates verbal possession beats.
- **Mate-mark / bond mechanic dialogue** — when characters discuss the bond, the dialogue must be canon-coherent (no rule violations slipping in). Cross-reference voice profile or series bible.

**Allowed exceptions:**
- Bare confirmations during heat — *"Yes."* during sex is genre-natural and often signature. Don't flag if part of established spice-cadence.
- The bratty "Make me" / clipped "Behave." exchanges that are RH catnip — preserve.

## Dark Academia

**Genre-required dialogue beats:**
- **Erudite register** — characters quote, cite, reference. Latin/Greek/literature drops in flavor.
- **Power dynamics through politeness** — manners-as-weapon. *"How kind of you."* meaning the opposite.
- **Rituals and forms** — formal address, ceremonial phrasing in specific contexts.

**Genre-specific dialogue traps:**
- **Pretentiousness without substance** — characters quote for the sake of quoting. Each quote should do plot or character work.
- **All characters sound equally erudite** — voice collapse via shared register. The smartest character should still sound *distinct* from the second-smartest.
- **Class-coded dialogue without specificity** — flag generic "upper-class" register vs. character-specific class register. A scholarship student's dialogue at an elite school should be audibly different.

**Allowed exceptions:**
- Latin / dead-language drops are tropic — preserve when character-coherent.

## Cozy Mystery

**Genre-required dialogue beats:**
- **Warm community register** — neighbors, regulars, found-family dynamics.
- **Sleuth's questioning register** — careful, curious, never aggressive.
- **Comfort-food sensory language in dialogue** — characters mention food, weather, hobbies in dialogue (which would be Category 2 filler in other genres but is genre-required here).

**Genre-specific dialogue traps:**
- **Aggressive interrogation creep** — sleuth dialogue trending toward thriller register.
- **All red-herring suspects have suspicious dialogue** — too obvious. Real cozy hides the killer in plain sight; their dialogue should be unremarkable.
- **Modern slang in period cozy** — flag heavily.

**Allowed exceptions:**
- Categories 2 and 3 (filler exchanges, acknowledgment fluff) tolerate higher density in cozy than in other genres. Audit for taste, not absence.

## Thriller / Crime

**Genre-required dialogue beats:**
- **Terse exchanges** — short lines, fast cuts, body-action between dialogue.
- **Procedural register** — when investigators speak, the procedural-correctness adds verisimilitude.
- **Antagonist menace through restraint** — the most threatening line is often the calmest.

**Genre-specific dialogue traps:**
- **Villain monologue** — exposition-as-confession. Old fix: don't let the villain explain.
- **Detective explaining their reasoning to a partner** — Category 11 (explainer dialogue) is endemic. Audit hard.
- **Tough-guy register collapse** — every male character sounds like the same hardboiled archetype.

**Allowed exceptions:**
- Categories 1 (bare confirmations) and 9 (adverbs on tags) — *"Yes."* in a tense interrogation is genre-natural; *she said quietly* during a stakeout adds atmosphere. Tolerate higher density.

## Literary Fiction

**Genre-required dialogue beats:**
- **Subtext as primary medium** — most emotional content lives in what's NOT said.
- **Pause-naming** — the prose names silences, hesitations, what someone almost said.
- **Register shifts as data** — when a character shifts register mid-conversation, the prose tracks it.

**Genre-specific dialogue traps:**
- **Self-conscious style** — dialogue performing literariness rather than serving character.
- **Therapy-speak slipping in** (Category 5) — endemic in contemporary literary fiction.
- **All characters articulate** — every character speaks in fully formed sentences with literary precision. Real characters trail off, contradict, miss the point.

**Allowed exceptions:**
- Category 4 (on-the-nose emotional naming) can be deliberate stylistic choice when used as voice (e.g., flat-affect first-person narrator who names emotions clinically).

## Epic Fantasy

**Genre-required dialogue beats:**
- **High-register and low-register characters** — kings vs. peasants vs. soldiers vs. mages should sound distinct.
- **In-world idioms** — characters don't say *"break a leg";* they say something the world has earned.
- **Council scenes / formal address** — ceremonial register in specific contexts.

**Genre-specific dialogue traps:**
- **Forsoothery** — fake-archaic dialogue that sounds like a Renaissance Faire. Earn the register; if the world is medieval-coded, study actual period speech.
- **Modern slang in secondary-world** — flag.
- **All characters sound equally formal** — see Dark Academia voice collapse note.

---

## Contemporary Romance

**Genre-required dialogue beats:**
- **Banter** — fast, playful, tension-loaded.
- **Internal-conflict dialogue** — characters articulating their own emotional barriers (often direct, in this genre — Category 4 has higher tolerance).
- **Grand-gesture or reckoning beats** — third-act apology / declaration scenes.

**Genre-specific dialogue traps:**
- **Banter that doesn't ladder** — banter without rising tension is just two people quipping. Each exchange should escalate or shift.
- **Black-moment dialogue resolved by external coincidence** — flag.
- **Internal conflict named in dialogue without buildup** — Category 4 violations that stick out.

## YA (overlay)

YA-specific notes layer on top of the genre overlay (e.g., YA romantasy gets RH/romantasy + YA).

- **Voice register** — accessible without being condescending
- **Adult characters not driving climax dialogue** — flag if the protagonist's parent / mentor / teacher delivers the climax line
- **Heat/profanity ceiling** — appropriate for the YA shelf
- **Modern slang** — character-period-appropriate; YA tolerates contemporary slang in contemporary settings

---

## A note on hybrid genres

Many current bestsellers are hybrids — dark academia romantasy, RH paranormal, thriller-with-romance-elements. When auditing, read all relevant overlays. Hybrids inherit genre-required beats and genre-specific traps from each parent genre. Clearing one genre's checklist while triggering another's is the most common hybrid bug.
