# Rewrite Patterns

Generic strategies for what replaces a flat dialogue line. Use when the audit flags a line and the author wants a fix-pattern menu rather than a specific rewrite. When a voice profile is loaded, draw character-appropriate rewrites by combining a pattern below with the character's documented voice rules.

---

## The five replacement strategies

Every flat or generic line can be replaced with one of these strategies. Most strong dialogue uses some combination.

### Strategy 1: Voice tic

Replace the flat line with the character's signature register doing its thing.

- **Dry deflector:** *"You're new?"* → *"Showing already?"*
- **Clipped reframer:** *"You're new?"* → *"I was last week, too."*
- **Bratty riff-and-drop:** *"You all right?"* → *"Haven't decided. Is the bread always like this?"*
- **Precision understatement:** *"You're hurt?"* → *"It's a bruise."*
- **Speaks-around evader:** *"Did you sleep?"* → *"I closed my eyes."*
- **Working-class deflation:** *"Did you find it?"* → *"It found me."*

Pattern: identify what makes this character's voice *sound like them.* Replace generic acknowledgment with that.

---

### Strategy 2: Plot information

Replace the flat line with specific information that advances or complicates the scene.

- ❌ *"Don't be late."* / *"Yes."*
- ✓ *"Don't be late."* / *"Five and eight. I'll be there for both."* (echo + confirm + extra)

- ❌ *"You hungry?"* / *"Yes."*
- ✓ *"You hungry?"* / *"Haven't eaten since yesterday."*

- ❌ *"Are we still on for tomorrow?"* / *"Yes."*
- ✓ *"Are we still on for tomorrow?"* / *"Eight, like we said. I'll bring the file."*

Pattern: what does this character know that they could share? What detail makes the answer specific?

---

### Strategy 3: Reframe

Reject the assumption embedded in the question. Forces the asker to recalibrate.

- ❌ *"You scared?"* / *"Yes."*
- ✓ *"You scared?"* / *"Wrong word."*

- ❌ *"Did he hurt you?"* / *"Yes."*
- ✓ *"Did he hurt you?"* / *"Not in the way you mean."*

- ❌ *"Are you mad?"* / *"Yes."*
- ✓ *"Are you mad?"* / *"That's not the part you should be asking about."*

Pattern: identify the assumption in the question. Refuse it.

---

### Strategy 4: Question back

Turn the exchange around. Often the most efficient way to reveal character — what someone asks shows what they care about.

- ❌ *"You okay?"* / *"Yes."*
- ✓ *"You okay?"* / *"Why are you asking?"*

- ❌ *"You ready?"* / *"Yes."*
- ✓ *"You ready?"* / *"Are you?"*

- ❌ *"Did you see her?"* / *"Yes."*
- ✓ *"Did you see her?"* / *"Did you?"*

Pattern: what would this character notice or wonder, given what was just asked? Ask that back.

---

### Strategy 5: Echo a detail

Repeat a specific element of the question — signals careful listening, voice tic of attention.

- ❌ *"You've been here all night?"* / *"Yes."*
- ✓ *"You've been here all night?"* / *"Since the second hour."*

- ❌ *"He's not coming?"* / *"No."*
- ✓ *"He's not coming?"* / *"Not coming."*

- ❌ *"Did she say where she was going?"* / *"Yes."*
- ✓ *"Did she say where she was going?"* / *"South. She didn't say further."*

Pattern: take one specific noun or verb from the question, return it.

---

## Combination strategies

Stronger lines often combine two or three strategies.

**Voice tic + plot info:**
- ❌ *"You found the file?"* / *"Yes."*
- ✓ *"You found the file?"* / *"Bottom drawer, mislabeled. Same place she always hides things."* (voice tic of dry observation + plot detail + character history)

**Reframe + question back:**
- ❌ *"Are you in love with him?"* / *"Yes."*
- ✓ *"Are you in love with him?"* / *"Wrong question. Ask me the next one."* (reframes + invites)

**Echo + reframe:**
- ❌ *"You don't trust me?"* / *"No."*
- ✓ *"You don't trust you me?"* / *"Don't trust. Different word from what you think."* (echo + reframes the assumption)

## Strategies for the surrounding patterns

Beyond the five primary, useful for specific category fixes:

### For filler exchanges (Category 2)

- **Cut entirely** — jump to the next charged beat
- **Replace with body action** — characters gesture, hand off objects, make eye contact
- **Compress to narration** — *"They greeted each other."* / *"He nodded; she nodded back."*

### For on-the-nose emotional naming (Category 4)

- **Show through body** — *Her hands had stopped on the bowl.*
- **Show through action** — *He left the room.*
- **Show through indirection** — *"Don't ask me right now."*
- **Show through deliberate inadequacy** — *"I'm fine."* (when the prose makes clear they're not)

### For tag-as-meaning (Category 8)

- **Strengthen the line** — make the line carry the emotion the tag was carrying
- **Use a body beat** — let an action between dialogue lines do the work the tag was doing

### For explainer dialogue (Category 11)

- **Cut entirely** — trust the reader to absorb later
- **Move to narration** — appropriate when the information is interior or world-building rather than character-revealing
- **Reframe so one character genuinely doesn't know** — outsider POV, new arrival, briefing scene
- **Show through scene** — let the reader see the behavior; trust them to infer

### For trailing dot-dot-dot fluency (Category 13)

- **Commit to the line** — let the character finish the thought
- **Use silence with prose-acknowledgment** — *She didn't finish the sentence. She didn't have to.*
- **Replace ellipsis with em-dash mid-sentence** — different beat (self-correction vs. trailing-off); use deliberately

### For speaker-voice collapse (Category 10)

- **Identify the weaker character's documented voice tics** (in the voice profile if loaded)
- **Sharpen with distinct rhythm** — short sentences vs. long; fragments vs. completion; questions vs. declarations
- **Sharpen with distinct vocabulary** — give each character a 5-word lexicon they reach for that the other characters don't
- **Sharpen with distinct pause mechanics** — one character stalls before answering; another interrupts; another over-explains

## Anti-patterns (don't deploy as default)

Things that LOOK like fixes but are actually new flatness:

- **Snarky one-liners as default** — every flat line replaced with a quip is its own kind of voice collapse (everyone sounds like a sitcom character)
- **Overlong rewrites** — a five-word *"Yes"* doesn't need to become a fifty-word monologue; the rewrite should be roughly the same length, just doing more work per word
- **Cleverness over character** — a rewrite that sounds witty but isn't true to the character is worse than the original
- **Pattern repetition** — using Strategy 4 (question-back) every time becomes its own tic. Vary across the chapter.

---

## When the line should stay flat

Sometimes a bare confirmation is RIGHT. Don't fix what's working.

- **Stonewalling characterization** — the character is refusing to engage; the bareness IS the data.
- **Genre-natural during heat** — *"Yes."* during sex is genre-appropriate and often signature.
- **The tic of a single character** — one character whose voice IS clipped acknowledgment, deployed consistently and earned across the manuscript.
- **The pause before a complication** — *"Yes."* followed by a body beat that complicates the *Yes* (*"Yes." She didn't move from the door.*) is a deliberate sequencing.

Audit catches the default flat lines. The author keeps the load-bearing flat lines.
