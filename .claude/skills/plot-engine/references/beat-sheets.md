# Beat Sheet Reference Templates

Detailed beat sheet templates with percentage markers, common mistakes, and genre-specific examples. Use alongside the Plot Engine skill for deep structural work.

---

## SAVE THE CAT! (NOVEL ADAPTATION)

### Beat 1: Opening Image (0-1%)

**What it occupies:** The first 1-2 pages to first scene.

**What it must accomplish:**
- Show the protagonist in their "before" state
- Establish the tone and world of the book
- Create a snapshot that the Final Image will mirror/contrast
- Give the reader a reason to care (voice, situation, intrigue)

**Common mistakes:**
- Starting with the character waking up (boring, overdone)
- Starting with weather or scenery description (no character, no hook)
- Starting in a scene that has nothing to do with the story's core conflict
- Making the opening image too subtle -- the reader doesn't realize it's significant
- Infodumping backstory instead of showing a moment

**Example scenes -- Romance:**
- Protagonist A at her best friend's wedding, catching the bouquet and immediately dropping it. She doesn't want this. She's fine alone. (Before state: closed off to love)
- Protagonist B alone in his new apartment in a small town, unpacking a single box. He moved here to disappear. (Before state: running from connection)

**Example scenes -- Thriller:**
- FBI agent Sarah Chen reviews cold case files at 2 AM, the only one still in the office. She hasn't solved a case in eight months. (Before state: losing her edge)
- A jogger finds a body in the park at dawn. We don't know who they are yet. The body is arranged with deliberate care. (Before state: the world before it knows a predator is active)

### Beat 2: Theme Stated (5%)

**What it occupies:** A single scene or moment, usually in dialogue.

**What it must accomplish:**
- State or imply the book's thematic question
- Come from a character OTHER than the protagonist
- The protagonist must not recognize it as significant
- Plant the thematic seed that the entire book will grow

**Common mistakes:**
- Being too on-the-nose ("You know, love is about letting people see the real you")
- Making it so subtle nobody catches it
- Having the protagonist agree with the theme immediately (defeats the purpose -- they need to LEARN this)
- Skipping it entirely, which leaves the book feeling thematically unanchored

**Example scenes -- Romance:**
- Protagonist's mother says offhandedly, "You can't build a life with someone by showing them only the parts you've polished." Protagonist rolls her eyes.
- Protagonist's business partner says, "The best deals are the ones where both sides risk something real."

**Example scenes -- Thriller:**
- Partner says to the protagonist, "You can't catch a killer by thinking like a cop. You have to think like someone with nothing left to lose."
- Victim's family member says, "The people who hurt us the most are the ones we never saw coming."

---

### Beat 3: Setup (1-10%)

**What it occupies:** The first 10% of the book, roughly 7,000-10,000 words in a 90K book.

**What it must accomplish:**
- Establish the protagonist's world: job, relationships, routines, environment
- Show the protagonist's flaw/wound in action (not explained -- shown)
- Introduce key supporting characters
- Plant at least 3 things that will pay off later
- Create reader empathy: we must root for this person
- Establish what the protagonist WANTS (external goal)
- Hint at what the protagonist NEEDS (internal truth they lack)

**Common mistakes:**
- Too much setup, not enough forward momentum (the reader gets bored)
- Not enough setup, character feels thin when the Catalyst hits
- Telling the reader about the character's wound instead of showing it in behavior
- Introducing too many characters at once (reader can't track them)
- Failing to make the protagonist sympathetic or interesting before disrupting their life

**Example scenes -- Romance:**
- Show the protagonist excelling at work but eating lunch alone. Show her dodging her mother's calls about a family event. Show her watching a couple argue outside her window with clinical detachment. We understand: she's successful, isolated, and convinced that's a feature, not a bug.
- Show the protagonist coaching his nephew's baseball team, joking with neighbors, fixing his sister's porch railing. But notice: he never talks about himself. Never mentions where he came from. The warmth is real, but it's also a wall.

**Example scenes -- Thriller:**
- Show the detective at a crime scene, competent and sharp. Then show her at home: takeout containers, a half-packed suitcase that's been sitting there for weeks, a wedding ring in the junk drawer. She's good at her job and terrible at her life.
- Show the protagonist giving a lecture on criminal psychology to a rapt audience. Then show him alone in his office, staring at a newspaper clipping tacked to the wall. Something personal is driving this.

### Beat 4: Catalyst (10%)

**What it occupies:** A single scene or chapter. Clear, unmistakable, disruptive.

**What it must accomplish:**
- Break the status quo permanently
- Be external -- something that happens TO the protagonist, not an internal realization
- Be big enough to justify the entire story that follows
- Be clear: the reader must recognize this as the moment everything changes

**Common mistakes:**
- Making it too small (the protagonist could ignore it and the story would stop)
- Making it an internal decision instead of an external event
- Burying it in the middle of other events so the reader misses its significance
- Having it happen too early (not enough setup) or too late (reader is bored waiting)

**Example scenes -- Romance:**
- The protagonist's company merges with a rival firm, and her new co-lead on the biggest project of the year is the man who destroyed her in college. She can't quit -- she needs this promotion. He can't leave -- he needs this redemption.
- A letter arrives from an attorney: the protagonist has inherited a house in the small town he swore he'd never return to. But there's a condition: he must live there for six months.

**Example scenes -- Thriller:**
- A body is found that matches the MO of a killer the protagonist put away ten years ago. That killer is still in prison. Either they got the wrong person, or someone is copying the kills. Either answer is terrifying.
- The protagonist receives a message that contains details only three people in the world should know. One of them is dead. One is missing. The third is the protagonist.

---

### Beat 5: Debate (10-20%)

**What it occupies:** Roughly 10% of the book. Multiple scenes.

**What it must accomplish:**
- Show the protagonist weighing the cost of action vs. inaction
- Reveal deeper fears and motivations
- Let the reader feel the stakes of the choice
- Build anticipation for the Break Into Two
- This is NOT inaction -- the protagonist is actively wrestling

**Common mistakes:**
- Protagonist debates too long (pacing dies)
- Debate is purely internal with no scenes to dramatize it
- The answer is too obvious (if the choice is easy, the Debate has no tension)
- Skipping it entirely -- protagonist jumps from Catalyst to action with no hesitation (makes them feel reckless or the stakes feel low)

**Example scenes -- Romance:**
- She researches him online. She asks her friend for advice. She tries to get reassigned and fails. She runs into him at a coffee shop and they have a tense, charged conversation. She debates: can she work with this man without it destroying her?
- He drives to the small town, sits in his truck outside the house for twenty minutes, then drives back to the city. He calls the lawyer: is there any way around the condition? There isn't.

**Example scenes -- Thriller:**
- The detective requests the old case files. Her captain says no: it's not her case. She visits the convicted killer in prison. He says something that doesn't add up. She debates: is she willing to blow up a conviction she built her career on?
- The protagonist tries to ignore the message. Then another arrives. Then a package. Each escalation makes ignoring it more impossible. They consult an old ally who warns them: once you go down this road, you can't come back.

### Beat 6: Break Into Two (20%)

**What it occupies:** A single decisive scene or chapter.

**What it must accomplish:**
- The protagonist makes an active choice to commit
- They step into a new world, situation, or reality
- There is no going back -- the bridge is burned
- This must be a CHOICE, not a push

**Common mistakes:**
- Making it passive (the protagonist is dragged into Act 2 by events rather than choosing)
- Not making it feel decisive enough
- Having it happen too gradually (it should be a clear turning point)
- The protagonist having no skin in the game

**Example scenes -- Romance:**
- She walks into the first project meeting, sits directly across from him, and says, "Let's set some ground rules." She's not running. She's playing to win.
- He unlocks the front door of the house, carries his bag inside, and calls the attorney: "I'm staying."

**Example scenes -- Thriller:**
- She takes the old case files home, locks the door, and starts building a parallel investigation on her own time. She knows what this means for her career if she's wrong.
- The protagonist boards a flight to the city where the message originated. No backup. No official sanction. Just the need to know.

---

### Beat 7: B Story (22%)

**What it occupies:** Introduced here, runs alongside the A story throughout Act 2.

**What it must accomplish:**
- Introduce or deepen the relationship that carries the theme
- In romance: the B Story and A Story are often the same -- the romance IS the story
- In other genres: this is the mentor, the friend, the love interest who reflects the theme back to the protagonist
- The B Story character often delivers the Break Into Three realization

**Common mistakes:**
- In romance: forgetting that there IS still a B Story (a friendship, a family relationship, a mentorship that deepens the theme)
- In other genres: making the B Story relationship feel disconnected from the main plot
- Introducing the B Story character too late to build genuine connection
- Making the B Story purely functional (only exists to deliver information)

**Example scenes -- Romance:**
- Her best friend / sister / coworker who challenges her on her avoidance patterns. The friend who says, "You don't let anyone in because you think vulnerability is weakness."
- His relationship with his nephew, who asks innocent questions that crack his armor. "Why don't you ever talk about where you grew up, Uncle Jack?"

**Example scenes -- Thriller:**
- A new partner assigned to the case who approaches crime differently -- intuitively where the protagonist is analytical. They clash, but the partner sees things the protagonist misses.
- An old mentor comes out of retirement when they hear about the case. They carry wisdom and regret in equal measure.

---

### Beat 8: Fun and Games (20-50%)

**What it occupies:** The largest single beat -- 30% of the book.

**What it must accomplish:**
- Deliver the PROMISE OF THE PREMISE
- This is what the reader bought the book for
- In romance: the banter, the tension, the near-misses, the falling
- In thriller: the investigation, the chasing, the uncovering
- In fantasy: the magic, the questing, the discovering
- Show the protagonist engaging with the new world/situation
- Build toward the Midpoint escalation

**Common mistakes:**
- Not delivering what the genre promises (a romance where the leads don't interact much, a thriller without investigation scenes)
- Making it too episodic with no building momentum
- Forgetting to escalate -- the fun and games should intensify, not plateau
- Losing sight of the internal arc during the external "fun"
- Mid-book sag: the dreaded section where the reader puts the book down

**Example scenes -- Romance:**
- Working late together, the hostile banter that slowly develops inside jokes. A business dinner where they have to pretend to get along and realize they're not pretending. A vulnerability: she sees him after a hard phone call and for the first time sees the person behind the armor.
- Small-town life forces them together constantly -- the one grocery store, the nosy neighbors, the community events. He starts renovating the house and she offers to help (she's a contractor). Proximity + shared work + grudging respect = falling.

**Example scenes -- Thriller:**
- Interviews with witnesses who contradict each other. Following a lead that opens three new questions. A crime scene that reveals the killer's pattern. A close call that shows the antagonist knows the protagonist is looking. Database work that turns up a connection nobody saw.
- Each new city, each new clue peels back another layer. The protagonist's skills are tested. Alliances form. The scope of the conspiracy becomes clear. Every answer generates two new questions.

### Beat 9: Midpoint (50%)

**What it occupies:** A single scene or chapter at the center of the book.

**What it must accomplish:**
- False victory (things seem great but aren't) OR false defeat (things seem terrible but there's a path forward)
- Raise the stakes dramatically
- Shift the protagonist from reactive to proactive (or vice versa)
- New information that reframes everything
- The "fun" is over -- now it gets real

**Common mistakes:**
- No midpoint at all (the book sags through the center)
- A midpoint that doesn't actually change anything
- Making it too subtle -- the reader and author should both feel the shift
- False victory without consequences or false defeat without hope

**Example scenes -- Romance:**
- First kiss / first night together (false victory: they think they can have this AND keep their walls up. They can't.)
- A public confrontation that forces both characters to reveal more than they intended. Everything shifts. (False defeat: they think they've ruined it. They haven't -- but the rules have changed.)

**Example scenes -- Thriller:**
- The protagonist discovers the convicted man really IS innocent. Everything she built her career on is a lie. (False defeat that's actually the key to solving everything.)
- They catch what they think is the killer. Case closed. Celebration. Then another body drops. (False victory -- the real antagonist is still out there.)

---

### Beat 10: Bad Guys Close In (50-75%)

**What it occupies:** 25% of the book. The pressure cooker.

**What it must accomplish:**
- External pressure: the antagonist, the opposition, the threat escalates
- Internal pressure: doubts, old wounds, self-sabotage resurface
- Interpersonal pressure: relationships fracture under stress
- Everything the protagonist built in Fun and Games starts to unravel
- The flaw the protagonist hasn't dealt with starts costing them

**Common mistakes:**
- Only escalating external threats without internal pressure (feels shallow)
- Only escalating internal angst without external stakes (feels navel-gazing)
- Making the protagonist passive under pressure (they should fight, just increasingly ineffectively)
- Not connecting the "bad guys" to the protagonist's flaw (the external threat should mirror or exploit the internal weakness)

**Example scenes -- Romance:**
- His ex shows up. Her boss finds out about the relationship and threatens the project. Old wounds get triggered: she pulls away because intimacy feels dangerous. He misinterprets her distance as rejection because that's his wound. They're hurting each other with the exact behaviors they started to heal from.
- The town discovers his real identity. The gossip starts. The house has structural problems that mirror the relationship: the foundation is cracked. She's offered a job in the city that would mean leaving. Everything they built is under threat from every direction.

**Example scenes -- Thriller:**
- Evidence disappears. A witness recants. Her partner is pulled off the case. Internal Affairs opens an inquiry into her methods. The real killer sends a message: I know what you did. The walls close in from every direction.
- Allies are killed or turned. The conspiracy is bigger than the protagonist imagined. Their own organization might be compromised. Trust evaporates. The protagonist is isolated and outmatched.

### Beat 11: All Is Lost (75%)

**What it occupies:** A single devastating scene or short chapter.

**What it must accomplish:**
- The worst thing happens. Not the second-worst. The WORST.
- A death (literal or metaphorical): a relationship ends, a career dies, a dream shatters, a person dies
- The protagonist's plan fails completely
- The reader should believe there is no way out
- This is the opposite of the Midpoint -- if the Midpoint was a false victory, this is a real defeat

**Common mistakes:**
- Pulling punches (the "worst thing" isn't actually that bad)
- Making it feel manufactured rather than earned by the preceding events
- Not connecting it to the protagonist's flaw (the All Is Lost should be a consequence of the thing they refused to deal with)
- Multiple "worst things" diluting the impact -- pick ONE devastating blow

**Example scenes -- Romance:**
- He finds out she knew about the merger before they met. She wasn't assigned to him randomly -- she was sent to manage him. Everything he believed about them was built on her lie. He walks out. (The relationship dies.)
- She reads his journal and discovers he's been writing about the town's residents for a magazine expose. She's in it. Their vulnerability, their trust, their slow-building love -- it's all material to him. Or so she thinks.

**Example scenes -- Thriller:**
- The protagonist is arrested for the very crimes she's been investigating. The evidence is airtight. The real killer planted it over months. She's in a cell, her badge gone, her reputation destroyed. The killer is free and she's locked up.
- The person the protagonist trusted most -- the mentor, the partner, the ally -- is revealed as the architect of everything. The betrayal is complete. Everything the protagonist learned, every lead they followed, was fed to them deliberately.

---

### Beat 12: Dark Night of the Soul (75-80%)

**What it occupies:** 1-3 chapters of reflection, despair, and reckoning.

**What it must accomplish:**
- The protagonist sits in the wreckage
- They face their core wound -- not the surface fear, but the DEEP one
- This is emotional, not action-based
- The lie they've believed about themselves is exposed
- They must choose: stay broken or grow
- This is where the theme becomes personal

**Common mistakes:**
- Rushing through it to get to the action (the reader needs to sit in the pain)
- Making it too long (wallowing kills pacing)
- Resolving it too easily ("I just realized I should be brave!" is not enough)
- Having someone else fix it for the protagonist (this must be internal work)

**Example scenes -- Romance:**
- She sits in her empty apartment. She got what she always wanted: success, control, no vulnerability. And it's empty. She realizes the wall she built to protect herself is actually a prison. She doesn't want to be safe anymore. She wants to be known.
- He packs the truck. He's leaving, like he always does. But this time he sits in the driveway and can't turn the key. He calls his sister and says the thing he's never said: "I left because I was afraid I'd turn into Dad. And now I'm turning into him anyway -- by running."

**Example scenes -- Thriller:**
- In her cell, she stops fighting. She reviews every choice, every missed sign, every moment she chose ambition over intuition. She was so focused on being right that she stopped looking for the truth. That's her flaw. That's why the killer targeted her -- he knew she'd build the case against herself.
- Alone, stripped of resources and allies, the protagonist finally confronts why they do this work. Not for justice. For control. For the illusion that they can make the world make sense. And that illusion is shattered.

---

### Beat 13: Break Into Three (80%)

**What it occupies:** A single scene or realization.

**What it must accomplish:**
- The protagonist discovers the solution -- not external, but INTERNAL
- The B Story delivers its lesson (the friend, the love interest, the mentor provides the final piece)
- The theme crystallizes: the protagonist understands the truth they've been running from
- They know what they have to do AND who they have to become to do it
- This is the character arc completing its transformation

**Common mistakes:**
- Making it purely external (they find a clue, not a truth)
- Making it come from nowhere (it must be earned by the Dark Night)
- Having someone else hand them the answer instead of them discovering it
- Resolving the internal arc without connecting it to the external climax

**Example scenes -- Romance:**
- Her best friend says, "She didn't lie to hurt you. She lied because she was terrified you'd leave if you knew the truth. Sound familiar?" He realizes: she did exactly what he does. She protected herself. And he punished her for the same thing he's guilty of.
- His nephew finds him packing and says, "You promised you'd stay for my game." It's small. It's everything. Staying for one person is the first step to staying for yourself.

**Example scenes -- Thriller:**
- In reviewing the killer's messages from her cell, she realizes the pattern she missed: the killer wasn't copying the old MO. He was correcting her mistakes. The answer was always in the original case -- the thing she got wrong. She knows what she has to do, and it means admitting she was wrong about everything.
- The old mentor's final words -- dismissed earlier as ramblings -- suddenly make sense. The protagonist was looking for a criminal. They should have been looking for a victim. The entire frame shifts.

### Beat 14: Finale (80-99%)

**What it occupies:** Nearly 20% of the book. This is not one scene -- it's a sequence.

**What it must accomplish:**
Five-part Finale structure:
1. **Gather the Team** -- rally allies, make a plan, prepare for the final push
2. **Execute the Plan** -- put the plan into action
3. **High Tower Surprise** -- the plan hits an unexpected obstacle
4. **Dig Deep Down** -- the protagonist uses their NEW self (post-transformation) to adapt
5. **Execute the New Plan** -- succeed using the internal truth learned in Break Into Three

**Common mistakes:**
- Making the Finale a single confrontation scene (it needs to be a sequence with escalation)
- The protagonist winning with skills they already had (they need to use what they LEARNED)
- Resolving the external plot without resolving the internal arc (or vice versa)
- Making it too easy after the All Is Lost was devastating
- Deus ex machina -- help arriving that wasn't set up

**Example scenes -- Romance:**
- She resigns from the project. Goes to him. Not with an excuse -- with the truth. All of it. He doesn't make it easy. She doesn't ask him to. She shows up as herself, unpolished, unprotected. He says no. She stays anyway -- not to convince him, but because staying is the person she's choosing to become. When he finally breaks, it's because he sees she's not performing vulnerability. She IS vulnerable. And that's the person he fell in love with.
- He doesn't just stay for the nephew's game. He stands up at the town meeting and tells the truth about who he is and why he came. The town's reaction is mixed. She's in the back row. She doesn't forgive him immediately. He proves it over days: he fixes what he broke. He writes the article -- but the real version, the one about what this town taught him. She reads it and finds herself in every line.

**Example scenes -- Thriller:**
- She convinces a guard to get a message out. Her partner -- the one she pushed away -- gets it. He doesn't trust her but he checks. What he finds confirms her claim. They build a case from inside and outside the walls simultaneously. The real killer learns she's onto him and accelerates his plan. The race against time collapses into a confrontation where she must use every tool AND every hard truth she learned about herself.
- The protagonist returns to where it started. But they're different now -- they see the situation with new eyes. The final confrontation requires the protagonist to do the thing they swore they'd never do. The cost is real, but they pay it willingly.

### Beat 15: Final Image (99-100%)

**What it occupies:** The last scene or last page.

**What it must accomplish:**
- Mirror the Opening Image to show transformation
- Show the protagonist in their "after" state
- Deliver emotional satisfaction
- Leave the reader with the RIGHT feeling (hopeful, thoughtful, satisfied, bittersweet)

**Common mistakes:**
- No connection to the Opening Image (lost the structural bookend)
- Going on too long after the climax (denouement that won't end)
- Introducing new information or conflict
- Being too neat (feels false) or too ambiguous (feels unfinished -- unless that's the genre expectation)

**Example scenes -- Romance:**
- Opening Image was her catching and dropping a bouquet. Final Image: she's at another wedding -- maybe her own, maybe a friend's. She catches the bouquet again. This time she holds it. She looks at him across the room. He's already looking.
- Opening Image was him unpacking a single box in an empty apartment. Final Image: every room is full. Tools on the porch, kids' drawings on the fridge (the nephew's), her boots by the door. The house is a home. He didn't just stay. He built.

**Example scenes -- Thriller:**
- Opening Image was her alone in the office at 2 AM with cold case files. Final Image: she's in the office, same time, but she's on the phone with someone. She laughs. The cold case wall is gone. A new case is up -- but she's different. She's not proving anything anymore. She's just doing the work.
- Opening Image was a jogger finding a body. Final Image: the same park, dawn, a jogger runs past. The world goes on. But in the protagonist's office, a framed photo has been moved from the drawer to the desk. They're no longer hiding from what this cost them.

## ROMANCE BEAT SHEET (EXPANDED)

### Full Romancing the Beat Structure

#### Phase 1: Setup (0-20%)

**Beat 1: Protagonist A in Their Normal World (0-5%)**
Show who they are BEFORE the love interest disrupts everything. Establish:
- Their daily life and competence (make the reader like them)
- Their wound in action (not explained -- SHOWN in behavior)
- Their want (external goal that has nothing to do with romance)
- Their wall (the defense mechanism that will prevent love)

**Beat 2: Protagonist B in Their Normal World (5-8%)**
Same treatment. In dual POV, give them their own opening chapter. In single POV, introduce them through A's perspective but make them dimensional immediately.

**Beat 3: The Meet (8-12%)**
How they meet sets the trajectory. Types:
- **Cute Meet:** charming, accidental, creates immediate chemistry. Best for: romcom, light contemporary.
- **Hostile Meet:** adversarial, combative, creates immediate tension. Best for: enemies-to-lovers, workplace romance.
- **Forced Meet:** they have no choice but to interact. Best for: forced proximity, arranged marriage, fake dating.
- **Reunion Meet:** they have history. Best for: second chance, childhood friends, ex-lovers.
- **Rescue Meet:** one helps the other in crisis. Best for: suspenseful romance, protector tropes.

**Beat 4: Establish the External Plot (12-20%)**
The non-romance reason they must interact. The job, the inheritance, the shared custody, the survival situation, the mission. This is the engine that keeps them in proximity when their walls would otherwise keep them apart.

#### Phase 2: Falling in Love (20-50%)

**Beat 5: Forced Proximity / Reasons to Interact (20-25%)**
The external plot does its job. They HAVE to be around each other. The trope engine drives this: the shared office, the snowbound cabin, the same small town, the fake relationship commitment.

**Beat 6: Resistance + Attraction (25-30%)**
Push and pull. They notice each other physically and try not to. They impress each other and resent it. Banter sharpens. Tension builds. The reader should feel the electricity before the characters admit it.

**Beat 7: First Touch / First Kiss / Escalating Intimacy (30-40%)**
Physical intimacy escalates in tandem with emotional vulnerability. The first accidental touch, the lingering handshake, the almost-kiss, the actual kiss. Each escalation should be EARNED by an emotional shift.

**Beat 8: Vulnerability Moment (35-45%)**
One of them lets the mask slip. The reader sees behind the wall. This is where the reader falls in love with the couple -- not when they kiss, but when they TRUST.

Possible forms:
- A confession about the past
- Being caught in a moment of genuine emotion
- Doing something kind when nobody's watching
- Breaking down and letting the other person witness it

**Beat 9: Deepening (40-48%)**
They start choosing each other. Small ways first: saving a seat, bringing coffee, defending each other to outsiders. Then bigger: sharing secrets, making sacrifices, rearranging priorities.

**Beat 10: The "No Going Back" Moment (48-50%)**
They are IN. Even if they won't say it. The reader knows. This might be the first time they sleep together (if heat level supports it), the first "I need you," or the moment they choose this person over the safe option.

#### Phase 3: Retreating (50-80%)

**Beat 11: External or Internal Crisis (50-55%)**
Something threatens what they've built. Ex returns, secret exposed, job conflict, family pressure, misunderstanding. The key: the crisis must exploit their WOUND, not be random bad luck.

**Beat 12: Pulling Away (55-65%)**
One or both retreat to their old patterns. She rebuilds her wall. He runs. The avoidant avoids. The controller controls. Regression is the point -- they haven't fully transformed yet.

**Beat 13: The Black Moment (65-75%)**
The breakup. The betrayal. The seemingly impossible obstacle. This is the emotional climax of the book. If the reader doesn't feel genuine despair here, the HEA won't land.

**Beat 14: Separation (75-80%)**
Time apart. Both characters face who they are without the other. What they lost. What they gained. This is where the character arc completes internally -- they realize what they need to change.

#### Phase 4: Resolution (80-100%)

**Beat 15: Recognition of Growth (80-85%)**
They see it -- they've changed because of this person. Not into someone new, but into the best version of themselves. The wound isn't gone, but it's healing.

**Beat 16: Grand Gesture / Declaration (85-90%)**
Not just "I love you" but PROOF of transformation. The person who never stays, stays. The person who never asks for help, asks. The person who hides, stands up in front of everyone and is seen.

**Beat 17: Reunion (90-95%)**
The getting-back-together scene. This must pay off EVERY ounce of tension. Don't rush it. The reader has been waiting the entire book for this. Let them savor it.

**Beat 18: HEA/HFN Delivered (95-98%)**
Clearly and satisfyingly. The reader must have NO DOUBT these two are going to be okay. In romance, this is a covenant with the reader. Do not break it.

**Beat 19: External Plot Resolution (95-98%)**
Tie up the non-romance threads. The job, the family conflict, the external threat -- all resolved or clearly on the path to resolution.

**Beat 20: Epilogue (Optional, 99-100%)**
A glimpse of their future. Romance readers love epilogues. Six months later, one year later, the wedding, the baby, the settled life. It's dessert after the meal.

### Heat Escalation Timeline Mapped to Emotional Beats

The physical and emotional arcs must be intertwined. Here is how they map:

```
EMOTIONAL ARC          PHYSICAL ARC              HEAT LEVEL
                                            Clean  Med  Hot  Dark
Awareness             Noticing details        x     x    x    x
Resistance            Accidental touch         x     x    x    x
Curiosity             Lingering proximity      x     x    x    x
Intrigue              First deliberate touch   x     x    x    x
Vulnerability         Almost-kiss / first kiss x     x    x    x
Trust                 First kiss / making out  -     x    x    x
Falling               First intimate scene     -     -    x    x
Surrender             Full intimacy            -     -    x    x
Deepening             Emotional + physical     -     -    x    x
Crisis                Withdrawal of intimacy   x     x    x    x
Despair               Absence felt physically  x     x    x    x
Growth                -                        x     x    x    x
Reunion               Kiss / touch of reunion  x     x    x    x
HEA                   Final intimate scene     -     -    x    x
```

**Rule:** Physical escalation without emotional escalation feels gratuitous. Emotional escalation without physical escalation (in appropriate heat level) feels incomplete. They must move together.

### How Beats Shift for Different Romance Subgenres

**Contemporary Romance**
- Beats play out in recognizable, modern settings
- External conflicts often career, family, or social pressure
- The Meet can be anything -- the setting flexibility is the advantage
- Pacing: moderate. Room for humor, slice-of-life, and internal reflection.

**Historical Romance**
- Social constraints add automatic conflict (class, propriety, reputation)
- Physical intimacy beats shift later due to era expectations (unless the subgenre leans into breaking rules)
- The Black Moment often involves social ruin or forced separation by external authority
- Pacing: slightly slower setup to establish the world, but escalation must still build

**Paranormal Romance**
- The Meet often involves the supernatural element (bonding, transformation, recognition)
- Fated mates tropes compress the Falling in Love phase -- they know early, but resist
- External threat is often existential (war between factions, ancient evil, curse)
- The Black Moment can involve literal death/transformation
- Pacing: faster due to high external stakes running alongside the romance

**Romantic Suspense**
- The external plot (the suspense) and the romance must escalate in parallel
- Intimacy often happens under duress (danger accelerates bonding)
- The Black Moment may overlap with the suspense climax
- Both plots must resolve -- the mystery AND the romance
- Pacing: thriller-paced chapters, but with emotional beats woven through every action scene

**Dark Romance**
- Consent and power dynamics are central
- The "Falling in Love" phase may involve captor/captive, manipulation, or morally gray territory
- The Black Moment is often the protagonist realizing what they've accepted
- The HEA may look different from traditional romance (redemption arc vs. mutual descent)
- Pacing: intense, compressed, emotionally extreme

---

### Dual POV Beat Structure

When writing dual POV romance, each protagonist needs their own beats:

**When to switch POV:**
- After a major moment in one POV that the reader wants to see processed by the other
- When one character has information the other doesn't (dramatic irony)
- When both characters experience the same event differently
- At the Black Moment: show BOTH sides of the devastation

**POV-Specific Beat Mapping:**

```
BEAT                    POV A                       POV B
Setup                   Show A's wound/wall         Show B's wound/wall
The Meet                A's reaction to B           B's reaction to A
Resistance              A's reasons to stay away    B's reasons to stay away
First Touch             A's physical awareness      B's physical awareness
Vulnerability           A witnesses B's moment      B witnesses A's moment
No Going Back           Show in BOTH POVs -- they may realize at different times
Black Moment            A's devastation             B's devastation
Growth                  A's internal reckoning      B's internal reckoning
Grand Gesture           Whichever character acts    Other character receives
Reunion                 Both -- alternate paragraphs or give the final beat to the POV
                        character whose arc required the bigger transformation
```

**Rule:** The character who needs to change MORE gets the Grand Gesture scene. They prove their growth. The other character's POV shows it landing.

---

### The Black Moment: 5 Types

**1. The Betrayal**
One character discovers the other lied, hid something, or manipulated them. Trust is shattered.
- Works best when: the lie connects to the liar's wound (they lied BECAUSE of their fear, not despite it)
- Common in: fake dating, secret identity, bet/dare tropes

**2. The Sacrifice**
One character walks away FOR the other -- they believe leaving protects the person they love.
- Works best when: the reader can see both sides -- the sacrifice is misguided but understandable
- Common in: class difference, danger/protector, terminal illness tropes

**3. The Misunderstanding**
One character misinterprets an event, a conversation, or a discovery.
- Works best when: the misunderstanding is EARNED by the character's wound (they see betrayal because they expect betrayal)
- DANGER: this is the most hated Black Moment when done poorly. If the misunderstanding could be resolved with a single conversation, readers will throw the book. The misunderstanding must be layered with enough evidence and emotional context that the leap is believable.
- Common in: romantic comedy, second chance, office romance

**4. The External Force**
Something outside both characters tears them apart: a job across the country, a family ultimatum, a war, an arrest, a natural disaster.
- Works best when: the external force exploits or mirrors the internal wound
- Common in: historical romance, military romance, forced separation tropes

**5. The Self-Destruction**
One character sabotages the relationship because they believe they don't deserve it. They push the other person away on purpose.
- Works best when: the reader has seen the wound that drives this behavior throughout the book
- Most emotionally devastating when done well
- Common in: dark romance, wounded hero/heroine, addiction recovery, trauma-heavy stories

## THRILLER BEAT SHEET (EXPANDED)

### Full Structure with Pacing Notes

#### Hook (0-10%)

**Opening Gambit**
Start with a scene that establishes the threat level. Options:
- **Cold open with the villain/threat:** show what the antagonist is capable of before the protagonist enters. Establishes stakes immediately.
- **Victim-first opening:** the reader experiences the threat through a victim's eyes. Creates empathy and dread.
- **Protagonist in their element:** show competence first, then disrupt it. Creates a character the reader trusts to lead them through the story.

**Establishing the Protagonist**
- Show them as competent but flawed
- The flaw should be the thing that ALMOST lets the antagonist win
- Their professional skill is the reason they're drawn into this case
- Their personal wound is the reason it becomes personal

**Stakes Declaration**
By the end of the Hook, the reader must know:
- What kind of threat exists
- Why the protagonist is the one facing it
- What happens if they fail

---

#### Investigation / Descent (10-50%)

**Information Reveal Pacing**

This is the engine of a thriller. Control what the reader knows vs. what the protagonist knows.

```
CHAPTER    READER KNOWS       PROTAGONIST KNOWS    TENSION SOURCE
1-3        Threat exists       Something's wrong     Anticipation
4-7        More than protag    Basic facts           Dramatic irony
8-12       Same as protag      Growing picture       Discovery
13-16      Reader suspects     Protagonist trusts    Dread
17-20      Revelation hits     Revelation hits       Shared shock
```

**Four information states to rotate:**
1. **Reader knows more than protagonist** -- creates dread ("Don't go in there!")
2. **Protagonist knows more than reader** -- creates mystery ("What did they see?")
3. **Reader and protagonist learn together** -- creates partnership
4. **Neither knows** -- creates shared tension

**Red Herring Placement**

Red herrings must be:
- Plausible enough to genuinely mislead
- Connected enough to the real plot that they don't feel wasted when debunked
- Planted with the same care as real clues (if the reader can distinguish red herrings from real clues, they're too obvious)

**Placement strategy:**
- First red herring: 15-20% (early enough to misdirect, before the reader has enough info to see through it)
- Second red herring: 35-40% (deeper, more convincing, possibly involving a character the reader trusts)
- Third red herring (optional): 55-60% (post-midpoint, when the reader thinks they've got it figured out)

**Each red herring should:**
- Point to a specific suspect or theory
- Be supported by at least 2-3 pieces of "evidence"
- Be debunked in a way that reveals NEW information about the real plot
- Never feel cheap -- the reader should think "I should have seen that" not "that's cheating"

---

#### Midpoint Reversal (50%)

The midpoint of a thriller must REFRAME THE STORY. Options:

**The Betrayal Reversal:** An ally is revealed as compromised. The protagonist can't trust their own team.

**The Scope Reversal:** The crime/threat is much bigger than it appeared. What looked like one murder is a pattern. What looked like a robbery is a conspiracy.

**The Identity Reversal:** The victim isn't who they seemed. Or the suspect isn't who they seemed. Or the protagonist's past is connected to the case.

**The Personal Reversal:** The case becomes personal. The antagonist targets the protagonist directly. Or the protagonist discovers a personal connection to the crime.

**The Rule Reversal:** The rules of the world change. The protocol fails. The system the protagonist trusted is corrupt. The safe haven is compromised.

---

#### Escalation (50-75%)

**Escalation Mechanics:**
- Each chapter should end with the situation WORSE than it began
- Remove resources: allies, weapons, safe houses, communication, time
- Increase personal cost: reputation, relationships, health, sanity
- Accelerate the clock: deadlines tighten, the antagonist's plan moves faster

**The Tightening Spiral:**
```
50% - Protagonist has allies, resources, time, and a theory
55% - Theory is wrong; must start over with less time
60% - An ally is lost (death, betrayal, removal)
65% - Resources are cut (access revoked, equipment destroyed)
70% - Time runs out on the original timeline; new deadline is worse
75% - The protagonist is alone, outmatched, and running out of time
```

---

#### The Twist: Where It Works, Where It Doesn't, How to Plant It

**Where twists work:**
- At the Midpoint Reversal (50%): reframes everything, gives the second half new energy
- Just before the Climax (75-80%): maximum emotional impact, drives the final confrontation
- In the Resolution (95%): recontextualizes everything the reader experienced (use sparingly -- can feel like a cheat)

**Where twists don't work:**
- Too early (before 30%): the reader hasn't invested enough to care
- During the climax itself (85-90%): interrupts momentum
- After the resolution: feels like a gotcha, not a revelation

**How to plant a twist:**
1. **The truth must be visible in retrospect.** When the reader rereads, they should see the clues they missed. This means planting at least 3 genuine hints.
2. **Hide clues in plain sight.** Put them in the middle of scenes focused on something else. The reader's attention is elsewhere.
3. **Use the protagonist's flaw as camouflage.** The protagonist misses the clues for the same reason the reader does -- both are looking at the wrong thing.
4. **Misdirect with emotion, not logic.** If the reader LIKES the character who will be revealed as the villain, they'll resist seeing the clues. Emotional misdirection is more powerful than informational misdirection.
5. **The twist must be BETTER than the expected answer.** If the reader's theory is more interesting than the twist, the twist fails.

---

#### Multiple Antagonist Structures

**The Ladder**
Multiple antagonists of escalating threat. Defeat one, face a worse one.
- Works best in: action thrillers, conspiracy thrillers
- Structure: each antagonist reveals the next level of the conspiracy
- Danger: can feel like a video game if not handled with escalating personal stakes

**The Web**
Multiple antagonists connected but not hierarchical. Each has their own agenda. Some oppose each other.
- Works best in: political thrillers, spy novels
- Structure: protagonist must navigate conflicting threats
- Danger: complexity can overwhelm if there are too many threads

**The Mirror**
A single primary antagonist who reflects the protagonist's flaw. The antagonist is what the protagonist could become.
- Works best in: psychological thrillers, crime fiction
- Structure: every confrontation is also an internal reckoning
- Danger: can become too cerebral if external stakes aren't maintained

**The Hidden**
The antagonist is unknown for most of the book. The investigation IS the plot.
- Works best in: mystery-thrillers, whodunits
- Structure: suspects are introduced and eliminated; the real antagonist is hiding in plain sight
- Danger: the reveal must be satisfying. If the reader guesses too early, tension dies. If they can't guess at all, it feels unfair.

**The System**
The antagonist isn't a person but an institution, a bureaucracy, a culture. The protagonist fights something they can't punch.
- Works best in: legal thrillers, corporate thrillers, conspiracy thrillers
- Structure: identify the human face of the system, then reveal the system behind them
- Danger: abstract antagonists can feel bloodless. Always give the system a human representative.

## FANTASY BEAT SHEET

### Full Structure

#### Phase 1: The Ordinary World (0-15%)

**World Introduction**
The reader enters the world through the protagonist's daily life. World-building is filtered through character experience -- what's normal to them, what they notice, what they take for granted.

**Protagonist in Their Place**
Where they fit (or don't) in this world. Their power level, social status, desires, and limitations. In fantasy, the protagonist often starts at the BOTTOM of the power/knowledge hierarchy.

**The Promise of Magic**
Show the world's defining feature early. If there's magic, show it. If there's a prophecy, hint at it. If there are dragons, at least mention them. The reader picked up a fantasy novel -- deliver the genre promise in Act 1.

**World-Building Integration Points (0-15%)**

Do NOT dump world-building. Integrate it:
- **Through conflict:** explain the magic system during a scene where magic is being used under pressure
- **Through contrast:** the protagonist encounters something that violates the rules they know, prompting a natural explanation
- **Through character need:** the protagonist must learn something about the world to solve an immediate problem
- **Through dialogue:** two characters disagree about how something works, revealing the rules through argument
- **Through sensory experience:** describe how magic FEELS, how the air changes near a portal, how a spell tastes. Immerse, don't lecture.

**Rules to establish by 15%:**
- How magic/power works (or establish that it's mysterious, if that's the system)
- What the social/political structure is
- What the immediate threat or conflict looks like
- What the protagonist wants and why they can't have it yet

---

#### Phase 2: Crossing the Threshold (15-25%)

**The Call**
The event that pulls the protagonist out of their ordinary world. In fantasy, this is often:
- A discovery of hidden power or identity
- A quest given or thrust upon them
- A mentor's arrival or a mentor's death
- An invasion, a prophecy, a transformation
- Being chosen (willingly or not)

**Refusal and Commitment**
The protagonist resists, then commits. In fantasy, the commitment often involves physically LEAVING the ordinary world -- a journey begins.

**The Mentor / Guide**
Fantasy relies on mentors because the protagonist (and reader) need someone to explain the world. But the mentor should be:
- Flawed or limited (not omniscient)
- Withheld about certain truths (creates mystery)
- Eventually removed or neutralized (the protagonist must stand alone)

---

#### Phase 3: Trials and Training (25-50%)

**Magic System Escalation**

If the protagonist has power, it must escalate on a curve:

```
25% - Discovery: they can do something. It's raw, uncontrolled, surprising.
30% - First use under pressure: messy, costly, but effective enough to survive.
35% - Training montage (but make it serve plot): each lesson reveals world-building AND character.
40% - First mastery moment: they do something deliberately and well. But it costs them.
45% - Complication: the power has limits, costs, or consequences they didn't expect.
50% - Midpoint use: they use everything they've learned, and it's not enough. Something more is needed.
```

**Rules for magic escalation:**
- Every power-up must cost something (energy, health, moral compromise, relationships)
- Every new ability must introduce a new limitation
- The protagonist should NEVER be the most powerful person in the room until the very end
- If the magic system is hard (rules-based), the reader must know the rules before they see them exploited in the climax
- If the magic system is soft (mysterious), the COST must be clear even if the mechanism isn't

**Alliance Building**
Fantasy protagonists need companions. The fellowship, the party, the crew. Each member should:
- Have a distinct skill the protagonist lacks
- Have their own mini-arc (want, need, wound, growth)
- Create interpersonal conflict that mirrors the thematic conflict
- Be vulnerable to loss (the reader must believe they could die or leave)

---

#### Phase 4: The Midpoint Revelation (50%)

**The Truth Shifts**
What the protagonist believed about the world, the quest, or themselves is wrong. Options:
- The enemy is not who they thought
- The quest objective is not what they were told
- Their own identity/power is different than they understood
- An ally is compromised
- The cost of victory is higher than they imagined

**From Reactive to Active**
Before the Midpoint, the protagonist responds to events. After, they drive them. They stop following the quest and start defining it.

---

#### Phase 5: Complications and Loss (50-75%)

**The World Darkens**
Consequences arrive. The antagonist's power is demonstrated fully. The political situation worsens. Allies are tested, some fail. The world-building reveals its darkest aspects.

**Multi-POV Weaving Patterns**

If using multiple POVs:

**Pattern 1: Parallel Tracks**
Each POV character has their own location and storyline. Tracks converge for the climax.
- Best for: epic fantasy, wide scope
- Risk: readers prefer one track and skim others
- Solution: make each track essential -- information from Track B is needed to understand Track A

**Pattern 2: Alternating Focus**
Two or three POV characters in the same location, seeing events from different angles.
- Best for: political fantasy, court intrigue
- Risk: repetitive (showing the same event multiple times)
- Solution: each POV reveals something the other couldn't see

**Pattern 3: Hub and Spoke**
One primary POV (the hub) with secondary POVs (spokes) that provide crucial information the main character can't access.
- Best for: quest fantasy, mystery-fantasy
- Risk: secondary POVs feel thin
- Solution: give each spoke character their own stakes

**Pattern 4: Rotating Ensemble**
Equal weight to 4-6 POV characters. No single protagonist.
- Best for: epic fantasy series, political saga
- Risk: diffuse focus, reader attachment spreads thin
- Solution: give each character a distinct voice AND a distinct genre. One POV reads like a thriller, another like a romance, another like a political drama.

**POV transition rules:**
- Never switch POV mid-scene
- End one POV on tension, start the next on different tension (don't resolve and restart -- chain them)
- Each POV chapter must advance both its own arc AND the overall plot
- The reader should never feel punished for a POV switch (each new chapter must offer something they want)

---

#### Phase 6: The Abyss (75-80%)

**Maximum Loss**
The worst happens. In fantasy, this often involves:
- A major character death
- Loss of the magical artifact/power/key
- The fall of a kingdom/stronghold/safe haven
- The antagonist's plan succeeding
- The protagonist's power turning against them

**The Prophecy Problem (and How to Handle It)**

Prophecy is everywhere in fantasy. Here is how to use it without making it boring:

**The problem with prophecy:** If the reader knows the protagonist is "destined" to win, there are no stakes. If the prophecy spells out exactly what happens, there's no mystery.

**5 ways to make prophecy work:**

1. **The Misinterpreted Prophecy:** The prophecy is real, but everyone (including the protagonist) understands it wrong. The actual fulfillment is surprising.
   - Plant: establish the prophecy's wording early
   - Payoff: the literal words come true in an unexpected way

2. **The Costly Prophecy:** The protagonist IS destined to win -- but the cost is devastating. The question isn't IF they'll succeed, but what they'll sacrifice.
   - Plant: hint at the cost throughout
   - Payoff: the protagonist chooses to pay it

3. **The Rejected Prophecy:** The protagonist decides they don't WANT to be the chosen one. They fight the prophecy itself.
   - Plant: establish the prophecy as a burden, not a gift
   - Payoff: they either break it (and face consequences) or fulfill it on their own terms

4. **The False Prophecy:** The prophecy was manufactured. It's propaganda, manipulation, or a test.
   - Plant: subtle inconsistencies in the prophecy's history
   - Payoff: the revelation that the prophecy was a tool, and the protagonist must succeed WITHOUT destiny on their side

5. **The Multiple Candidate Prophecy:** The prophecy could apply to more than one person. Being "chosen" is not a given -- it's a competition or a choice.
   - Plant: introduce other candidates who fit the prophecy equally well
   - Payoff: what makes the protagonist THE one is their character, not their destiny

**Rule:** Never let prophecy remove agency. The protagonist must CHOOSE to act, not be dragged by fate. Even in a prophecy-driven story, the interesting moments are when the character defies, reinterprets, or pays the cost of destiny.

---

#### Phase 7: The Final Battle (80-95%)

**Convergence**
All threads, all POVs, all subplots converge for the climax. Every skill learned, every alliance built, every sacrifice made comes to bear.

**Escalation structure for the fantasy climax:**
1. **Gathering:** allies reunite, plans are made, final preparations
2. **Opening gambit:** the plan is executed. It works -- partially.
3. **Complications:** the antagonist's counter. Something unexpected. An ally falls.
4. **The protagonist's choice:** the moment where they must use everything they've become. Not just their power -- their CHARACTER.
5. **The cost:** victory comes at a price. Something is lost that can't be recovered.
6. **The turn:** the protagonist does the thing that no one expected -- including themselves. This is the character arc completing.

**Magic in the climax:**
- If the magic system is hard: the protagonist finds a creative application of KNOWN rules. The reader should be able to look back and see how it was possible. The satisfaction comes from cleverness.
- If the magic system is soft: the protagonist reaches a new level through emotional truth, sacrifice, or connection. The satisfaction comes from meaning.
- In BOTH cases: the magic alone doesn't win. Character does.

---

#### Phase 8: Resolution (95-100%)

**The New World**
Show how the world has changed. In fantasy, this is literal -- the map has changed, the political order has shifted, the magic system has been altered.

**Character Landing**
Each POV character gets their resolution:
- What did they gain?
- What did they lose?
- Where are they now vs. where they started?
- (For series) What new challenge awaits?

**Series Setup (if applicable)**
The current book's main plot MUST resolve satisfyingly. The reader must feel the book was worth reading on its own. But:
- The series-level antagonist should be glimpsed or escalated
- A new question should be raised
- A character should shift position (ally becomes leader, student becomes master)
- The world should be slightly larger than when the book started

**The final line should make the reader want the next book without feeling cheated by this one.**

---

### World-Building Integration Summary

Where to reveal world-building rules without info-dumping:

```
BOOK %    WHAT TO REVEAL              HOW TO REVEAL IT
0-5%      Immediate setting, tone      Sensory immersion, daily life
5-10%     Social structure basics       Character's position in it
10-15%    Magic/power exists            Show it in action (not explanation)
15-20%    Basic rules of magic          Mentor teaches OR protagonist discovers
20-30%    Political landscape           Conflict forces protagonist to navigate it
30-40%    History that matters           A character argues about the past
40-50%    Deeper magic rules            Training/practice scenes under pressure
50-60%    The dark side of the world    Consequences reveal hidden costs
60-70%    Historical truth shifts        Discovery reframes earlier knowledge
70-80%    Final rules revealed           What was hidden becomes clear
80-95%    Everything converges           Reader has all pieces for the climax
95-100%   New questions raised           The world is bigger than this book
```

**Golden rule:** If you can cut a world-building passage and the scene still works, the world-building is decoration. It should be load-bearing. Every piece of lore, every rule, every historical detail should connect to character, conflict, or theme. If it doesn't, save it for the appendix.
