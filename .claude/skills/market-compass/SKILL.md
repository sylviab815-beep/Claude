# Market Compass: Pre-Writing Commercial Viability Analyzer

## WHAT THIS SKILL DOES

- Evaluates a book concept's commercial viability BEFORE the author writes it
- Saves authors from spending 3-6 months writing a book that has no market
- Provides actionable market intelligence, not just opinions
- Delivers a Go/No-Go recommendation with specific reasoning
- If "No-Go" -- provides pivot suggestions to make the concept viable
- This is the business brain that authors often lack or ignore

## WHY THIS MATTERS

Most indie authors skip market research entirely. They write the book, THEN figure out how to sell it. This is backwards. The most successful indie authors validate their concept before they outline. This skill brings that discipline to every book.

The goal is NOT to kill creativity -- it's to make sure the creative investment has a commercial path. Sometimes the answer is "write it anyway" -- but the author should make that choice with eyes open.

---

## PHASE 1: CONCEPT INTAKE

Before analyzing anything, gather the following from the author. Ask direct questions. If they don't know an answer, help them figure it out -- that's part of the value.

**Required information:**

1. **Book concept / premise** -- even a rough idea works. A sentence, a paragraph, a rambling voice note transcription. Work with what they have.
2. **Genre and subgenre** -- help them get specific. "Romance" is not specific enough. Push until you reach something like "small-town contemporary romance with a second-chance trope" or "dark fantasy romantasy with fated mates." If they're unsure, suggest 2-3 subgenres that fit their description and let them pick.
3. **Target word count** -- if they don't know, suggest the standard range for their subgenre.
4. **Heat level** (for romance, 1-5 scale):
   - 1 = Sweet/clean (closed door, no explicit content)
   - 2 = Mild (kissing, fade-to-black)
   - 3 = Moderate (some steam, not highly explicit)
   - 4 = Steamy (explicit scenes, integral to story)
   - 5 = Dark/erotic (frequent explicit content, may include kink/taboo)
5. **Tropes in play** -- list every trope they're considering. If they can't name tropes, describe the story dynamics and identify the tropes for them.
6. **Series or standalone** -- and if series, how many books planned, what links them (same couple? Same world? Interconnected standalones?)
7. **Publishing path:**
   - KDP exclusive (Kindle Unlimited enrollment)
   - Wide distribution (multiple retailers: Kobo, Apple, B&N, Google Play, etc.)
   - Hybrid (some books exclusive, some wide)
   - Querying agents / traditional publishing
8. **Author's existing platform:**
   - First book ever? Or backlist size?
   - Newsletter subscriber count
   - Social media following (and which platforms)
   - Any existing readership or ARC team?
9. **Comparable authors they admire** -- who do they want to write like? Whose career do they want to emulate?
10. **Hard constraints** -- anything non-negotiable:
    - Must be clean/sweet
    - Must be under 60K words
    - Cannot include certain content
    - Must fit an existing series brand
    - Timeline constraints (NaNoWriMo, rapid release schedule, etc.)

**If the author can only provide items 1-3, that's enough to start.** Fill in the gaps with reasonable assumptions and flag them as assumptions the author should confirm.

## PHASE 2: COMP TITLE RESEARCH

Identify and analyze 5-8 comparable titles. This is the foundation of the entire analysis -- get this right and everything else follows.

### For Each Comp Title, Provide:

- **Title, Author, Publication Date** -- full citation
- **Amazon Best Seller Rank (BSR) range** -- and what it tells us:
  - BSR under 10,000 = strong daily sales
  - BSR 10,000-50,000 = solid, consistent seller
  - BSR 50,000-100,000 = moderate sales, typical mid-list
  - BSR 100,000+ = slow sales or backlist trickle
  - Note: BSR fluctuates. A snapshot is useful but not definitive. Look for patterns.
- **Price point** -- ebook, paperback, audiobook (if available)
- **Page count / estimated word count** -- KENP if in KU
- **Category placement** -- which Amazon browse categories is it listed in?
- **Review count and average rating** -- proxy for readership size:
  - 50+ reviews = has found an audience
  - 500+ reviews = solid performer
  - 5,000+ reviews = breakout or established author
- **What's working** -- analyze the cover, blurb, trope combination, series position, pricing, marketing visible from the listing
- **What's not working** -- read the negative reviews. What disappointed readers? These are gold for understanding reader expectations.
- **KU vs. wide** -- is this comp enrolled in Kindle Unlimited?

### Comp Selection Rules:

- At least 3 comps published in the last 2 years (market relevance)
- Mix of breakout hits AND solid mid-list performers (don't only pick #1 bestsellers -- that's aspirational, not comparable)
- At least 1 comp from a similarly-positioned author (debut or small-platform indie, not just trad-pub juggernauts with Big 5 marketing budgets)
- Include at least 1 comp that's underperforming -- understanding what DOESN'T work is as valuable as seeing what does
- If you cannot find 5 close comps, that's a signal. Analyze which:
  - **Red flag:** No market exists for this concept. Readers aren't looking for it.
  - **Opportunity:** Untapped niche with reader demand but low supply. Validate by checking search volume, Reddit threads, BookTok requests, and reader forum posts asking for "books like X."

### Comp Analysis Summary:

After individual breakdowns, provide:
- The price range these comps cluster around
- The word count range
- The trope patterns (which tropes appear in 3+ comps)
- The cover pattern (visual commonalities)
- The blurb pattern (structural commonalities)
- Where the author's concept fits -- and where it diverges

## PHASE 3: CATEGORY & KEYWORD ANALYSIS

### Amazon Category Research

- **Primary category recommendation** -- full browse path (e.g., Kindle Store > Kindle eBooks > Romance > Contemporary > New Adult & College)
- **Secondary category recommendation** -- full browse path
- **Category competition assessment:**
  - How many books are in this category?
  - What BSR gets you on page 1? (Top 20 in the category)
  - What does page 1 look like? (All trad-pub? Mix? Indie-friendly?)
- **Niche vs. broad strategy:**
  - Narrow categories = less competition, but less traffic
  - Broad categories = more traffic, but harder to rank
  - Best approach: start narrow to build rank, then request broader category placement once you have momentum

### Keyword Research

Identify 7-10 keyword phrases readers actually search. For each keyword:

- **The phrase itself** (exactly as a reader would type it)
- **Search volume reasoning** -- high, medium, or low based on category size, trend data, and autocomplete suggestions
- **Competition level** -- how many books target this keyword?
- **Relevance to this concept** -- direct match, adjacent, or stretch?

**Keyword types to cover:**
- **Trope-based:** "enemies to lovers romance," "forced proximity thriller," "fated mates fantasy"
- **Setting-based:** "small town romance," "space opera," "Regency England"
- **Tone-based:** "dark romance," "cozy mystery," "clean romance," "funny rom-com"
- **Comp-based:** "books like [comp author]," "similar to [comp title]"
- **Temporal:** "[genre] 2026," "new [genre] releases"
- **Reader-need:** "romance with strong heroine," "mystery with no gore," "fantasy with map"

### Category Strategy

- **BISAC categories** for wide distribution (these differ from Amazon browse categories)
- **Amazon-specific browse nodes** -- the internal category IDs
- **Dual-category strategy** -- how to maximize visibility with primary + secondary placement
- **When to change categories post-launch** -- timing and rationale for category swaps after initial launch momentum

## PHASE 4: COVER DIRECTION

Based on what is actively selling in the target category right now.

### Current Cover Trends
- What do the top 20 books in this category look like right now?
- What changed in the last 12 months? (Trends shift fast -- what worked in 2024 may look dated in 2026)

### Must-Have Signals
- Genre markers the cover MUST contain to be clicked by the target reader
- Examples: shirtless man = steamy romance, dark moody colors = thriller, illustrated couple = rom-com, ornate typography = fantasy
- These are not suggestions. Missing a genre signal means the right reader scrolls past.

### Must-Avoid Signals
- Elements that signal a DIFFERENT genre or reader
- A pastel watercolor cover on a dark romance will attract the wrong reader and repel the right one
- Mismatched signals = bad reviews from readers who expected something else

### Typography Direction
- Font style (serif, sans-serif, script, decorative)
- Title placement (top, center, bottom)
- Author name sizing (bigger for established authors, smaller for debuts)
- Series branding (how to visually connect books in a series)

### Color Palette
- What colors dominate the category right now?
- What would stand out WITHOUT looking like the wrong genre?
- Seasonal considerations (holiday releases, summer reads)

### Style Recommendation
- **Illustration** -- trending in rom-com, cozy, YA
- **Photography/stock images** -- standard in contemporary romance, thriller
- **Typography-forward** -- common in literary fiction, some thrillers
- **Digital art/mixed media** -- growing in fantasy, sci-fi, romantasy
- Which style is working for THIS specific subgenre right now?

### Series Branding
- If applicable: how to create visual continuity across books
- Consistent elements (color scheme, font, layout, character illustration style)
- Numbered vs. unnumbered series -- how covers should signal reading order

### Budget Guidance
- **Premade covers:** $50-200. Best for: testing a new pen name, genre-standard concepts, tight budgets. Where to buy: quality premade cover sites.
- **Custom covers:** $300-800+. Best for: series branding, unique concepts, established authors investing in their brand.
- **What to look for in a cover designer** who works in this subgenre
- **Red flags** in cover designers (no genre experience, won't show recent comps, prices far below market)

## PHASE 5: BLURB FRAMEWORK

Based on what's working for the comp titles analyzed in Phase 2.

### Hook Formula
Study the top-performing blurbs in this subgenre. What's the pattern?
- Question hook? ("What happens when...")
- Situation hook? ("She never expected...")
- Stakes hook? ("One chance to...")
- Voice hook? (Character voice in the blurb itself)

### Blurb Structure by Genre

**Romance:**
1. Character introduction (who is she/he, what's their wound or situation?)
2. Inciting situation (what throws them together?)
3. Conflict/stakes (what keeps them apart?)
4. Tease (the emotional promise without spoiling)
5. Trope signal line (optional but effective: "A second-chance, small-town romance with all the heat and heart.")

**Thriller/Suspense:**
1. The threat or mystery (what's wrong?)
2. The protagonist (who's in danger or investigating?)
3. The ticking clock (why does it matter NOW?)
4. The question (what will they sacrifice/discover?)

**Fantasy/Sci-Fi:**
1. The world (one line that grounds us)
2. The character (who matters and why)
3. The quest or conflict (what must be done?)
4. The stakes (what happens if they fail?)

**Cozy Mystery:**
1. The setting (charming location)
2. The protagonist (relatable amateur sleuth)
3. The murder/mystery (the hook)
4. The complication (why it's personal)

### Trope Signaling
- Which tropes to name-drop explicitly (readers search for these)
- Which tropes to imply but not name (let the reader discover them)
- Trope order: lead with the most marketable trope

### Heat Signaling (Romance)
How comp titles signal their heat level in blurbs without being explicit:
- Clean: emphasizes "heartwarming," "sweet," "wholesome"
- Moderate: "chemistry," "sparks," "tension"
- Steamy: "burning," "irresistible," "between the sheets"
- Dark/high heat: "consumed," "possession," "no limits," content warnings

### Keyword Integration
- Weave 2-3 target keywords into the blurb naturally
- Amazon indexes blurb text for search -- this matters for discoverability
- Don't keyword-stuff. It has to read well first, optimize second.

### Draft Blurb
Based on the concept provided in Phase 1, write a draft blurb:
- Clearly marked as DRAFT / CONCEPT-STAGE
- Follows the structure identified above
- Includes trope signals and keyword integration
- Formatted for Amazon (short paragraphs, line breaks, no walls of text)
- Two versions if appropriate: one for Amazon listing, one shorter version for social media / ads

## PHASE 6: READER EXPECTATION MAP

For the target reader of this specific book:

### Who Is This Reader?
- Demographics (age range, gender skew if any)
- Reading habits (how many books per month, KU subscriber, library user, buyer)
- Where they hang out online (BookTok, Bookstagram, Reddit, Facebook groups, Goodreads, genre-specific forums)
- How they discover new books (also-boughts, newsletter swaps, BookTok recs, Amazon browse, Bookbub deals)

### What Do They Expect From This Subgenre?
Non-negotiable promises the book MUST deliver:
- Romance: HEA or HFN (happily ever after or happy for now) is non-negotiable in genre romance
- Mystery: the mystery must be solved
- Thriller: the pacing must be relentless
- Fantasy: the world must be internally consistent
- Each subgenre has its own set of promises. Name them specifically.

### What Are Their Deal-Breakers?
Content or craft issues that will trigger DNFs (did not finish) or 1-star reviews:
- Cheating heroes in contemporary romance
- Animal death in cozy mysteries
- Slow pacing in thrillers
- Lazy world-building in fantasy
- Source this from negative reviews of comp titles -- these are reader expectations stated explicitly

### Content Warnings Expected
- What does this reader expect to be warned about?
- What content warning format does this subgenre typically use? (Front matter? Author website? Goodreads listing?)
- Missing content warnings = angry reviews. Over-warning = spoilers. Find the balance for this subgenre.

### Pacing Expectations
- Fast burn or slow burn?
- Action-heavy or character-heavy?
- How long should chapters be?
- Multiple POV or single POV?
- What comp title's pacing should the author study?

### Ending Expectations
- HEA required? (Romance: yes, always)
- Open ending acceptable? (Literary fiction: often. Genre fiction: rarely.)
- Cliffhanger tolerance? (Series fantasy: moderate. Romance: low unless clearly marketed as serialized.)
- Series arc vs. book arc -- does each book need to feel complete?

### Series Expectations
- Does this reader prefer standalones or series?
- If series: interconnected standalones or continuing arc?
- How long will they wait between books? (Romance readers: 3-4 months max. Fantasy readers: more patient.)
- Do they binge? (KU readers binge. Wide readers may not.)

## PHASE 7: REVENUE PROJECTION

Honest, realistic estimates. Not hype. Not doom. Math.

### Factors Considered:
- Author platform size (newsletter, social following, backlist)
- Genre average performance for debut vs. established backlist
- Pricing strategy (higher price wide vs. lower price in KU)
- Marketing budget availability
- Series potential (series almost always outperforms standalones in indie)

### Projection Tiers:

**Conservative (Floor)**
- Minimal marketing, new author, standalone, small or no list
- Estimate units sold and gross revenue for first 90 days and first 12 months
- This is the "what if nothing goes viral and I just publish and do basic promo" scenario

**Moderate (Realistic)**
- Some marketing spend ($200-500/month on ads), small newsletter (500-2,000), series potential
- Estimate units and revenue for 90 days and 12 months
- This is the "I'm doing the work but I'm not a full-time marketer" scenario

**Optimistic (Stretch)**
- Active marketing, growing newsletter (2,000+), series with rapid release (book every 2-3 months), ad spend of $500-1,000/month
- Estimate units and revenue for 90 days and 12 months
- This is the "I'm treating this like a business and executing well" scenario

### Revenue Model Breakdown:

**Ebook Revenue:**
- Price x units x royalty rate
- 70% royalty for $2.99-$9.99 on KDP
- 35% royalty below $2.99 or above $9.99 on KDP
- Wide retailer royalty rates vary (typically 60-70%)

**KU Page Reads:**
- KENP (Kindle Edition Normalized Pages) x estimated full reads x rate
- Current KU per-page rate: approximately $0.004-0.005
- A 300-page KENP book fully read = ~$1.20-$1.50 per read
- KU borrows often exceed purchase revenue for genre fiction

**Paperback Revenue:**
- Print price x units x royalty (varies by page count, trim size, marketplace)
- Typical indie paperback royalty: $1-4 per unit
- Print is a smaller percentage of indie revenue but adds legitimacy and gifting potential

**Audio Potential:**
- ACX royalty share: 20% for 7-year exclusive (avoid this if possible)
- ACX royalty share plus: 25% with promo code commitment
- Rights holder flat rate: pay narrator upfront ($150-400/finished hour), keep 40% royalty
- AI narration: lower cost, growing acceptance, platform-dependent policies
- Audio can be 10-30% of total book revenue for popular genres

### Important Caveats (State These Directly):
- These are educated estimates, not guarantees. Nobody can predict book sales with certainty.
- First books rarely earn back the time investment on pure unit economics alone -- but they're the foundation for everything that follows.
- Series revenue compounds: book 2 sells book 1. Book 3 sells books 1 and 2. A 5-book series earns exponentially more than 5 standalones.
- Long-tail matters: books earn for years, not just launch month. A "slow" launch doesn't mean a failed book.
- Backlist is the real money in indie publishing. The first book is an investment in future revenue.

## PHASE 8: GO / NO-GO ASSESSMENT

The verdict. Delivered directly. No hedging, no corporate speak.

### GO
This concept has a clear market path. Here's the strategy.
- The specific market conditions that support this concept
- The competitive advantage this book has (unique angle, underserved trope combination, timing)
- The biggest risk and how to mitigate it
- Recommended next steps, in order:
  1. Finalize outline with reader expectations in mind
  2. Commission cover (or select premade)
  3. Write to target word count
  4. Execute launch strategy (provide specifics)

### GO WITH ADJUSTMENTS
The core concept works, but it needs sharpening. This is the most common verdict -- most concepts need tweaking to maximize their commercial potential.
- What specifically needs to change:
  - Trope swap? (Replace a weak trope with a trending one)
  - Heat adjustment? (Market wants hotter/cleaner than proposed)
  - Category shift? (Better market fit in an adjacent subgenre)
  - Word count adjustment? (Too long or too short for the category)
  - Series pivot? (Standalone should be a series, or vice versa)
- Why the current version is weaker than it could be
- The adjusted concept and its improved market path
- What stays the same (protect the author's creative vision where it doesn't conflict with market realities)

### NO-GO
This concept doesn't have a viable commercial path right now. Say it plainly.
- Why -- specific market reasons:
  - Saturated category with no differentiation
  - No reader demand (can't find comps, can't find search volume, no reader discussions)
  - Concept conflicts with reader expectations for the target genre
  - Timing is wrong (trend has peaked, or hasn't arrived yet)
- What would need to change for this concept to work
- Alternative concepts that use the author's same skills, interests, and voice but have better market fit
- The honest "write it anyway" option: if the author loves this concept, acknowledge that some books are passion projects and that's valid. But they should go in knowing this is a creative investment, not a commercial one. No false hope, no crushing dreams -- just clarity.

### RISKY BUT INTERESTING
The market data doesn't fully support it, but there's something here worth exploring.
- What makes it risky:
  - Thin market (small readership, niche audience)
  - Saturated category with entrenched competitors
  - Emerging genre with uncertain staying power
  - Concept requires a readership that may not exist yet
- What makes it interesting:
  - Gap in the market that readers are requesting
  - Emerging trend with early-mover advantage
  - Unique angle that could create its own category
  - Cross-genre appeal that's underserved
- Strategy for de-risking:
  - Write a novella or short first to test audience response
  - Pre-launch validation (reader survey, social media polling, cover test ads)
  - Start with KU to minimize reader risk (free to read via subscription)
  - Build audience around the concept before writing the full book

## OUTPUT FORMAT

The final deliverable should be structured as follows. Each section should be thorough but scannable -- use headers, bullet points, and bold text for key findings. The author should be able to skim the headers and get the verdict, then dive deep where they need detail.

### Deliverable Sections:

1. **Concept Summary** -- premise, genre, tropes, target reader, publishing path (1 paragraph)
2. **Comp Title Analysis** -- 5-8 comps with full breakdown per the Phase 2 framework
3. **Category & Keyword Strategy** -- recommended categories, 7-10 keywords with reasoning, placement strategy
4. **Cover Direction Brief** -- trends, must-have signals, palette, typography, budget guidance
5. **Blurb Framework + Draft** -- structure for this subgenre, trope/heat signaling, draft blurb
6. **Reader Expectation Map** -- who the reader is, what they demand, what they won't tolerate
7. **Revenue Projection** -- conservative/moderate/optimistic tiers with methodology shown
8. **Go/No-Go Verdict** -- the assessment with full reasoning and prioritized next steps

### Formatting Rules:
- Use clear headers and subheaders
- Bold key findings and action items
- Use tables for comp title comparisons where helpful
- Keep paragraphs short (3-4 sentences max)
- End with a prioritized action list the author can execute immediately

### Tone:
- Direct. No fluff.
- Honest. If the concept is weak, say so and explain why.
- Constructive. Every "no" comes with a "but here's what could work."
- Respectful of the author's creative vision while being realistic about the market.
- Data-driven where possible, clearly labeled as opinion where it's judgment.
