## 1. Reverse Harem (RH) / Why Choose

**Sub-genre conventions:**

- True RH = HEA with all romantic interests; protagonist never picks
- Why Choose = same; the term is current preferred genre-shelf language
- Typical MMC count: 3-5
- POV: first-person, often present tense; multi-POV common
- Length: 80-130k Book 1 typical; series 3-7 books common
- Heat: explicit on-page is genre default; clean RH exists but is a niche

**Trope checklist (Book 1 must hit most):**

- Each romantic interest has a distinct trope archetype (broken bad-boy, sunshine, doomed warrior, cold-prince, etc.)
- First-charged-proximity per romantic interest, planted early
- First-kiss per romantic interest in Book 1 (or at minimum in the first half of the series)
- First-on-page-spice per romantic interest in Book 1 for explicit RH
- Possessiveness with stakes (canonical bond mechanic preferred)
- Jealousy beats — but jealousy that resolves into MMC-vs-MMC bonding, not violence
- Group/shared spice scheduled for Book 1 or Book 2 (genre default; deferred is fine if signaled)
- Claim language ("mine," "yours," "say it") in spice scenes
- Praise / worship / bratty register differentiated per romantic interest
- Wound-tending scene per romantic interest at minimum
- Found-family beat — the men become a unit *because of her*
- Class/station forbidden-pairing trope often present
- Mate-mark / soul-tether / bond mechanic visible on-page

**DNF triggers (Book 1):**

- Heroine apologizing for being herself or for the men's feelings
- An MMC making a major decision about her body without consent (forced bond, surprise mating, etc.)
- Fated mate used as consent override
- Heroine ranks the MMCs in voice — that's love-triangle voice, not Why Choose
- Heroine kissing or sleeping with an MMC she barely knows (under ~3 charged interactions)
- An MMC physically harming the heroine (even "by accident") without major arc reckoning
- MMC violence against another MMC over the heroine
- A "primary" MMC implied — RH readers want parity
- Group scene without prior dynamic setup
- Heroine forgives a true betrayal without sufficient reckoning

**Common RH outline bugs:**

- One MMC has dramatically more page time / buildup than others
- Heat-cadence per MMC is uneven (one gets first kiss + first spice + bond all in 10 chapters, another's full arc spans 40 chapters)
- Forbidden-pairing dynamic implied but not pressed in Book 1
- Bond mechanic introduced but never visualized on the page
- No same-sex friend for the heroine — reads brittle
- Each MMC has a "thing" but the things don't differentiate enough
- Antagonist's threat doesn't escalate proportionally to the heroine's bonding pace

**Heat-curve math (RH explicit):**

For Book 1 with 3 MMCs at ~100k:
- First kiss per MMC by ~50% mark
- First on-page spice per MMC by ~75% mark
- First bond/Tether/mate-mark moment in the back third
- Group scene Book 2 typical (or late Book 1 if signaled hard)

If only 2 of 3 MMCs hit first-spice in Book 1, the third should be promised by an explicit on-page heat moment so readers don't feel shortchanged.

## 2. Romantasy

**Sub-genre conventions:**

- Single primary love interest (sometimes a love triangle that resolves)
- Fantasy world with romance-as-engine
- POV: usually first-person past or third-limited
- Length: 100-180k Book 1 typical (often longer than RH)
- Heat: 3-5 chili common; explicit growing as default
- Series: 2-5 books common

**Trope checklist:**

- Strong worldbuilding setup
- Slow-burn or enemies-to-lovers most common
- Magic system tied to romance/bond
- Court / political intrigue often present
- A "fated" element (mates, prophesied, marked)
- Heroine has agency in the magic, not just the romance
- Stakes are political AND personal AND magical

**DNF triggers:**

- Heroine has no power of her own (everything magical is the love interest's domain)
- Romance overrides plot for chapters at a time
- Worldbuilding info-dumped through dialogue
- Prophecy used as plot crutch — heroine doesn't have to choose
- Love interest is simply "broken bad boy" with no specificity
- Magical solution arrives at climax without setup

**Common bugs:**

- Acts I-II are too slow (worldbuilding + romance setup eats first half)
- Heat curve too gentle for current market (genre is trending more explicit)
- Heroine-as-prophesied-one without enough agency in the prophecy
- Antagonist is a system/empire abstraction without a face

## 3. Dark Academia

**Sub-genre conventions:**

- Setting is a school/university with dark undertones
- Often a found-family group of brilliant outsiders
- Mystery or transgression at the center (hidden murder, secret society, forbidden knowledge)
- Tone: ornate, atmospheric, often gothic
- POV: usually first-past, single
- Length: 80-120k typical
- Heat: often muted (literary register) but trending explicit when crossed with RH/romantasy
- Often standalone or duology

**Trope checklist:**

- Atmospheric setting (the school is a character)
- A small group of intense friends/lovers
- A secret or transgression that shapes the plot
- Class/privilege tensions
- Books, language, ritual as texture
- An outsider protagonist
- Aesthetic-specific objects (oxfords, libraries, ivy, candles)

**DNF triggers:**

- Setting is named "academy" but feels generic
- Aesthetic without intellectual substance
- Found family declared but never earned in scene
- Mystery resolved by external rescue (not the protagonist's reasoning)

**Common bugs:**

- Atmosphere overrides plot — beautiful prose, no escalation
- Found family told, not shown
- The school is a backdrop, not a participant
- Mystery is visible to the reader long before the protagonist

## 4. Paranormal Romance (single-MMC)

**Sub-genre conventions:**

- Single love interest who is a paranormal being (vampire, werewolf, ghost, demon, fae, etc.)
- POV: usually first-past or third-limited
- Length: 80-110k typical
- Heat: explicit common
- Series: connected standalones common (each book a different couple)

**Trope checklist:**

- Mate-bond / soul-tether mechanic
- Possessive love interest
- Heroine introduced to a hidden world
- Mortality / immortality stakes
- A territorial or political conflict in the paranormal world

**DNF triggers:**

- Heroine has no agency in the paranormal world's politics
- Mate bond used as consent shortcut
- Love interest's "danger" is performative, not real
- Heroine-as-savior-by-existence (not by action)

**Common bugs:**

- Romance arc resolves too early; back half loses tension
- Worldbuilding rules trip during spice scenes
- Heroine's mortal life vanishes from the page once paranormal world enters

## 5. Cozy Mystery

**Sub-genre conventions:**

- Amateur sleuth in a small community
- Murder happens off-page or politely
- Tone: warm, observational, often comedic
- POV: usually first-past, single
- Length: 70-90k typical
- Heat: usually clean or sweet
- Series: long-running (8-30+ books) common

**Trope checklist:**

- A defined small community with recurring side characters
- An amateur sleuth with a reason to investigate
- Red herrings
- A clue-trail the reader can follow
- Comfort-food sensory texture (food, place, hobby)

**DNF triggers:**

- Graphic violence on the page
- Sex scenes (clean is the norm; explicit is a wrong-shelf signal)
- The sleuth solves by happenstance, not reasoning
- A side character is the killer with no fair-play planting

**Common bugs:**

- Clue-placement uneven — some clues plant, others surface from nowhere
- Sleuth's "reason to investigate" is thin
- Red herring is too obviously a red herring
- Side character bench too small to support series longevity

## 6. Thriller / Crime

**Sub-genre conventions:**

- High-stakes plot, often life-and-death
- Pacing tight; chapters often short
- POV: variable; multi-POV common; first-present rising
- Length: 80-110k typical
- Heat: usually background, not central
- Series: ongoing detective common; standalones for psychological

**Trope checklist:**

- Inciting incident very early (Ch 1-3)
- A ticking clock or escalating threat
- A protagonist with a personal stake in the case
- Twists planted, not bolted on
- A worthy antagonist
- Tight chapter-end hooks

**DNF triggers:**

- Inciting incident later than Ch 5
- Long stretches without escalation
- Twist that isn't fairly planted
- Protagonist is purely passive
- Magical/coincidence resolution

**Common bugs:**

- Pacing flattens in middle act
- Antagonist screen-time too thin
- Twist signaled too clearly OR not planted at all
- Protagonist's personal stake fades after Act I

## 7. Literary Fiction

**Sub-genre conventions:**

- Plot serves character and theme
- Voice is foreground
- POV: variable
- Length: 60-110k typical
- Heat: variable
- Standalone is dominant

**Trope checklist:**

- A clear thematic question
- Characters with specific interiorities
- Setting / time / culture that shapes the story
- A reckoning or transformation by the end
- Prose that earns attention

**DNF triggers (genre-specific):**

- Plot mechanics overshadowing character
- Theme stated rather than dramatized
- Generic setting
- Sentimentality without earning it

**Common bugs:**

- Outline is character-rich but plot-thin — book may resist forward motion
- Theme is stated in the outline but not embedded in beats
- Ending is reckoning-without-cost

## 8. Epic Fantasy

**Sub-genre conventions:**

- Large-scale conflict (war, succession, world-ending threat)
- Multiple POVs common
- Worldbuilding extensive
- POV: usually third-limited, multi-POV
- Length: 120-250k Book 1 typical
- Heat: variable, often muted
- Series: 3-10+ books

**Trope checklist:**

- Multiple POV characters with their own arcs
- A magic system with explicit rules
- Geography that matters
- Political/cultural complexity
- A central threat that requires scale

**DNF triggers:**

- Magic system rules broken for plot convenience
- POV characters introduced and abandoned
- Worldbuilding info-dumped
- Climax resolved by deus ex machina

**Common bugs:**

- Too many POV characters too early
- World-detail-per-page ratio overwhelming
- Subplots compete with main plot for screen time
- Book 1 doesn't have a satisfying internal arc within the larger series

## 9. Contemporary Romance

**Sub-genre conventions:**

- Real-world setting
- Single love interest
- POV: dual-POV common (his/hers)
- Length: 70-100k typical
- Heat: variable, sub-genre dependent (sweet → spicy)
- Standalone or connected standalones

**Trope checklist:**

- Meet-cute or meet-disaster
- Forced proximity, fake-dating, friends-to-lovers, second-chance, etc. — pick one and lean
- Internal conflict equal to or greater than external
- Black moment that tests the relationship
- Grand-gesture or reckoning at the end

**DNF triggers:**

- Internal conflict feels manufactured (no character truth behind it)
- Black moment resolved by a coincidence
- One partner does the emotional labor for both
- Epilogue overrides the book's actual character arc

**Common bugs:**

- Trope deployed without specifics — "fake dating" with no unique angle
- Internal conflict resolves by external event
- Side characters carry the comedy / emotional weight while the leads underdeliver

## 10. Young Adult (YA) — overlay

YA isn't a genre on its own; it's an audience overlay. Apply on top of any of the above. Specifics to check:

- Protagonist age 14-18
- Adult characters not driving the resolution
- Coming-of-age arc embedded
- Heat ceiling lower than adult version of same sub-genre
- Voice register accessible to teen readers without being condescending

**DNF triggers (YA):**

- Adult characters making the climax decisions
- Sex scenes outside YA-permitted heat range
- Protagonist whose problems require an adult to solve
- Voice that talks down to teen readers

---

## A note on hybrid genres

Many current bestsellers are hybrids — dark academia romantasy, RH paranormal, thriller-with-romance-elements. When critiquing, read all relevant sections. Hybrids inherit DNF triggers and trope checklists from each parent genre. Clearing one genre's checklist while triggering another's DNF list is the most common hybrid bug.
