# CRITIQUE CATEGORIES

The 20-category audit framework. Run each in order on the outline. Skip a category in your written critique if it's clean — don't pad with "looks fine" notes. Every category here exists because it has cost authors hours of post-draft revision when missed.

## 1. Structure

**What to check:**
- Act balance: do the acts roughly fit standard ratios for the genre? (Three-act ~25/50/25; five-act varies; RH/romantasy often back-loads payoffs.)
- Hinge placement: is there a clear midpoint shift? An end-of-Act-II low point? A Convergence/black-moment beat?
- Climax shape: is the climax earned by setup planted in Acts I-III?
- Cliffhanger shape (if series): does the book end on an irrevocable change?
- Five-act vs. three-act structural confusion (some outlines hybrid in ways that break).

**Bug patterns:**
- "Act I is Ch 1-15 and Act II is Ch 16-22" — Acts II is too short; midpoint will read missing
- Climax beat appears with no setup payoff — reader feels manipulated
- The outline lists chapter beats but never says what the *ending* is — author hasn't decided the ending
- Pre-climactic act and post-climactic resolution are imbalanced — e.g., 40 chapters of buildup, 2 chapters of resolution

**Proposed-fix template:** "Compress Act X from N chapters to M by [specific moves]." / "Add a midpoint hinge at Ch X — [specific event]." / "Lock the ending shape before drafting; current outline ambiguous on whether [event] resolves or persists."

## 2. Pacing

**What to check:**
- Chapter cadence: do beats come at consistent intervals or clump?
- Slow spots: which acts have stretches of medium-stakes maneuvering?
- Where exposition clumps: information delivered in dialogue blocks or info-dumps vs. in scenes that DO things
- How often the protagonist is *acting* vs. *being acted upon* (links to Category 8)
- First-major-event timing — for fast-paced genres, is the inciting incident early enough?

**Bug patterns:**
- Inciting incident at Ch 8 in a fast-paced thriller (too late)
- First-kiss at Ch 25 of 50 in spicy RH (too late by genre norms)
- Acts II-III have 12 chapters of medium-stakes Politicking before the next escalation
- Major plot reveal at Ch 35 with no prior planting

**Proposed-fix template:** "Move [event] from Ch X to Ch Y." / "Compress Ch A-D into a single chapter — they share the same beat at different scales." / "Add a stakes-bump in [act] between Ch X and Ch Y."

## 3. Word-count math

**What to check:**
- Total target word count vs. chapter count (does avg chapter length make sense?)
- Big-scene budget: each first-spice ≈ 4-5k, formal/ball ≈ 3-4k, climax ≈ 4-5k, opening ≈ 2-3k. Sum these and check total.
- Back-loaded vs. front-loaded math: are Acts I-III's chapters going to need to be very short to leave budget for the back half?
- Realistic chapter count given the budget

**Bug patterns:**
- 50 chapters at 90k = 1.8k avg, but the outline has six 4-5k showcase chapters in Acts IV-V → other chapters need to average 1.4k → drafting reality will push to 110k+
- Outline targets 80k but covers four full romantic arcs + cosmology + plot escalation → underestimated by 20-30k
- Author says "fast-paced" and outlines 130k → fast-paced ceiling is generally 90-100k

**Proposed-fix template:** "Target word count is [X] but the showcase chapters alone budget [Y]. Either increase target to [Z] or cut [specific beats]." / "Acts I-III chapters need to average [N]k — flag for tight prose discipline."

## 4. Genre expectations

**What to check:**
- Sub-genre conventions: does the outline hit the sub-genre's structural and beat-level norms?
- Comp-title alignment: does the outline read like it would shelve next to its stated comps?
- Sub-shelf placement: is there a clear answer to "where does this book live in a bookstore"?
- Market-position fit: heat level, length, POV, tense, pen-name positioning

Read the relevant section of GENRE_CHECKLISTS.md for sub-genre-specific norms.

**Bug patterns:**
- Outline says "RH" but the heroine ends with one MMC → that's not RH, it's a love-triangle resolution
- Outline says "cozy mystery" but the body count is six → that's a thriller
- Comp titles are *Game of Thrones* and *The Hunger Games* (genre-incoherent)
- Length and pacing don't match the comp's reader expectations

**Proposed-fix template:** "Outline is sub-genre-incoherent — calling it [X] but it reads as [Y]. Pick: rebrand to [Y] OR rework [specific beats] to fit [X]." / "Comp titles need lock — closest fit based on the outline is [comp 1] and [comp 2]."

## 5. Heat expectations (romance-adjacent only)

Skip this category if the outline is non-romance. If romance-adjacent, check:

**What to check:**
- Heat ceiling locked (clean / sweet / steamy / explicit / 5-chili)
- Per-romantic-interest cadence: first-charged-proximity, first-kiss, first-on-page-spice, first-bond-moment timing
- Claim-language / possessive beats per romantic interest
- Group/shared scenes scheduled or deferred (RH/why-choose specific)
- Anatomical vocabulary register (clinical vs. softer)
- Aftercare register (warm and present vs. terse)
- Verbal possession beats ("yours / say it")
- Praise / worship / bratty register per character

**Bug patterns:**
- Outline says "5-chili explicit" but no on-page spice scenes are scheduled
- Three MMCs but only one has a first-spice scene in Book 1 — readers will demand parity
- Group scene scheduled in Book 1 with no setup of the dynamic that would make it work
- "Why Choose" promised but heroine narrates ranking the men — that's love-triangle voice, not Why Choose

**Proposed-fix template:** "Heat ceiling [X] — schedule first-spice for [character] in Acts [N]." / "Group scene needs setup — add [specific dynamic beats] before deploying."

## 6. Cosmology / magic-system coherence

**What to check:**
- Rules introduced and consistently applied
- No rule introduced after Ch 30 that wasn't planted earlier
- No rule violated by spice-scene logistics (a common bug — a bond mechanic that should fire during sex but doesn't, or fires accidentally)
- Lore delivered through *scenes that do something,* not info-dumps
- Names of mechanics consistent throughout (no drift between *the Sigil* and *the Mark*)
- If multiple sources of magic, they don't trip each other

**Bug patterns:**
- "Bond forms when two partners' Marks consent" planted Ch 5; Ch 28 has a bond form against a partner's stated wishes — rule violated
- Outline introduces a "void-stone basin" Ch 47 with no prior setup
- Mechanic A and Mechanic B have overlapping rules that aren't reconciled
- Rule X is needed for Ch 30 plot but the rule isn't compatible with Ch 12's mechanic

**Proposed-fix template:** "Rule [X] introduced Ch [Y] with no prior planting — add seeds in Ch [A] and [B]." / "Mechanic [X] and [Y] conflict at Ch [Z]; reconcile by [specific change]."

## 7. Stakes ladder

**What to check:**
- Stakes climb across the book — each act has a higher high-water mark than the previous
- No accidental stakes deflation (a beat where the protagonist's situation actually improves without a corresponding new threat)
- Different *kinds* of stakes interleaved: body stakes, love stakes, institutional/political stakes, identity stakes, family stakes
- Stakes are felt (specific, located in characters who matter) not abstract

**Bug patterns:**
- Acts II-III have a 10-chapter stretch where stakes plateau
- Major reveal arrives but the reveal doesn't change what's at risk
- Stakes are abstract ("the world is at stake") with no specific person/object/relationship anchoring them
- A character is introduced as endangered, and chapters later they're forgotten

**Proposed-fix template:** "Add a stakes-bump in [act] — [specific event that raises the high-water mark]." / "[Character] is introduced as endangered Ch [X] but disappears from the threat math — either reincorporate or cut the threat."

## 8. Character agency

**What to check:**
- Protagonist's active beats vs. passive beats — count them, especially in Acts I-II
- Front-loaded vs. back-loaded agency (front-loading is genre-flexible; back-loaded only is a problem)
- Each major character has at least one beat where they choose to risk something *for* the protagonist unprompted
- Protagonist drives at least the climax decision — not driven into it

**Bug patterns:**
- Acts I-II have the protagonist getting recruited, getting attacked, getting summoned, getting kissed — all reactive
- The romantic interests choose, decide, act; the protagonist responds
- The climax happens TO the protagonist; she doesn't choose it
- Protagonist is rescued more often than she rescues

**Proposed-fix template:** "Add a proactive [protagonist] beat in Act [X] — [specific event where she goes after something instead of having it come to her]." / "Climax decision at Ch [X] needs to be [protagonist]'s, not [other character]'s."

## 9. Series architecture

Skip if standalone.

**What to check:**
- Series length specified (3-book, 5-book, etc.)
- Each later book sketched in at least two lines
- What threads are deliberately left unresolved for which later book
- Book 1 plants for Book N pay-offs are explicit
- Cliffhanger shape calibrated to the series length (a 5-book series cliffhanger differs from a duology's)

**Bug patterns:**
- Outline says "Book 2 picks up" without specifying what Book 2 is about
- Multiple unresolved threads at end of Book 1 with no plan for which book resolves what
- Series is implied but the publishing math (pen name positioning, series banner) isn't decided
- Threads planted that are too small to carry a full Book 2 plot

**Proposed-fix template:** "Lock series length and sketch each book in two lines before drafting Book 1." / "[Thread X] is too small to carry Book 2; either expand or mark as Book-3 background."

## 10. Theme / reader-promise

**What to check:**
- The book's theme expressible in one sentence
- The book's reader-promise expressible in one sentence (what will the reader feel by the end)
- Theme is reflected in major character arcs and the climax
- Promise is testable against every climactic beat

**Bug patterns:**
- Outline is plot-rich, trope-rich, theme-silent — the book isn't *about* anything yet
- Theme is articulated but doesn't show up in the climax
- Reader-promise mismatched with cover/blurb implications
- Theme reads as marketing copy, not character truth ("believe in yourself" is not a theme; "what you protect protects you" is)

**Proposed-fix template:** "Lock the theme in one sentence before structural revisions." / "[Theme X] doesn't land at the climax — climax beat needs to *answer* the theme question."

## 11. Side-character payoff

**What to check:**
- Every named character on the outline pays off in Book 1
- Characters who appear once and don't return are either cut or scheduled for series payoff
- Found-family / friend-circle density is calibrated to the genre (RH/romantasy needs at least one strong same-sex friend for the protagonist)
- Mentor figures pay off (don't introduce a wise mentor and abandon them)

**Bug patterns:**
- Named character introduced Ch 5 with promise of significance; doesn't reappear
- Protagonist has no female friends in a romance — reads brittle
- Mentor introduced Ch 7, removed Ch 32, never paid off in Book 1's climax
- Cast bloat — too many named side characters; reader can't track them

**Proposed-fix template:** "[Character X] introduced Ch [Y] but doesn't pay off — either give them a Book 1 climax beat or cut." / "Add same-sex friend density — protagonist needs one female confidante by Ch [X]."

## 12. Worldbuilding economy

**What to check:**
- Every lore mechanic on the outline is delivered through a scene that DOES something (advances plot, deepens character, escalates stakes)
- No info-dump chapters
- Lore quantity is calibrated to genre (literary fiction tolerates less; epic fantasy tolerates more)
- World-detail-per-page ratio appropriate for sub-genre

**Bug patterns:**
- Cosmology rule X is needed for Ch 30 but never delivered to the reader before then
- Chapter dedicated to "Astra learns the magic system" with no plot progression
- World-detail dumped in dialogue: "As you know, [character], the Council was formed three centuries ago when..."
- Mechanic not visible to the reader until it pays off — surprises read as cheats

**Proposed-fix template:** "Plant [mechanic X] in Ch [Y] through [specific scene that also does plot work]." / "Cosmology delivery is currently info-dump in Ch [Z]; redistribute as [embedded scenes]."

---

## 13. Chapter-end hook discipline

**What to check:**
- Each chapter ends on a propulsive last line, not just an event
- Chapter-end discipline serves both voice and genre
- No chapter ends with the protagonist falling asleep, eating dinner, or resolving comfortably
- Cliffhanger calibration: not every chapter needs a thriller-style cliffhanger, but every chapter needs *forward motion*

**Bug patterns:**
- Chapter ending: "She went to bed."
- Chapter ending: A character delivers a wise summary
- Multiple consecutive chapters ending on dialogue
- Chapter-end events that are just the END of a beat with no door opening to the next chapter

**Proposed-fix template:** "Audit chapter-ends in Acts [I-II] — each needs a propulsive last line, not just the event landing." / "Ch [X] ends on dialogue summary; reach for a forward-motion image instead."

## 14. DNF-trigger audit

Use the genre-specific section of GENRE_CHECKLISTS.md. Different genres have different dealbreakers. RH has its list; thriller has its; cozy has its.

**What to check (general):**
- Does the outline include any beats that match the genre's known DNF triggers?
- For each potential trigger, is the consent/agency framing right?
- Sensitive content handled appropriately for the genre's audience expectations

**Bug patterns:**
- (RH) Heroine apologizes for being herself
- (RH) MMC makes a major decision about her body without consent
- (RH) Fated mate used as consent override
- (Thriller) Protagonist is purely passive; villain drives all action
- (Cozy) On-page graphic violence
- (Romantasy) Heroine ranks the love interests in voice — reads "love triangle" and breaks "why choose" promise

**Proposed-fix template:** "Beat at Ch [X] is a known [genre] DNF trigger because [reason]; reframe by [specific change]."

## 15. Cliché-trap inventory

**What to check:**
- Beats that risk reading derivative if not handled with specific craft
- Common scene types that need specifics to land
- Lifted lines from the author's previous work or genre conventions
- "Standard" trope deployments that aren't given a unique angle

**Bug patterns:**
- "It's beautiful up close" / "Don't lose" / "I burned it down for you" — common heroine-lifting phrases that need character-specific anchoring
- Lamp goes out during sex — needs to be a *consequence* of magic, not weather
- Forced-proximity / one-bed scene with no twist on the trope
- Mean-girl-becomes-bestie pivot too clean
- Wound-tending scene with no character-specific intimacy

**Proposed-fix template:** "Beat at Ch [X] risks reading derivative; save it with [specific character-anchoring craft move]." / "Trope [X] deployed at Ch [Y] needs an angle — currently reads stock."

## 16. Voice-vs-genre register match

**What to check:**
- The prose voice (if known from the author's other work or the outline's voice samples) and the genre's pacing demands
- Chapter-end discipline serves both lyrical/atmospheric and propulsive registers
- No structural beats that would require a register the voice can't deliver
- Voice doesn't fight the genre's reader-expectations

**Bug patterns:**
- Lyrical-gothic voice + spicy-RH genre = atmospheric build collides with propulsive demands → solution is craft-aware density
- Punchy-thriller voice + romantasy genre = fights the romance genre's lingering proximity beats
- Voice is set up to do one thing (irony) and the outline asks for another (sincerity)

**Proposed-fix template:** "Voice and genre will collide at [specific beat] — flag for drafting attention." / "Chapter-end discipline needs to serve both [voice quality] and [genre quality] — Acts I-II current beats lean too [X]."

---

## 17. Diversity / representation

**What to check:**
- Cast composition — whose stories get told
- Tokenization risks (a single character carrying the burden of representing a whole identity)
- Specific fits or misfits with the comp-title shelf
- Setting / cultural specificity if relevant

**Bug patterns:**
- Single non-white character in an otherwise white cast carrying all the representational weight
- Marginalized identity introduced as a plot hurdle for the protagonist rather than as a character with their own arc
- Setting requires cultural specificity that the outline isn't engaging with

**Proposed-fix template:** "Cast composition concentrates representational weight on [character] — broaden by [specific change]." / "Setting calls for [cultural specificity] that the outline isn't engaging with — either embrace or reposition."

---

## 18. Antagonist coherence

**What to check:**
- Antagonist's motivation legible
- Screen-time sufficient for the threat to feel real
- Strategy articulated to the reader (why is the antagonist letting the protagonist run free?)
- Payoff: does the antagonist's defeat (or stalemate) make narrative sense?

**Bug patterns:**
- Antagonist mentioned but never on-page until the climax
- Antagonist's strategy is "wait" with no in-text justification — reads slow
- Multiple antagonists with overlapping but uncoordinated agendas
- Antagonist-defeat-via-trick that wasn't planted

**Proposed-fix template:** "[Antagonist]'s strategic patience needs to be on-page — give it a [specific scene] where the strategy is articulated." / "Antagonist screen-time too thin in Acts [X-Y]; add [presence beats]."

---

## 19. Cover-blurb test

**What to check:**
- Can a three-line back-cover blurb be derived from the outline?
- Does the blurb have a clear hook, a tension/promise, and a stakes line?
- If you can't pitch the book in three lines, the outline is muddy at the macro level

**Bug patterns:**
- Multiple competing high-concept hooks fight each other
- Outline is plot-detailed but no single sentence captures what the book *is*
- Stakes line is abstract ("the world is at stake") rather than specific

**Proposed-fix template:** "Test: [draft three-line blurb]. If this lands, outline is macro-coherent. If not, [specific clarification needed]."

---

## 20. Continuity prep

**What to check:**
- Names, surnames, capitalizations decided (not "TBD")
- Recurring objects / mantras / italicized phrases logged
- Capitalization canon for in-world terms
- Series-bible-ready data captured in the outline

**Bug patterns:**
- Multiple "TBD" placeholder names that will need to be remembered for drafting
- Capitalizations inconsistent across the outline
- Mechanics named differently in different sections (the *Sigil* in §3, the *Mark* in §6)

**Proposed-fix template:** "Lock [specific TBD items] before drafting." / "Capitalization drift between [section A] and [section B] — pick a canonical form."

---

## A note on category weighting

In a typical pass-1 critique, structure / pacing / cosmology / stakes / character agency are the highest-leverage categories. In pass-2 and pass-3 critiques, theme / reader-promise / series architecture / DNF audit / chapter-end hook discipline / voice-vs-genre register surface deeper bugs that weren't visible until the surface was clean.

If a pass-3 critique only has five new items across the deeper categories, that's a healthy outline. Don't pad.
