# Manuscript Revision Guide

This is the AI artifact and prose cleanup pass (Step 6) that transforms raw drafted prose into polished, human-sounding fiction. Every manuscript must go through this revision before the developmental edit.

**Note:** This guide covers the AI artifact and prose cleanup pass. After this pass, each manuscript also goes through a full developmental edit (Step 7) and a beta read (Step 7c). The dev edit catches structural and genre problems; the beta read catches reader-experience issues.

## How to Apply This Revision

1. Read back the full manuscript text (all chapters)
2. **Start with the Genre Compliance Check (Section 0) — if the book isn't hitting its genre, fix that FIRST**
3. **Continuously cross-reference the user's style guide** — every fix must sound like the user's voice, not a generic editorial voice
4. Apply all other fixes below systematically from beginning to end
5. Regenerate the .docx with the revised text
6. Verify the revised manuscript retains full chapter length — revision means improving, not cutting

## 0. Genre Compliance Check (DO THIS FIRST)

Before touching anything else, verify the manuscript is hitting its genre targets.

### Is the romance the A-plot?

Read through every chapter and ask: "Is this chapter primarily about the romantic relationship?" The love interest should be present, mentioned, or actively on the narrator's mind in EVERY chapter. If any chapter is primarily about career, family, self-discovery, or personal growth with the romance as a side note, that chapter needs rewriting to center the romance.

**Red flags:**
- A chapter where the love interest doesn't appear and isn't meaningfully on the narrator's mind
- Career/family subplots getting more page time than the romance
- The climax being about personal breakthrough rather than the relationship
- The book summarizable as "person finds themselves" rather than "two people fall in love"

**The fix:** For any chapter that's drifted, rewrite it to center the romance. The career problem should be tangled up with the love interest. The family drama should force the characters together or drive them apart. Every subplot should pressure the romance.

### Does the heat level match?

For Steamy/Scorching: Are the intimate scenes present at the right frequency and intensity? Do they match the spicy style guide?

For Sweet/Clean: Is there any content that crosses the line? Remove any explicit content.

For Moderate (fade to black): Do scenes build tension and then fade at the right moment? Not too soon (cutting off before any chemistry) and not too late (crossing into open-door territory)?

### Does the subgenre deliver?

- **Romcom**: Is it actually funny? Every chapter needs 2-3 laugh-out-loud moments. If the narrator sounds like a therapy journal instead of a comedian narrating their own romantic disaster, it's not a romcom.
- **Romantic suspense**: Does the suspense thread through every chapter? Is the danger real and escalating?
- **Paranormal**: Are the paranormal rules consistent? Does the magic create escalating complications?
- **Dark romance**: Is the darkness consistent and earned? Does it cross the user's stated boundaries?
- **Historical**: Are the period details consistent and woven in naturally?

## 1. Structure, Pacing, and Flow

The most common AI-writing tell is mechanical chapter structure: setup → conflict → realization → tidy emotional wrap-up → dramatic last line. Real novels don't do this.

### Chapter endings — the biggest fix

**Kill these patterns:**
- One-line dramatic closers: "And nothing would ever be the same." / "Little did I know..." / "That was the moment everything changed."
- Philosophical summaries: "Maybe that's what love really was — learning to be seen."
- Emotional bow-tying: wrapping up the chapter's emotional arc in a neat final paragraph
- Manufactured cliffhangers that feel forced rather than organic

**Replace with:**
- End mid-conversation, mid-action, or mid-thought
- End on a question the character hasn't answered yet
- End on forward momentum — the character is about to do something
- End on an unresolved feeling, not a labeled one

### Chapter openings

- Don't start every chapter with the character waking up, arriving somewhere, or reflecting on what just happened
- Vary the entry points: mid-dialogue, mid-action, a sensory detail, a stray thought
- The opening should feel like joining a life already in progress

### Transitions

- Don't use "The next morning..." or "Three days later..." as a crutch. Show time passing through changed details
- Don't recap the previous chapter's events in the opening of the next one
- Each chapter should feel like a continuation, not a reset

## 2. AI Artifact Removal

### Obvious artifacts
- "As an AI..." or any reference to being artificial
- "Write a scene where..." or any prompt leakage
- "In this chapter..." or any structural meta-commentary
- Author-voice analysis disguised as character thought

### Connector words and phrases — the #1 giveaway

| AI Connector | What to Do |
|---|---|
| "Additionally" | Delete. Start the sentence without it. |
| "Furthermore" | Delete. |
| "Moreover" | Delete. |
| "It's worth noting that" | Delete. Just state the thing. |
| "However, it's important to remember" | Cut to the actual point. |
| "Interestingly" | Delete. Let the reader decide what's interesting. |
| "In fact" | Usually unnecessary. Delete. |
| "Essentially" | Delete. |
| "Fundamentally" | Delete. |
| "Ultimately" | Delete 90% of the time. |

### Emotional over-labeling

AI loves to name and analyze emotions instead of letting the reader feel them.

**Before (AI-style):**
> I felt a complex mixture of relief and anxiety, tinged with something that might have been hope.

**After (human-style):**
> My chest loosens. Not all the way. But enough that I take a breath that doesn't hurt.

**The rule:** Show the body, show the behavior, show the thought — don't label the emotion. Trust the reader.

### Clinical precision

- "After approximately forty-five seconds of silence..." → "The silence stretches."
- "She maintained eye contact for three full seconds before..." → "She holds my gaze, then looks away."
- "The temperature in the room seemed to drop by several degrees" → Cut entirely.

### POV discipline

The narrator can only know what they can perceive. Watch for:
- Describing their own facial expression (you can't see your own face)
- Knowing what the other character is thinking with certainty
- Omniscient observations about what's happening elsewhere
- Describing themselves from the outside

## 3. Dialogue Fixes

### Surname overuse
Characters who are dating, flirting, or becoming friends don't address each other by last name constantly. Use first names, nicknames, or no name at all.

### Call-and-response patterns
AI dialogue ping-pongs in perfect exchanges. Real conversation is messier — interruptions, non-sequiturs, moments where someone doesn't respond to what was actually said.

### Overwritten dialogue tags
- "he said with a sardonic grin" → "he said" or cut the tag
- "she murmured thoughtfully" → "she said"
- Let the dialogue do the work. If it needs an adverb to land, the dialogue itself needs rewriting.

### Perfect banter problem
AI writes banter where every line is a zinger. Real banter has setup lines and moments where someone just doesn't have a comeback.

## 4. Narrative Authenticity

### Forced metaphors
If a metaphor feels "writerly" rather than something the POV character would actually think, cut it.

### Inflated sentences
AI prose tends toward unnecessary complexity. Simplify without dumbing down.

### The POV character's real limitations
A first-person narrator doesn't have perfect recall, doesn't analyze situations like a therapist, and has blind spots about themselves. Lean into the limitations.

## 5. Style Guide Compliance Check

**This is unique to this skill and CRITICAL.** After applying all the above fixes, re-read the user's style guide and verify:

- Sentence lengths match their patterns
- Dialogue-to-narrative ratio matches
- Descriptive density matches (don't over-describe if they write spare)
- Humor style matches (don't inject humor if they write dark and atmospheric)
- Vocabulary level matches (don't elevate or simplify beyond their range)
- Their distinctive quirks are preserved (fragment usage, em-dash style, etc.)

**If the revision has drifted from the user's voice, fix it.** The goal is prose that sounds like the user wrote it on a really good day — not prose that sounds like a different, "better" writer.

## 6. Intimate Scene Revision (Steamy/Scorching Only)

For manuscripts with open-door intimate scenes, also check:

- **Vocabulary matches the spicy style guide** — use the words the user uses, avoid the words they avoid
- **Emotional integration** — intimate scenes should advance the romance, not be disconnected physical interludes
- **Pacing matches their patterns** — if they write slow builds, don't rush; if they write intense and fast, don't linger
- **No purple prose unless that's their style** — "his throbbing manhood" is only appropriate if the user actually writes like that
- **Consent is clear** — even in Scorching content, both characters should be enthusiastic participants
- **Scene entry/exit matches their style** — how do they fade in and out of intimate moments?

## 7. Final Checklist

Before generating the final .docx:

**GENRE COMPLIANCE:**
- [ ] The romance is the A-plot in every chapter
- [ ] The heat level matches what was specified
- [ ] The subgenre delivers what it promises

**STYLE GUIDE COMPLIANCE:**
- [ ] The prose sounds like the user's voice, not generic editorial prose
- [ ] Sentence patterns, dialogue style, and descriptive density match
- [ ] Intimate scenes (if any) match the spicy style guide

**PROSE AND STRUCTURE:**
- [ ] ALL chapters are fully written prose — no summaries or placeholders
- [ ] No chapter ends on a tidy emotional summary or one-line dramatic closer
- [ ] No AI connectors remain
- [ ] Dialogue sounds like actual humans talking
- [ ] Each POV character has a distinct voice maintained throughout
- [ ] No POV breaks
- [ ] Chapter lengths are preserved — revision improved quality, not reduced quantity
- [ ] All major emotional turning points happen on-page
- [ ] Third-act conflict emerges from established character fears/values
