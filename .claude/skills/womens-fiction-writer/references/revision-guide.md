# Manuscript Revision Guide — Women's Fiction

This is the AI artifact and prose cleanup pass that transforms raw drafted prose into polished, human-sounding fiction. Every manuscript must go through this revision before the developmental edit.

## How to Apply This Revision

1. Read back the full manuscript text (all chapters)
2. **Start with the Genre Compliance Check (Section 0) — if the protagonist's transformation isn't the A-plot, fix that FIRST**
3. **Continuously cross-reference the user's style guide** — every fix must sound like the user's voice, not a generic editorial voice
4. Apply all other fixes systematically from beginning to end
5. Regenerate the .docx with the revised text
6. Verify the revised manuscript retains full chapter length — revision means improving, not cutting

---

## 0. Genre Compliance Check (DO THIS FIRST)

Before touching anything else, verify the manuscript is hitting its genre targets.

### Is the protagonist's transformation the A-plot?

Read through every chapter and ask: "Is this chapter primarily about who this woman is becoming?" Her inner arc — her wound, her resistance, her cracks, her choices — should be the beating heart of the book. Every subplot (including any romance) should create pressure on or illuminate that arc.

**Red flags:**
- Chapters where the protagonist is passive — things happen to her but she makes no meaningful choice
- A romance subplot that has grown to dominate the book, with the protagonist's inner journey as a side note
- Chapters where the protagonist's emotional state is static — she begins and ends in the same inner place
- The book summarizable as "woman meets love interest and falls in love" rather than "woman learns who she is"
- The climax being about an external plot event rather than the protagonist's transformation

**The fix:** For chapters that have drifted, rewrite to center the protagonist's inner experience. The romance problem should illuminate her wound. The family drama should force her to confront her lie. Every scene should be doing double duty — external event AND internal shift.

### Is the ending earned?

- Does the protagonist's resolution grow from choices she's made throughout the book?
- Does it feel honest — not perfect, not devastating, but true?
- Is the hope in the ending credible given everything that came before?

If the ending feels like a gift — a relationship mended too easily, a self-realization that arrives too conveniently — it needs to be rebuilt from the inside out. The ending is only as good as the work the protagonist has done to earn it.

### Does the category deliver?

- **Midlife reinvention**: Is the protagonist's prior self-erasure specific and believable? Is her new direction something she actively chooses rather than falls into?
- **Grief & healing**: Does the grief have texture — not just sadness but also strange moments of humor, relief, guilt, and mundane inconvenience?
- **Family saga / secrets**: Does the secret feel genuinely hidden in the first half? Does its revelation reframe what came before?
- **Sisterhood**: Do the female friendships feel specific and earned — not generic "supportive best friend"?

## 1. Structure, Pacing, and Flow

The most common AI-writing tell is mechanical chapter structure: setup → conflict → realization → tidy emotional wrap-up → dramatic last line. Real novels — especially women's fiction — don't do this.

### Chapter endings — the biggest fix

**Kill these patterns:**
- One-line dramatic closers: "And nothing would ever be the same." / "Little did I know..."
- Philosophical summaries: "Maybe that's what belonging really felt like."
- Emotional bow-tying: wrapping up the chapter's emotional arc in a neat final paragraph
- Manufactured cliffhangers that feel forced rather than organic

**Replace with:**
- End mid-conversation, mid-action, or mid-thought
- End on a question the character hasn't answered yet
- End on forward momentum — the character is about to do something
- End on an unresolved feeling, not a labeled one

### Chapter openings

- Don't start every chapter with the character waking up, arriving somewhere, or reflecting on what just happened
- Vary the entry points: mid-dialogue, mid-action, a sensory detail, a stray thought
- The opening should feel like joining a life already in progress

### Transitions

- Don't use "The next morning..." or "Three weeks later..." as a crutch — show time passing through changed details
- Don't recap the previous chapter's events in the next chapter's opening
- Each chapter should feel like a continuation, not a reset

## 2. AI Artifact Removal

### Obvious artifacts
- "As an AI..." or any reference to being artificial
- "Write a scene where..." or any prompt leakage
- "In this chapter..." or any structural meta-commentary

### Connector words and phrases — the #1 giveaway

| AI Connector | What to Do |
|---|---|
| "Additionally" | Delete. Start the sentence without it. |
| "Furthermore" | Delete. |
| "Moreover" | Delete. |
| "It's worth noting that" | Delete. Just state the thing. |
| "However, it's important to remember" | Cut to the actual point. |
| "Interestingly" | Delete. Let the reader decide what's interesting. |
| "In fact" | Usually unnecessary. Delete. |
| "Essentially" | Delete. |
| "Fundamentally" | Delete. |
| "Ultimately" | Delete 90% of the time. |

### Emotional over-labeling

AI loves to name and analyze emotions instead of letting the reader feel them.

**Before (AI-style):**
> She felt a complex mixture of grief and relief, tinged with something that might have been hope.

**After (human-style):**
> Her chest was too tight and too loose at the same time. She didn't know what to do with her hands.

**The rule:** Show the body, show the behavior, show the thought — don't label the emotion. Trust the reader.

### Clinical precision

- "After approximately forty-five seconds of silence..." → "The silence stretched."
- "She maintained eye contact for three full seconds before..." → "She held my gaze, then looked away."
- "The temperature in the room seemed to drop by several degrees" → Cut entirely.

### POV discipline

The narrator can only know what they can perceive. Watch for:
- Describing their own facial expression (you can't see your own face)
- Knowing what another character is thinking with certainty
- Omniscient observations about what's happening elsewhere
- Describing themselves from the outside

## 3. Dialogue Fixes

### Therapy-speak — the women's fiction trap
Characters in women's fiction should NOT explain their feelings clearly and directly to each other unless the scene is specifically about that breakthrough. Most of the time, characters talk around things, deflect, change the subject, or say the wrong thing. Women's fiction is often built on subtext.

**Before:**
> "I think my issue is that I've spent my whole life defining myself through my role as a mother, and now that the children are grown, I don't know who I am anymore."

**After:**
> She picked up the wine glass, set it back down. "It's a nice kitchen," she said. "Very quiet."

### Call-and-response patterns
AI dialogue ping-pongs in perfect exchanges. Real conversation has interruptions, non-sequiturs, moments where someone doesn't respond to what was actually said, long pauses that say more than words.

### Overwritten dialogue tags
- "she said with a rueful smile" → "she said" or cut the tag
- "he murmured thoughtfully" → "he said"
Let the dialogue and action beats do the work.

### Surname overuse
Characters who are close don't address each other by last name. Use first names, nicknames, or no name at all.

## 4. Narrative Authenticity

### Forced metaphors
If a metaphor feels "writerly" rather than something the POV character would actually think, cut it. A 54-year-old woman grieving her marriage does not think in perfectly constructed similes.

### The POV character's real limitations
A first-person narrator doesn't have perfect recall, doesn't analyze situations like a therapist, and has blind spots about herself — especially in women's fiction, where the whole point is that she's been lying to herself. Lean into the limitations.

### Over-explaining the theme
Women's fiction carries thematic weight, but that weight should emerge from events and relationships — not be stated directly by characters or the narrator. If a character says "I've learned that I need to stop living for others and start living for myself," that's a problem. Show her doing it.

### Inflated sentences
AI prose tends toward unnecessary complexity. Simplify without dumbing down.

## 5. Style Guide Compliance Check

After applying all the above fixes, re-read the user's style guide and verify:

- Sentence lengths match their patterns
- Dialogue-to-narrative ratio matches
- Descriptive density matches (don't over-describe if they write spare)
- Humor style matches (don't inject humor if they write emotionally raw)
- Vocabulary level matches
- Their distinctive quirks are preserved (fragment usage, em-dash style, etc.)

**If the revision has drifted from the user's voice, fix it.** The goal is prose that sounds like the user wrote it on a really good day.

---

## 6. Final Checklist

Before generating the final .docx:

**GENRE COMPLIANCE:**
- [ ] The protagonist's transformation is the A-plot in every chapter
- [ ] The ending feels earned and honest — not saccharine, not convenient
- [ ] The story delivers what its specific women's fiction category promises

**STYLE GUIDE COMPLIANCE:**
- [ ] The prose sounds like the user's voice, not generic literary fiction
- [ ] Sentence patterns, dialogue style, and descriptive density match the style guide
- [ ] The narrative voice is consistent throughout

**PROSE AND STRUCTURE:**
- [ ] ALL chapters are fully written prose — no summaries or placeholders
- [ ] No chapter ends on a tidy emotional summary or one-line dramatic closer
- [ ] No AI connectors remain
- [ ] Dialogue avoids therapy-speak and sounds like real people
- [ ] Subtext is doing work — characters don't over-explain their feelings
- [ ] No POV breaks
- [ ] Chapter lengths are preserved — revision improved quality, not reduced quantity
- [ ] All major transformation moments happen on-page, not reported after the fact
- [ ] The protagonist's arc is traceable from first page to last
