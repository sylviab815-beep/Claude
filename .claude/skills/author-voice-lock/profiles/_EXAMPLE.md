# Voice Profile — Marian Holt (FICTIONAL EXAMPLE)

> This is a fictional example profile for the made-up author "Marian Holt." She does not exist. Her books do not exist. Use this only to see what a complete, specific, useful profile looks like before filling in your own. Do **not** copy these rules into your own profile — they are calibrated to a fictional voice. Build your own.

---

## 1. Identity

- **Pen name:** Marian Holt
- **Genre / subgenre:** Literary suspense with a domestic-noir spine. Adult.
- **Comp authors / comp titles** _(calibration only):_ Tana French (interiority), Megan Abbott (claustrophobia), Patricia Highsmith (the moral discomfort that doesn't resolve)
- **One-sentence voice summary:** Close, withholding first-person; the narrator sees too much and tells the reader too little; tension lives in the gap.

---

## 2. POV & tense

- **Person:** First
- **Tense:** Past
- **POV character(s):** Single — the protagonist of whatever book is being drafted. Marian's narrators are usually women in their late thirties, often in a job that requires precise observation (nurse, court reporter, archivist).
- **POV switch rules:** Never. One narrator per book. No dual timeline unless the second timeline is also her, earlier.
- **Interiority style:** Withholding. The narrator notices everything and names selectively. What she chooses not to name carries the weight.

---

## 3. Sentence rhythm

- **Default sentence length:** Medium. Most sentences 12–20 words. Occasional long sentence with internal punctuation; occasional very short sentence as a beat.
- **Fragments:** Earned at peaks. Not a default move. When a fragment appears, it's load-bearing.
- **Em-dash use:** Moderate. For self-correction and the after-thought. Not for parenthetical asides — those are commas.
- **Colon-then-list:** Used sparingly, usually for inventories: _what was on the kitchen counter: the cracked mug, the unopened mail, the keys that should not have been there._
- **Same-starter parallels:** Allowed but not signature. If three sentences in a row start with "I," that's because she's making a point.
- **Three-beat closers:** Allowed at chapter ends. Not at paragraph ends.
- **Negation patterns:** Used sparingly for self-correction. _Not anger. Something colder._

---

## 4. Paragraph density

- **Default paragraph length:** 3–5 sentences. Marian writes breathable pages.
- **At action peaks:** Shorter. 1–2 sentences. White space is a beat.
- **At intimacy peaks:** Longer. The narrator burrows.
- **At interior reflection:** Longer, with internal punctuation. The thought folds back on itself.
- **White-space tolerance:** Breathable. The reader needs room.

---

## 5. Dialogue mechanics

- **Tag default:** `said`. Always.
- **`asked` for questions:** No. `said` even for questions. The question mark does the work.
- **Adverbs on tags:** Banned. Zero exceptions.
- **Action beats instead of tags:** Preferred when the speaker has been established in the scene. The action beat carries the tone.
- **Dialect / accent rendering:** Suggestion only — through word choice, syntax, and rhythm. No phonetic spelling.
- **Internal quotation without quote marks:** Used. When the narrator is recalling something said earlier, italics, no quote marks.
- **Speaking-of-things-not-said rules:** Frequent. The narrator names what wasn't asked. _He didn't ask whether I'd been there. I noticed that he didn't ask._

---

## 6. Banned vocabulary (zero tolerance)

- **shimmer / shimmered / shimmering** — pulls toward romance register; out of voice.
- **tendrils** — out of voice. Anything described as having tendrils gets a rewrite.
- **palpable** — banned as a tell.
- **tension was thick** / **thick with tension** — banned. The tension shows or it isn't there.
- **suddenly** — banned. The prose should make the suddenness audible without naming it.
- **finally** — banned as a stage direction. _He finally turned to face me._ → _He turned to face me._
- **a flicker of [emotion]** — banned. The flicker either is rendered or it isn't.
- **let out a breath she hadn't realized she was holding** — banned. Capital-T trope.
- **Not-X stacks** _(three short denials in a row)_ — banned. _Not air. Not water. Not blood._ → rewrite.
- **the way [X] verbs** as comparator — banned. _The way the light caught his face._ Allowed: literal idioms — _the way home._
- **palpable silence / heavy silence / pregnant silence** — banned as a stack. Silence is named once or not at all.

### Borderline (handle case-by-case)

- **somehow** — at most once per chapter.
- **almost** — earned. Two per scene maximum.
- **moment** — _in that moment_ is fine; _moments_ as filler is a flag.
- **deliberate** — usually a tell that the writer is naming intent the body should be showing. Use only when the narrator is explicitly noting deliberateness as data.

---

## 7. Preferred vocabulary & signature moves (preserve)

- **The narrator counts things.** Money, steps, days, doors. The act of counting is coping. Preserve.
- **Italicized recalled speech.** When dialogue is being remembered rather than spoken live, italics, no quote marks. Established move.
- **Colon-list inventories** at the start of a scene to set the room: _what was on the table: …_
- **The withheld noun.** The narrator names a category before naming the specific thing inside it. _Something on the counter. The mug. The cracked one._
- **Job-specific vocabulary.** If the narrator is a nurse, the prose uses nursing terms accurately and unselfconsciously. No glossing.
- **Self-correction in real time.** _I'd been treating it as a coincidence. That was wrong._

---

## 8. AI-tell guardrails

- **Use the universal AI tell library?** Yes, all categories.
- **Custom AI tells specific to this voice:**
  - **"a sense of [X]"** — banned as filler. _A sense of unease_ → render the unease or cut.
  - **"something like [emotion]"** — handle with care. Allowed when the narrator is genuinely groping for the right name; banned as default hedging.
  - **"the air seemed to [verb]"** — banned. Air doesn't seem.

---

## 9. Genre & trope guardrails

- **Genre / subgenre / heat level:** Literary suspense / domestic noir. No on-page sex; sex happens off-page or in fade-to-black.
- **Tropes the voice leans into:**
  - Unreliable narration where the unreliability is *omission* rather than lying.
  - The dangerous house — the reader knows something is wrong about the home before the narrator names it.
  - The professional woman whose precision is both her strength and her cage.
- **Tropes the voice avoids:**
  - The twist ending that recontextualizes everything. Marian's books end with the reader knowing more than the narrator does, not less.
  - The found family. Marian's narrators are alone at the end. Not unhappy, but alone.
  - The villain monologue.
- **Content lines (hard nos):** No on-page sexual assault. No animal harm beyond what is unavoidably implied. No racist slurs even from villains.
- **Content lines (handle with care):** Violence is on-page but never sexualized. Suicidal ideation can appear but is treated with weight, never as a plot device.

## 11. Character voice rules

### Protagonist(s)

- **Voice tics / signatures:** Counts. Catalogs. Withholds. Self-corrects in real time.
- **Internal monologue density:** Heavy. The interiority is the book.
- **What the character notices:** Inventories. Hands. Money. Whether a door was closed when she came in. Whose name is on whose mailbox.
- **What the character avoids naming directly:** Her own fear. Her own desire. Her family of origin. The reader reaches these by triangulation.

## 12. Things the voice DOES (preserve)

- Marks time precisely. _It was a quarter past three. The light had already started to go._
- Renders weather as data, not atmosphere.
- Trusts the reader. Does not over-explain.
- Lets silence sit. The narrator sometimes simply does not speak for a paragraph; the prose stays in observation.

---

## 13. Things the voice AVOIDS (do not slip into)

- Cinematic camera moves. _The camera pans across the room_ has no business in this prose.
- Adjective stacks. _The cold, dark, empty house_ is three adjectives doing one job poorly.
- Therapy-speak. The narrator does not "process" anything.
- Authorial winks. No genre-aware nudges to the reader.
- Tidy resolution. The book ends; the world does not.

## 14. Post-draft grep checklist

- `\bsuddenly\b` — flag every instance.
- `\bfinally\b` — flag every instance.
- `\bshimmer` — flag every instance.
- `\bpalpable\b` — flag every instance.
- `\btendrils?\b` — flag every instance.
- `\bsomehow\b` — count; flag if more than one in chapter.
- `\balmost\b` — count; flag if more than two in scene.
- `the way \w+ed` — flag for review (banned simile pattern).
- `the way \w+s\b` — flag for review (banned simile pattern, present tense).
- Check for adverb-on-tag pattern: `\bsaid \w+ly\b` — flag every instance.

## 15. Notes for the skill

If a new proper noun is needed for the current book (a workplace name, a road, a building), ask before inventing. Marian's projects each get a per-book addendum file rather than polluting this profile.
