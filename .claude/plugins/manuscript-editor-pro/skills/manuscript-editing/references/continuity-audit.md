# Continuity Audit Framework

A systematic sweep of the entire manuscript to catch every factual contradiction, relationship inconsistency, timeline impossibility, and world-rule violation. This is not a skim — it requires building reference tables from a full read and then auditing every chapter against them.

## Why This Is a Separate Step

The proofread pass catches obvious consistency errors (name spelling, formatting). But continuity problems are subtler: a character's eye color changing between chapters, two characters who meet for the "first time" twice, a location that moves from one side of town to the other, a magical ability that works differently in Chapter 3 than Chapter 15. These require cross-referencing the entire manuscript, not just reading linearly.

## CRITICAL: Thoroughness Requirements

**Build COMPLETE reference tables before auditing.** Do not skip Phase 1. Every named character, every named location, every significant timeline event, and every world rule must be logged. The tables are the foundation — a shallow table produces a shallow audit.

**Minimum table depth:**
- **Character Bible:** Every character who appears more than once must have an entry. For a typical novel, expect 8-20+ character entries. If you have fewer than 5, you are under-tracking.
- **Location Register:** Every named location must have an entry. Expect 5-15+ for most novels.
- **Timeline:** Every scene should be placed on a day/time grid. Do not skip scenes or assume days match up — count them.

**Minimum expected findings:** Every manuscript has continuity issues. Expect at least 3 issues per 10,000 words. A 60,000-word novel should have 18+ continuity findings. If you find fewer than 10 for a full-length novel, your reference tables are not detailed enough — rebuild them with more entries and re-audit.

**Audit EVERY chapter against the tables.** Do not spot-check. Every chapter must be cross-referenced against every relevant table. Log which chapters you audited and what you checked in each.

**The reference tables themselves are a deliverable.** Even if there were zero contradictions (which is unlikely), the character bible, relationship tracker, and timeline reconstruction are valuable to the author as a series bible. Build them thoroughly.

## How to Run the Audit

### Phase 1: Build the Master Reference Tables

Read the entire manuscript from beginning to end. As you read, build the following tables. Do NOT try to fix anything during this phase — just document.

---

### 1. Character Bible

For every named character (including minor characters who appear more than once), track:

**Physical Description:**
| Character | Eye Color | Hair | Height/Build | Age | Distinguishing Features | First Appears |
|-----------|-----------|------|-------------|-----|------------------------|---------------|
| [Name] | | | | | | Ch. X |

**Identity & Role:**
| Character | Occupation | Relationship to Protagonist | Key Personality Traits | Speech Pattern Notes |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| [Name] | | | | |

**What to flag:**
- Eye color, hair color, or other physical details that change between chapters
- Age contradictions (stated as 42 in Ch. 1, referenced as "in her late thirties" in Ch. 8)
- Occupation or role changes without explanation
- Character names that shift (Katherine becomes Catherine, Bob becomes Bobby inconsistently)
- Nicknames used before they're established
- Characters described as having a trait but never demonstrating it (or demonstrating the opposite)

---

### 2. Relationship Tracker

Track every significant relationship in the manuscript:

**First Meetings:**
| Characters | How They Meet | Chapter | Context |
|-----------|--------------|---------|---------|
| A + B | | Ch. X | |

**Relationship Progression:**
| Characters | Ch. 1-5 Status | Ch. 6-10 Status | Ch. 11-15 Status | Ch. 16+ Status |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|-----------------|---------------|
| A + B | Strangers | Acquaintances | Friends | Close friends |

**What to flag:**
- Characters who already know something about each other before they've been told on-page
- Relationship jumps without development (antagonistic in one chapter, allied in the next with no bridge)
- Characters referencing shared history that was never established
- Terms of address that don't match the relationship stage ("honey" before any romantic development)
- A character reacting to news they shouldn't know yet
- The protagonist explaining something to a character who was present when it happened
- Power dynamics that flip without cause (mentor/student suddenly reversed)

---

### 3. Location & Geography

**Location Register:**
| Location | Description | Spatial Relationship | Key Features | First Appears |
|----------|------------|---------------------|-------------|---------------|
| [Place] | | "north of town square" | | Ch. X |

**What to flag:**
- A location described differently in different chapters (the café has a patio in Ch. 3 but no outdoor seating in Ch. 9)
- Spatial contradictions (the library is "across the street" from the bakery in Ch. 2 but "three blocks north" in Ch. 7)
- Travel times that don't match distances (a 5-minute walk in one scene, a 20-minute drive in another between the same two places)
- Rooms or buildings whose internal layout changes (the kitchen is left of the front door, then right of it)
- Weather or atmospheric details that contradict the established location (desert town with frequent rain)

---

### 4. Timeline & Chronology

Build a day-by-day (or scene-by-scene) timeline:

| Scene/Chapter | Day | Time of Day | Key Events | Weather/Season |
|--------------|-----|------------|-----------|----------------|
| Ch. 1, Scene 1 | Day 1 (Monday) | Morning | | Autumn, cool |
| Ch. 1, Scene 2 | Day 1 (Monday) | Afternoon | | |

**What to flag:**
- Days of the week that don't add up (Monday → Wednesday with no mention of Tuesday, but characters say "yesterday")
- Characters in two places at once (leaves the house in one scene, referenced as being at home in the next scene set at the same time)
- Seasons or weather inconsistent with the timeline (snow in what should be July based on the day count)
- Events referenced as happening "last week" when the timeline shows it was three days ago
- Pregnancy, healing, or growth timelines that don't match biology
- The sun setting/rising at impossible times for the season and latitude
- Food/drink that's seasonal (fresh strawberries in December without explanation)
- "Tomorrow" and "yesterday" references that break when you count the days

---

### 5. World Rules & Systems

For fantasy, sci-fi, paranormal, or any manuscript with established rules:

| Rule | First Established | Chapter | Details |
|------|------------------|---------|---------|
| Magic costs energy | Ch. 2 | "Every spell drains..." | |

**What to flag:**
- Rules that contradict themselves (magic costs energy in Ch. 2, character casts dozens of spells with no consequence in Ch. 10)
- Abilities that appear or disappear (character can sense emotions in Ch. 3, never uses this ability again despite situations where it would help)
- Power levels that shift without explanation
- Social rules that apply to some characters but not others without in-world justification
- Technology or knowledge that's anachronistic within the world's rules

---

### 6. Object & Detail Tracking

**Important Objects:**
| Object | First Appears | Current Location | Last Referenced |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|----------------|
| The letter | Ch. 3 | In protagonist's pocket | Ch. 7 |

**Information Flow — Who Knows What:**
| Information | Who Knows | How They Learned | Chapter |
|------------|-----------|-----------------|---------|
| The victim's secret | Protagonist, Detective | Found the diary | Ch. 5 |

**What to flag:**
- Objects that teleport (put in a drawer in Ch. 5, pulled from a pocket in Ch. 6 with no retrieval scene)
- Characters who know things they shouldn't (weren't present when information was revealed, weren't told on-page)
- Dropped plot threads (a clue is discovered but never followed up, a promise is made but never addressed)
- Chekhov's gun violations (an important object is introduced prominently but never pays off, or an object pays off that was never introduced)
- Running jokes or motifs that appear inconsistently (present in odd chapters, absent in even ones, as if the author forgot)

---

## Phase 2: Audit

After building all tables, go chapter by chapter and check every factual claim against the master tables. Produce a contradiction report:

```
## Continuity Issues Found

### Character Contradictions (count: N)
1. [Character Name] — [Detail]: Ch. X says [A], Ch. Y says [B]
   Recommendation: Standardize to [chosen version]

### Relationship Issues (count: N)
1. [Characters] — [Issue description]
   Recommendation: [Fix approach]

### Geography/Location Issues (count: N)
1. [Location] — [Contradiction]
   Recommendation: [Fix approach]

### Timeline Issues (count: N)
1. [Issue] — [Details]
   Recommendation: [Fix approach]

### World Rule Violations (count: N)
1. [Rule] — [Violation]
   Recommendation: [Fix approach]

### Object/Detail Issues (count: N)
1. [Issue]
   Recommendation: [Fix approach]
```

## Phase 3: Fix

For each contradiction, the fix should:
- Choose the version most consistent with the surrounding text
- Apply the change in ALL affected locations (not just one)
- Be minimal — change only what's needed to resolve the contradiction
- Preserve voice and style
- **NEVER add new content.** Do not insert bridging text, new sentences, transitions, or scenes. If a relationship jump or information gap needs bridging, flag it in the Continuity Audit report as a suggestion for the author. The editor standardizes contradictory DETAILS (eye color, directions, timelines) — it does not write new material to fill gaps.

## Phase 4: Verify

Re-read all fixed sections against the updated master tables. Confirm:
- Every flagged contradiction is resolved
- No new contradictions were introduced
- The reference tables are now clean and internally consistent
- The tables are ready for inclusion in the Editorial Report
