# Line Edit Checklist

A comprehensive framework for evaluating and fixing prose-level issues in fiction manuscripts. Use this checklist during the IDENTIFY phase of Pass 2.

## How to Use This Checklist

Work chapter by chapter. For each issue found, quote the specific text and provide a chapter/paragraph reference. The goal is a precise map of every prose-level problem so the FIX phase can work surgically.

## CRITICAL: Thoroughness Requirements

**Every chapter gets the full checklist.** Do not skip sections because the first few chapters seemed clean. Run AI Artifacts, Show-vs-Tell, Crutch Phrases, Dialogue, Prose Mechanics, and Chapter Endings for EVERY chapter.

**Run each checklist section as a separate sweep.** Do not try to catch everything in one read. Go through each chapter looking ONLY for AI artifacts first, then ONLY for show-vs-tell, then ONLY for crutch phrases, etc. This catches 3-5x more issues than a single pass.

**Minimum expected findings:** A professional line editor flags 5-15 issues per chapter. If you find fewer than 10 issues per 10,000 words across the full manuscript, you are skimming. Go back and process each checklist section as a dedicated sweep.

**No manuscript is issue-free at the prose level.** Every manuscript has show-vs-tell instances, weak verbs, filter words, and prose mechanics issues. If you're finding very few, you are not applying the checklist rigorously enough — re-read the checklist criteria and look again.

**Log per-chapter counts.** Every chapter must have an issue count broken down by category. If any chapter shows fewer than 3 total issues, re-read it with each checklist section as a separate pass.

**Crutch phrase search is MANDATORY and LITERAL.** Run an actual text search (case-insensitive) for every pattern in Section 2. Report the count for EACH pattern. Do not eyeball it — search systematically. This is the section most commonly skipped, and it's the easiest to verify.

---

## 1. AI Artifacts & Tells

These are the fingerprints of AI-generated or AI-assisted text. They break immersion for savvy readers and signal inauthenticity. Search aggressively.

### Robotic Transitions
Flag every instance of:
- "Furthermore," / "Moreover," / "Additionally,"
- "It's worth noting that" / "It's important to remember"
- "However, it's important to" / "That being said,"
- "In fact," (at sentence start) / "Indeed,"
- "Interestingly," / "Notably," / "Significantly,"
- "As [time] went on," / "As the [noun] progressed,"

### Clinical Emotion Descriptions
Flag prose that names emotions instead of rendering them:
- "She felt a wave of sadness wash over her"
- "He experienced a surge of anger"
- "A sense of unease settled in her stomach"
- "She felt something that could only be described as..."
- "The emotion was palpable" / "Tension filled the room"

### Meta-Commentary & Author Intrusion
- Characters who think in thesis statements about their own development
- Internal monologue that reads like a psychology textbook
- Observations that no human would actually make in the moment ("She realized that her childhood trauma had shaped her inability to trust")
- Characters diagnosing each other's emotional states with clinical precision

### Hedging Language
Track frequency of:
- "seemed to" / "appeared to" — weakens every sentence
- "couldn't help but" — removes character agency
- "found herself [verb]-ing" — distancing construction
- "somehow" — vague hand-wave
- "almost" / "nearly" / "practically" — commitment-phobic prose
- "a bit" / "slightly" / "somewhat" — deflating modifiers

**CRITICAL: Hedging vs. Characterization.** Before flagging any hedging language, ask: is this the AI being vague, or is this the CHARACTER being uncertain? In close POV or first person, "seemed to" and "appeared to" often reflect the narrator's LIMITED KNOWLEDGE — they genuinely can't tell what someone else is feeling, so they say "seemed." Removing this hedging turns an uncertain observation into a definitive statement, which changes characterization and POV fidelity. ONLY flag hedging that is clearly an AI writing artifact (generic, adds nothing, weakens prose). LEAVE hedging that reflects the POV character's genuine uncertainty, limited perspective, or personality.

### Artificial Precision
- "Exactly 3.2 seconds later" (unless the character would actually count)
- "The room was approximately twelve feet wide" (unless relevant to plot)
- Over-specific body language ("Her left eyebrow raised 2 millimeters")

## 2. Crutch Phrases

Track these patterns. If any appears more than 3 times in the full manuscript, every instance after the third must be rewritten:

| Pattern | Why It's Bad | Replace With |
|---------|-------------|--------------|
| "the air of someone who..." | Tells instead of shows | Specific body language or action |
| "the expression of someone who..." | Same | Describe the actual expression |
| "a sound that suggested..." | Vague sensory writing | Describe the actual sound |
| "apparently" (>5 total) | Hedge word | Cut or commit to the observation |
| "the kind of [noun] that..." | Distancing | Put the reader IN the moment |
| "something about [noun] suggested..." | Vague intuition | Show the specific detail |
| "it was clear that..." | If it's clear, show it | Just show it happening |
| "seemed to" / "appeared to" (>5) | Weakens sentences | Commit to the action |
| "couldn't help but..." | Removes agency | Character does the thing |
| "found herself [verb]-ing" | Distancing | She [verb]-ed |
| "let out a breath she didn't know she was holding" | Dead cliché | Literally anything else |
| "the silence was deafening" | Dead cliché | Describe what fills the silence |
| "a [emotion] she couldn't quite name" | Tells while pretending to show | Name it through physical sensation |

Run a literal text search (case-insensitive) for each pattern. Log the count. Report in the issue list.

## 3. Show vs. Tell

The most common prose-level failure. Look for:

### Emotion Telling
- **Bad:** "She was angry."
- **Good:** "Her jaw clenched. She set the mug down hard enough to crack the handle."

### Relationship Telling
- **Bad:** "They had always been close."
- **Good:** Show it through interaction — she finishes his sentences, he saves her the last cookie without being asked.

### Backstory Telling
- **Bad:** "She had grown up in a small town where everyone knew everyone, which meant she'd learned early how to keep secrets."
- **Good:** Deliver this through present-day behavior and dialogue, not backstory exposition.

### Theme Telling
- **Bad:** Characters stating the moral of the story in dialogue
- **Good:** The theme emerges from what happens, not what characters say about what happens

### The Test
For every flagged instance, ask: "Could a camera capture this?" If not, it's probably telling. Convert it to something observable — action, dialogue, physical sensation, environmental detail.

### CRITICAL: Meaning Preservation in Show-vs-Tell Conversions
When converting "tell" to "show," the EXACT SAME emotion, relationship dynamic, or backstory fact must survive the conversion. Examples:
- "She felt betrayed" → show BETRAYAL specifically (clenched fists, turned away, cold silence). Do NOT accidentally show anger, sadness, or confusion instead.
- "They had always been close" → show CLOSENESS specifically (finishing sentences, inside jokes, comfortable silence). Do NOT accidentally show romantic tension or dependency instead.
- If you cannot show the EXACT emotion/fact without changing it, LEAVE THE ORIGINAL and flag it in the report as a suggestion for the author. A "tell" that preserves meaning is better than a "show" that changes the story.

## 4. Intimate Scene Review

If the manuscript contains intimate or romantic scenes (any heat level from sweet/closed-door to explicit), apply this additional review layer. These scenes are where prose quality matters most because the reader is emotionally vulnerable and fully immersed — any craft failure hits harder here.

### Emotional Intensity & Authenticity
- Does the heat of the moment grow from the characters' emotional stakes (slow burn tension, unresolved conflict, finally giving in) rather than feeling inserted or mechanical?
- Is every physical beat connected to what the characters are feeling? What does each person want, fear, or resist in this moment?
- Are emotional motivations layered beneath the physical action so the scene feels meaningful?

### Show Don't Tell (Heightened)
- Are there places relying on flat description where sensory detail would bring the moment to life?
- Replace exposition with: body language, breath, proximity, subtle touch, internal reactions, dialogue, micro-expressions revealing desire, hesitation, or vulnerability
- The reader should feel what the characters feel, not be told about it

### Pacing & Rhythm
- Does the scene read like mechanical choreography (action, action, action) or does it breathe?
- Look for opportunities to add: moments of pause, hesitation, surprise, humor, emotional shifts
- The interaction should feel human and spontaneous, not scripted
- Vary sentence length — short punchy sentences for intensity, longer flowing ones for tenderness

### Cliché Purge
- Flag and replace overused romance metaphors: "electricity," "sparks," "fireworks," "molten," "pooled heat"
- Replace formulaic lines with language reflecting the characters' unique voices and the story's tone
- Keep prose intimate, grounded, and specific to THESE characters in THIS moment

### Physical Realism & Continuity
- Are clothing and accessories removed or adjusted in physically possible ways?
- If restraints, props, or positioning are involved, do they realistically affect what characters can do?
- Are body movements, spatial orientation, and physical logistics consistent moment to moment?
- Flag anything that would break immersion or require impossible contortions

### Emotional Closure
- Does the scene close with a believable emotional beat — connection, tension, awkwardness, relief, humor, or unresolved longing?
- The ending should feel true to the characters rather than a tidy fade-out
- What happens immediately after matters as much as the scene itself

### Output for Intimate Scenes
Provide specific, surgical line edits delivered as Track Changes — never wholesale rewrites of intimate passages. Each change must:
- Fix a specific, identifiable prose issue (cliché, physical impossibility, flat description, pacing problem)
- Preserve the exact emotional dynamic between the characters
- Keep the same sequence of events and the same outcome
- Match the manuscript's established heat level and tone
Do NOT rewrite intimate scenes to match a different vision of how the scene "should" go. The author wrote this scene deliberately. Fix craft issues only.

## 5. Dialogue Quality

### Distinctiveness Test
Cover the dialogue tags. Can you tell which character is speaking from voice alone? If not, the dialogue needs work. Each character should have:
- A vocabulary range (educated vs. casual, formal vs. slang)
- Speech patterns (long sentences vs. clipped, questions vs. statements)
- Verbal tics or habits (specific to that character, not generic)

### Subtext
Good dialogue means two things at once. Characters rarely say exactly what they mean. Look for:
- Conversations where both people say what they actually think/feel (too on-the-nose)
- Arguments where characters explain their positions rationally instead of emotionally
- Declarations of love/hate/fear that would be more powerful if indirect

### Dialogue Mechanics
- Avoid "said-bookisms" (hissed, exclaimed, breathed, opined, retorted) — use "said" and "asked" 90% of the time
- Replace adverb-heavy tags ("she said angrily") with action beats ("She slammed the door. 'Get out.'")
- Ensure dialogue is broken up with action, internality, and setting — no walls of talking heads

### Therapy-Speak
Flag dialogue where characters:
- Diagnose each other ("You're projecting your abandonment issues")
- Communicate with impossible emotional maturity ("I hear what you're saying, and I want to validate your feelings")
- Have perfect conflict resolution conversations instead of messy human ones
- Use vocabulary that belongs in a therapist's office, not in their mouth

## 6. Prose Mechanics

### Weak Verbs
Track frequency of was/were/had. If any chapter has more than 15% weak verbs, flag it. Replace with:
- Active, specific verbs ("was walking" → "strode," "shuffled," "marched")
- Direct sensory language ("was cold" → "the cold bit through her jacket")

### Passive Voice
Not all passive voice is bad, but clusters of 3+ passive sentences in a row signal lazy prose. Flag clusters.

### Filter Words
These put a pane of glass between the reader and the experience:
- saw, heard, felt, noticed, realized, wondered, thought, knew, watched, observed, smelled
- **Bad:** "She saw the door was open."
- **Good:** "The door was open."
- **Bad:** "He heard footsteps in the hallway."
- **Good:** "Footsteps echoed in the hallway."

### Redundancy
- "She nodded her head" (what else would she nod?)
- "He shrugged his shoulders"
- "She blinked her eyes"
- "The cold winter wind" (winter winds are cold)
- Emotional double-dipping: showing the emotion AND then telling it ("Her hands trembled. She was terrified.")

### Purple Prose
Flag descriptions that are overwrought relative to the scene's emotional weight. A sunset doesn't need three sentences unless the sunset matters to the character in that moment.

## 7. Chapter Endings

Every chapter ending should be evaluated:

**Strong endings:**
- Mid-scene cuts (the reader HAS to turn the page)
- New information that changes everything
- A decision made, with consequences pending
- A question asked that won't be answered until next chapter

**Weak endings:**
- Philosophical wrap-ups ("And as the sun set, she realized that home was wherever love lived")
- One-line dramatic mic-drops that feel manufactured
- Literal cliffhangers that feel cheap (protagonist in physical danger, every time)
- The character going to sleep (unless the next chapter opens with an alarming awakening)

Produce a chapter-by-chapter issue list:

```
## Chapter [X]: "[Chapter Title if any]"

### AI Artifacts (count: N)
- [Line/paragraph ref]: "quoted text" — [issue type]

### Show vs. Tell (count: N)
- [Line/paragraph ref]: "quoted text" — [what it should show instead]

### Crutch Phrases (count: N)
- [Pattern]: [count in this chapter]

### Intimate Scene Issues (if applicable)
- [Specific issues with line references and suggested approach]

### Dialogue Issues (count: N)
- [Line ref]: [issue description]

### Prose Mechanics (count: N)
- [Line ref]: [issue type]: "quoted text"

### Chapter Ending Assessment
- [Strong/Weak]: [explanation]
```

Then a manuscript-wide summary:
- Total issues by category
- Crutch phrase frequency table (full manuscript)
- Top 10 most impactful fixes (highest reader-experience improvement per edit)
